# The Cultural Diagnostic Move

## Pattern

Across his entire preaching ministry, Keller employs a consistent apologetic method that can be called the "cultural diagnostic move." The pattern has three steps:

1. **Enter the secular worldview.** Take its aspirations, values, and objections seriously. Do not caricature. Find what is genuinely good and true in the position.
2. **Show the internal tension.** Follow the worldview to its logical conclusions and demonstrate where it breaks down — not by attacking it from the outside, but by exposing the contradiction already present within it.
3. **Offer the gospel as the resolution.** Show how the gospel addresses the deepest aspiration that the broken worldview was reaching toward, and does so without the contradiction.

This method is derived from Paul's approach in Acts 17 at the Areopagus in Athens: he does not begin with "you are all wrong." He begins with "I see that you are very religious" and finds the altar to the unknown god — the place where Athens's own piety acknowledged that it had not yet reached ultimate truth. Only then does he preach Jesus. Keller calls this approach "theorizing" the culture — using the Greek *theoreo*, meaning to see underneath, to read what is beneath the surface of cultural forms.

## Instances

- **In apologetics:** The most explicit instantiation. Keller takes the objection against Christian exclusivity (blind men and elephant) and shows it is self-refuting. He takes Foucault's argument that truth claims are power plays and presses it until it dissolves its own foundation. He takes Kafka's vision of unreachable ultimate truth and shows that Jesus's argument from covenant love goes the exact opposite direction. In each case: enter the objection, expose its internal incoherence, resolve with gospel.

- **In idolatry:** When Paul "theorizes" Athens and sees an idol-saturated city, Keller finds this the prototype for reading modern Manhattan. Career anxiety, romantic obsession, the politics of identity — all are analyzed as what they are: substitute-god structures, organized with full religious devotion, promising what only God can deliver. The cultural diagnostic move here is: take the aspiration seriously (achievement is good, love is good, meaning is good), show where the broken cistern leaks (idols enslave, disappoint, and leave you empty), and offer the only spring that doesn't run dry.

- **In identity:** Keller engages the secular project of self-authorship directly. The modern self is defined by internal desires and authentic self-expression. This is a genuine aspiration — the desire to be known for who you really are is deep and right. But the self that must continuously generate its own identity is an exhausting taskmaster: your identity is always at risk, always requiring performance. The gospel says: your identity is given, not achieved. You are named before you perform. The aspiration (being truly known) is fulfilled, but not by the route secular culture prescribes.

- **In hope:** Keller takes modern culture's obsession with newness (new phones, new fashion, new experiences) seriously as evidence that we are subconsciously fleeing entropy. "I look in the mirror and I'm falling apart." The aspiration is genuine: we were made for incorruption. The secular strategy (diets, cosmetics, experiences) fails because it fights the symptom without addressing the cause. The gospel's answer is not delay but reversal — not the *neos* of youth but the *kainos* of qualitative newness forever.

- **In work and vocation:** Keller takes the secular devotion to career seriously — the ambition, the drive, the meaning sought in professional excellence. He does not dismiss it as worldliness. He asks: why does work feel so important? Why does a layoff feel like an identity crisis? Because humans were made to work, to create, to exercise dominion — and they know it. The aspiration is right. But when career becomes the source of ultimate meaning, it cannot bear the weight. The gospel restores work to its proper dignity: significant because it is service to God and neighbor, not because it constitutes the self.

- **In marriage and family:** Keller engages the romantic ideal directly: the search for a "soulmate," the belief that one person can complete you and meet your deepest needs for intimacy and acceptance. He finds this aspiration beautiful and takes it seriously. But romantic fulfillment as ultimate meaning produces precisely the dynamic that destroys marriages: using the spouse rather than serving them, demanding from a finite person what only an infinite person can give. The gospel says: the intimacy you seek with a spouse is real, but it is a foretaste of the intimacy God intends, not the thing itself.

- **In sin:** Keller reads secular intellectuals — H.G. Wells, W.H. Auden, Beatrice Webb, Ernest Becker — as witnesses to the doctrine of sin. Each began with confidence in human goodness and ended their lives conceding that they had been wrong. The aspiration (human dignity, moral progress, social justice) is genuine. But without the doctrine of sin, the aspiration keeps running aground on human nature. The gospel does not abandon the aspiration — it supplies the diagnosis that explains why it keeps failing, and the grace that provides the only non-coercive way to change.

## What This Means

The cultural diagnostic move is what makes Keller's preaching and apologetics legible to secular people rather than alien. He meets them where they are, takes what they believe seriously, and shows that the gospel does not come to abolish but to fulfill — to give what the aspiration was reaching for but couldn't grasp.

This method also explains why Keller's sermons are dense with secular sources: Foucault, Kafka, Tolkien, Dostoevsky, C.S. Lewis, Jonathan Edwards. He is not showing off. He is demonstrating that the world's deepest thinkers — even those hostile to Christianity — keep stumbling toward problems that only the gospel resolves. The secular thinker is an unwitting witness. Keller reads the culture as Paul read Athens: as a city full of altars to unknown gods, each one pointing to a need that only Jesus can meet.

For users of this wiki: when reading Keller's engagement with any topic, look for this three-step pattern. The opening move is almost always "here is what the secular world gets right about this." The middle move is "here is where it breaks down." The closing move is "here is the gospel resolution." Understanding this structure makes Keller's arguments much easier to follow and reproduce in conversation.
