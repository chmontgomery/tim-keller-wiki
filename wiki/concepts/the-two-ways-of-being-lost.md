# The Two Ways of Being Lost

## Pattern

Keller's most distinctive theological contribution — and his most sustained apologetic move — is the insistence that there are two equally lost postures before God, not one. The parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15) has two sons, and both are outside the father's house at the end. The younger son is lost through license: he takes his inheritance, wastes it on himself, and ends up in a pigsty. The older son is lost through moralism: he stays home, keeps all the rules, demands his father's recognition, and refuses to come to the party. Both brothers are outside. Both are alienated from the father. Only one of them knows it.

This pattern recurs across virtually every topic Keller addresses. In his analysis of sin, idolatry, salvation, apologetics, doubt, and sanctification, he returns again and again to the same structure: the two paths away from God are irreligion and moralism, and they are more similar to each other than either is to the gospel. The moralist is not closer to God than the sensualist — he is often further, because he is unaware of his condition. The Pharisee is as lost as the tax collector. The elder brother needs the same grace as the younger, and finding it requires the same humility: acknowledging that you are not the center.

## Instances

- **In sin:** Keller preaches a dedicated series "Introduction: The Two Prodigals" on Luke 15, showing both brothers as paradigms of the human condition. The younger son's sin is obvious; the older son's sin — pride, resentment, the feeling that his obedience has earned the father's love — is invisible and more dangerous precisely because it wears the face of virtue.

- **In idolatry:** Keller argues that idolatry operates through two mirror movements. The irreligious person makes pleasure, romance, or career their god. The religious person makes righteousness, devotion, or their own obedience their god. Both are idolaters. The idol in both cases is the self — either its desires (younger brother) or its achievements (elder brother).

- **In salvation and justification:** Keller's "two kinds of self-righteousness" sermon diagnoses both antinomianism (I can do whatever I want, God will forgive me) and moralism (I earn my standing before God through behavior). Both positions treat grace as unnecessary. Both produce people who are fundamentally transactional with God.

- **In apologetics:** When Jesus was attacked by both the Pharisees (conservative moralists) and the Sadducees (liberal relativists), Keller reads this as evidence that the gospel is categorically different from any human tribe. The gospel is not halfway between religion and irreligion. The God of Jesus is more demanding than the Pharisees' God (his justice cannot be satisfied by human effort) and more loving than the Sadducees' God (he went to the cross). Both parties recognized something alien in Jesus and rejected it.

- **In faith and doubt:** Keller distinguishes two kinds of doubt: the intellectual doubt of the skeptic ("I can't believe in miracles") and the spiritual doubt of the moralist ("I've been good — why is God doing this to me?"). The moralist's doubt reveals an assumed bargain: I performed, therefore God owes me. This is the elder brother complaining at the party door.

- **In sanctification:** Keller argues that obedience driven by fear of punishment (elder brother) and obedience driven by desire for reward (younger brother wanting his inheritance) are both forms of self-centered motivation. True obedience flows from gratitude for grace already received. The grammar changes: not "I obey in order to be accepted" but "I obey because I am accepted." The motive shift is the difference between a slave and a child.

## What This Means

The two-ways-of-being-lost pattern is Keller's answer to the standard criticism of Christianity as "just for rule-followers" or "just for people who need a crutch." The gospel is not a stricter form of moralism, and it is not a permission slip for self-indulgence. It addresses both conditions simultaneously, from outside both categories.

This insight is also Keller's primary pastoral tool. When someone comes to him with a presenting problem — addiction, failed marriage, career despair, persistent guilt — the question is not just "what did you do wrong?" but "which brother are you?" The younger brother usually knows he is lost. The elder brother usually does not. The pastoral work with each is radically different.

Finally, the pattern illuminates why the gospel produces what no other worldview can: genuine humility that does not collapse into self-contempt, and genuine confidence that does not inflate into pride. The person who truly understands grace knows they were lost (humility) and knows they are found (confidence). Neither posture is self-generated. Both come from outside, from the father who runs down the road.
