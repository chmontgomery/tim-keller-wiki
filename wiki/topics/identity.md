# Identity

## Summary [coverage: high — 21 sources]

No subject runs more deeply through Tim Keller's preaching than the question of who we are and what makes us someone. Across more than three decades of sermons — from a systematic three-part series in October–November 1989 ("Finding Our Identity in Christ," preached during his Growth in Christ series) through his late career apologetics series "Questioning Christianity" in 2014 — Keller returns again and again to a single diagnostic claim: the modern self is unstable because it is self-constructed.

Keller teaches that the human person was created to receive identity from God — to be named, known, and loved by the one who made us. The Fall in Genesis 3 is not merely a moral failure; it is an identity collapse. Adam and Eve reach for self-definition apart from God, and from that moment every human being is born into what Keller calls "the courtroom" — a daily trial in which we prosecute and defend ourselves, desperate for a verdict that never finally comes.

The gospel, Keller insists, offers something no other worldview can: the verdict before the performance. In Jesus Christ, God declares his people beloved before they have earned it, accepted before they deserve it, delighted in not because of what they have done but because of what Christ has done in their place. This verdict — "You are my beloved child in whom I am well pleased" — is the only foundation stable enough to bear the full weight of a human self.

The practical consequence Keller calls "blessed self-forgetfulness." The Christian is not trying to build a higher opinion of herself (the modern therapeutic answer) or suppress her ego through sheer moral discipline (the traditional moralistic answer). She is freed from thinking about herself at all. The ego, no longer empty and scrambling to fill itself, is filled up by God's love and stops demanding constant attention.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 21 sources]

### The Diagnosis: The Empty, Painful, Busy, Fragile Self

Keller's starting point is Paul's unusual Greek word for pride in 1 Corinthians 3–4: a word meaning literally "overinflated," "distended beyond its proper size" — the image of an organ swollen with air until it aches and threatens to burst. This image, Keller argues, describes the natural condition of every human ego. It is:

- **Empty** — Because the human heart was made for God, and anything else placed at its center is too small and rattles around in the space. Kierkegaard says in *Sickness Unto Death* that it is the normal state of the human heart to build identity on something other than God.
- **Painful** — The ego draws attention to itself constantly, the way a body part does only when something is wrong with it. You cannot get through a day without feeling snubbed, ignored, or diminished — which proves the self has something broken in it.
- **Busy** — Because it is empty and painful, the ego is relentlessly comparing and boasting. C.S. Lewis in *Mere Christianity* (the chapter on pride) notes that pride is essentially competitive: "Pride gets no pleasure out of having something, only having more of it than the next person."
- **Fragile** — What is overinflated is always on the verge of deflation. Superiority and inferiority complexes are the same condition: an ego puffed up with air, not filled with something solid.

Madonna, Keller notes in the Blessed Self-Forgetfulness sermon, articulated this perfectly: "My drive in life comes from the fear of being mediocre... I've become somebody, I still have to prove that I'm somebody. My struggle has never ended, and I guess it never will." She knows herself better than most people do, Keller says — because she sees clearly that the emptiness cannot be filled by accomplishment.

### The Root: The Fall as Identity Collapse

In Genesis 3, Keller locates the historical origin of this condition. When God says "Who told you that you were naked?" the question is the diagnostic: before the Fall, Adam and Eve received their identity, worth, and standing from God's prior love. They were naked and unashamed because they were held entirely in the gaze of the one who knew them perfectly and loved them completely. After the Fall, that covering is gone. They must cover themselves — with fig leaves, with accomplishments, with moral performance, with the exposure of other people's failures.

The "reach" in Genesis 3:22 — God's warning that humanity must not reach out and take from the Tree of Life — is a picture of the post-Fall human condition: we know we have lost God, but we cannot forget what we were made for. We keep reaching for love, success, meaning, and identity in things that can never finally give them. Every disillusionment — every marriage that doesn't satisfy the longing, every career win that leaves you immediately hungry for the next — is what Keller calls "cosmic disappointment." It is not psychological. It is ontological. We are reaching for a garden we were expelled from.

The "covering" in Genesis 3:21 — God making garments of skin — shows both the problem and its solution. Jean-Paul Sartre in *Being and Nothingness* (the chapter "The Look") captures the problem vividly: he is traumatized not merely because someone sees him, but because he is seen doing to another what he refuses to let anyone do to him. Nakedness is the universal human sense that there is something wrong with me that I must not let anyone see. We spend our lives covering (perfecting ourselves, performing for approval) and uncovering others (gossip, judgment, contempt) in order to manage the exposure. It does not work. Fig leaves are always drafty.

The "sword" in Genesis 3:24 — the flaming sword guarding the Tree of Life — is the justice of God. The way back to fullness of life is not scaling a wall; it is passing under a sword. A debt must be paid.

Jonathan Edwards' distinction in *The Nature of True Virtue* between "common virtue" (virtue motivated by fear and pride) and "true virtue" (virtue flowing from delight in God himself) illuminates why moral effort cannot solve the identity problem. Most human goodness is rooted in the very things that cause sin: fear of exposure and pride in comparative superiority. You cannot cure an ego-problem with morality that is itself ego-driven.

### The Gospel Answer: Verdict Before Performance

Paul in 1 Corinthians 4:3–4 maps out the only alternative: "I care very little if I am judged by you or by any human court. Indeed, I do not even judge myself." He refuses both options the modern world offers — caring what others think, or caring what he thinks. He exits the courtroom entirely. Why? "It is the Lord who judges me."

Keller's summary of the distinction: in every other religion, philosophy, and therapeutic approach, performance leads to the verdict. You work, achieve, obey, improve — and hope eventually to win a judgment that you matter, that you are enough. In Christianity alone, the verdict precedes the performance. The moment you believe, God says "You are my beloved child in whom I am well pleased." Romans 8:1: "There is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." The trial is over. Court is adjourned.

This is possible because Jesus went to trial in our place. He was condemned — unjustly, on a kangaroo charge — so that we would not be condemned. He took the sword. He went under the knife. He was "cut off from the land of the living" (Isaiah 53) so that we could re-enter the Garden.

John the Baptist in John 1:19–31 models the resulting identity. His self-assessment is radical: "I am not worthy to untie his sandals" — a status lower than a slave. But far from producing timidity, this produces extraordinary boldness. He confronts Herod to his face. He speaks without flinching before the entire religious establishment. Why? Because his sense of self comes neither from self-esteem (the inferiority complex healed by a superiority complex) nor from self-abasement (self-consciousness dressed as humility), but from looking away from himself entirely toward the Lamb. "For every one second John takes to look at himself, he takes an hour to look at his Savior."

### The Result: Blessed Self-Forgetfulness

Gospel humility, Keller argues, is not thinking more of yourself (the modern therapeutic prescription) or thinking less of yourself (the traditional moralistic prescription). It is thinking of yourself less. The ego that is filled up — not puffed up — simply stops demanding attention, the way healthy toes don't demand attention. It just works.

C.S. Lewis: "Do not imagine, if you meet a truly humble person, you would ever come away from him thinking he was humble. All you will remember is how much he seemed totally interested in you."

In practical terms, this identity:

- Is not devastated by criticism (you are not over-invested in other people's verdicts), but actually *listens* to criticism (you are not defensively protecting a fragile self-image)
- Does not lust for recognition, but is not frightened of it either
- Can enjoy other people's excellence — their triple jumps, their better arguments, their achievements — without jealousy, because your worth is not on the line
- Can enter a room asking "What can I give?" rather than "What will I get?" — because you are not there to fill a hole in yourself

Keller frames this identity as the structural root of Christian ethics. Why do Christians in the early Roman Empire combine sexual chastity with extravagant generosity — a combination no other worldview produced? Because both flow from an identity that no longer needs to perform, protect, or extract. The identity is given; the love can be spent freely.

### Sermon Notes

**Finding Our Identity in Christ – 1, 2, 3** (Oct–Nov 1989) is the earliest known systematic treatment of the identity theme in Keller's preaching catalog — predating "Christ Provides New Identity Papers" by a full year. Preached as an evening series titled "Growth in Christ, Part 1," these three sermons on Colossians 3 establish the core architecture that Keller would refine for thirty years. Sermon 1 defines *righteousness* as a relational verdict (finding favor, being received) rather than moral performance — a definition that becomes the load-bearing concept for everything else. It introduces the Kafka "Trial" analogy, the active/passive obedience distinction, the comparison to a defense attorney who demands acquittal on grounds of justice (not mercy), and the phrase that will echo through all future identity preaching: "Stop trying to steal self-acceptance from other sources and warm yourself at the fire of our standing in Jesus Christ." Sermon 2 develops the *passive righteousness* concept via Luther's Commentary on Galatians (which, Keller notes, converted Wesley, Bunyan, and others) and introduces Richard Lovelace's diagnostic that only a fraction of professing Christians are "solidly appropriating" their justification — leading to either legalism (works-righteousness dressed as Christianity) or inspirationalism (cheap grace that never confronts sin). The practical application: all anger, worry, and depression trace back to something operating as our righteousness being threatened. Sermon 3 introduces the *identity-as-layers* model from urban anthropologist Paul Hebert — Christian maturity is the long process of moving Christ-identity from an upper layer down to the foundational layer so that professional failure, romantic rejection, and political loss no longer shake the core. Paul's "Be what you are" imperative (Col 3:12ff) names the mechanism. The elder brother parable is re-read as a pointer to the real elder brother: Christ's robe, ring, and fatted calf were his, and he gave them away at dear cost.

**Blessed Self-Forgetfulness** (Feb 2002) is the landmark treatment of this theme and arguably the single most concentrated statement of Keller's theology of identity. It is the source of the phrase "blessed self-forgetfulness" and the "courtroom" metaphor. Paul's unusual Greek word for pride (overinflated, distended) anchors the whole argument.

**Christ Provides New Identity Papers** (Oct 1990) is one of Keller's earliest systematic treatments, using John the Baptist as the case study. Preached in the founding years of Redeemer, it introduces many ideas he would refine for decades: the three options (other people's verdict / my own verdict / God's verdict), the move from inferiority to superiority complex to gospel self-forgetfulness, and the "blessed realm" of not needing to think about oneself.

**An Identity That Doesn't Crush You or Others** (Feb 2014) addresses a secular, skeptical audience as part of the "Questioning Christianity" apologetics series. It is Keller's most culturally engaged treatment, engaging directly with Charles Taylor's concept of "expressive individualism" and arguing that identity built from within is structurally unstable.

**East of Eden** (Nov 2000) grounds the identity theme in the narrative of Genesis 3 through the triple images of reaching, covering, and sword. The Sartre analysis of nakedness as loss of control over one's own visibility is particularly strong here.

**A Promise of Hope** (Jul 1998) develops the Jonathan Edwards framework (common virtue vs. true virtue) and the definition of sin as self-enthronement. The key insight: you cannot cure the ego problem with moralism, because moralism is itself an ego strategy (fear and pride are both ego-defenses).

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 21 sources]

### Primary Texts

**Colossians 3:1–4, 12–17** — The earliest scriptural anchor for Keller's identity teaching. "Your life is now hidden with Christ in God. When Christ, who is your life, appears, then you also will appear with him in glory." Keller reads verse 3 as both a declarative statement and an identity claim: you have died, your self is no longer available as the referent of your worth. The practical implications emerge in verses 12–17: "As God's chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with compassion..." — the indicative precedes the imperative. Paul is not commanding Christians to become holy; he is commanding them to act from the holiness they already are in Christ. "Be what you are. Take hold of what you really are and the standing you really have and live on the platform of it." (Keller, Oct–Nov 1989)

**1 Corinthians 3:21–4:7** — The doctrinal anchor for Keller's teaching on self-forgetfulness. Paul's unusual word for pride (overinflated/distended), his double refusal to be judged by either others or himself ("I do not even judge myself"), and his declaration that "it is the Lord who judges me" provide the theological structure for the entire argument. The phrase "the verdict leads to performance" derives from this passage.

**John 1:19–31** — John the Baptist's threefold self-identification ("I am not the Christ," "I am not Elijah," "I am not the prophet") as a model of identity defined by what one is not and by whom one serves. His declaration "I am not worthy to untie his sandals" followed immediately by fearless confrontation of the religious establishment demonstrates self-forgetfulness in action. "Behold the Lamb of God" — the gaze moved entirely off the self and onto Christ — is the mechanism.

**Genesis 3:11–24 and 3:20–4:2** — The narrative anatomy of identity collapse. The sequence: (1) God's question ("Who told you that you were naked?") diagnoses the loss of received identity; (2) the fig leaves show the human attempt to cover the resulting inadequacy; (3) God's provision of garments of skin (the result of sacrifice) previews the gospel solution; (4) the flaming sword shows that re-entry to fullness requires justice to be satisfied.

**1 John 2:28–3:6** — The identity of adopted children of God. "Behold, what manner of love the Father has lavished on us, that we should be called children of God — and that is what we are." John cannot get over it. The redundancy ("that is what we are") is the point: beholding the lavishness of adoptive love is both the source of new identity and the remedy for most practical problems of the Christian life.

**Ephesians 2:11–18** — The social dimension of identity: how the gospel destroys the "comparison apparatus" by which humans build self-worth through exclusion and contempt. The "dividing wall of hostility" is not merely ethnic but structural to how identity forms without Christ.

### Supporting Texts

- **Romans 8:1** — "No condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." The verdict announced.
- **Isaiah 53** — The suffering servant, "cut off from the land of the living," whose substitution is the basis for all new identity claims.
- **Philippians 2:5–11** — The DNA of God: "I will not grasp equality... I will descend... I will serve." Contrasted with the DNA of the fall: "I will ascend, I will grasp, I will rule."
- **Luke 18:11** — The Pharisee's prayer as the compressed version of identity-through-exclusion: "I thank you that I am not like other men."
- **2 Peter 1:4** — "Partakers of the divine nature." Being born again means the family DNA of God — the "imperishable seed" of holiness — is planted in the believer.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 21 sources]

### Expressive Individualism and the Modern Self

Keller engages directly with Charles Taylor's account in *Sources of the Self* and *The Secular Age* of what Taylor calls "expressive individualism" — the modern belief that authentic selfhood is discovered by looking inward and expressing outward what you find there. "The self is authentic when it is self-authored," Taylor summarizes. Keller calls this the dominant cultural consensus in New York City and the operating assumption of most secular therapy: find out who you really are, be true to yourself, don't let others define you.

The problem, Keller argues, is structural. If identity must be self-generated, it can never be finally secure, because the self that is doing the generating is the same self whose stability is in question. You cannot pull yourself up by your own bootstraps. You cannot give yourself a verdict of worthiness; you are both judge and defendant, and the verdict you render is always suspect.

### The Self-Esteem Debate

Keller engages the self-esteem movement directly, citing a February 2002 New York Times Magazine article by psychologist Lauren Slater ("The Trouble with Self-Esteem") which reported what researchers had been finding for years: high self-esteem does not produce prosocial behavior and may increase aggression. Keller uses this to reframe the debate: the problem is not the quantity of self-esteem (too high or too low) but its structural instability. Whether inflated or deflated, the ego puffed up on air is the problem. The gospel offers something qualitatively different, not just a better version of the same thing.

### New York City: Career Anxiety and the Identity Resume

Keller's NYC context suffuses his identity preaching. He speaks directly to:

- **Overwork** — The compulsive New York work ethic as fig-leaf covering: working to patch up a sense of inadequacy, to control what people know about you, to ensure you are never exposed as insufficient. "Why did you move to New York when you knew you'd just work and work and work?"
- **Romantic rejection** — The inability to date someone "two notches down in coolness" as identity protection: you have worked hard to arrive at your current social tier and cannot afford the downgrade.
- **The resume life** — Keller's image for the ego always building its self-worth portfolio: doing things not for their intrinsic worth but for what they prove about you. He confesses to doing things "I had absolutely no interest in for themselves, but I was putting together a resume."
- **Social comparison on the left and right** — In a secular city where explicit class or race contempt is taboo, Keller notes that identity-through-exclusion simply migrates to politics: "the idiots who are ruining the world."

### Identity as Layers: An Anthropological Model

In the 1989 series, Keller draws on urban anthropologist Paul Hebert's framework for how identity actually works. Every person carries multiple identity-labels (profession, nationality, political affiliation, religion) arranged in layers, with some more foundational than others. A lawyer whose deepest identity-layer is his professional excellence will be shaken mainly by career failure; one whose deepest layer is ethnic belonging will be controlled primarily by family dynamics. Christian maturity, Keller argues, is not adding Christianity to the stack — it is the long, slow process of moving it from the top layer down to the bottom layer, so that it becomes the most fundamental thing: the thing you are when everything else has been stripped away. The practical effect: the further down Christ goes, the more open and undefended you become in every other layer. You are no longer frightened by competing ideas (your identity doesn't rest on winning arguments), racial difference (your identity doesn't rest on tribal belonging), or professional failure (your identity doesn't rest on achievement). "A truly committed, sold-out Christian does not become a fanatic or a bigot. They become more open-minded." (Keller, Nov 1989)

### Literature and Philosophy

Keller draws richly from outside sources to diagnose the identity crisis:

- **C.S. Lewis**, *Mere Christianity*, chapter on pride: "Pride is essentially competitive... Pride gets no pleasure out of having something, only having more of it than the next person." Also: "Humility is not thinking more of yourself or less of yourself; it is thinking of yourself less."
- **Søren Kierkegaard**, *Sickness Unto Death*: "It is the normal state of the human heart to build its identity around something besides God."
- **Jean-Paul Sartre**, *Being and Nothingness*, "The Look": The experience of being seen through a keyhole — being the object of an unviewed viewer — as the primal human horror, which Keller uses to expose the deeper problem: Sartre is traumatized not by being seen but by being seen doing exactly what he refuses to let be done to him. Nakedness = the deep human sense of inadequacy that must be controlled and concealed.
- **Jonathan Edwards**, *The Nature of True Virtue*: The distinction between common virtue (fear and pride) and true virtue (delight in God) as the key to understanding why moralism cannot solve the ego problem.
- **Hannibal Lecter** (*Silence of the Lambs*): The serial killer's challenge to Clarice Starling ("I happened. You can't reduce me to a set of influences") as a slam on the modern therapeutic worldview that explains evil through external causes and thus cannot account for radical moral evil.
- **Madonna**: Keller quotes a Vogue interview at length — "My drive in life comes from the fear of being mediocre... I've become somebody. I still have to prove that I'm somebody. My struggle has never ended." He calls her more self-aware than most, because she sees clearly what fame cannot give her.
- **Dylan Thomas**: "Do not go gentle into that good night. Rage, rage against the dying of the light" — used to argue that the universal human resistance to death proves we know we were made for more than this life.
- **Voltaire**: "Tormented atoms in a bed of mud" — his phrase for the human condition, used to show that even secular thinkers feel the cosmic dissonance between what we are and what we sense we should be.
- **Irma Bombeck**: Her essay on housekeeping — "You spend all your life fighting the dirt, and at the end, you get six feet of it" — cited as an unwitting gloss on Genesis 3:19.
- **Franz Kafka**, *The Trial*: Joseph K. wakes on his 30th birthday to find himself arrested for a crime no one will name. He spends the novel cataloguing his failures and patching up his own justification — "maybe I've been arrested for this, I didn't know anyone knew that one" — growing increasingly unable to hold himself together under scrutiny. Keller uses the novel (1989, 1990) as a picture of the universal human condition: every person senses they are under indictment and knows the verdict will be catastrophic, which drives either desperate religious performance (patch up the righteousness) or desperate secular escape (deny the courtroom exists entirely). Neither works.
- **George Whitefield**, *The Method of Grace* (sermon on Jer 6): Keller quotes at length (1989) Whitefield's claim that a person must be made sick not only of their sins but of their *righteousness* — "God may damn you for the best prayer you ever put up." The pride hiding inside religious effort is the last idol taken out of the heart.
- **Richard Lovelace**, *Dynamics of Spiritual Life*: "Only a fraction of the present body of professing Christians are solidly appropriating the justifying work of Christ in their lives." Lovelace diagnoses two failure modes — those with so light a view of sin that they feel no need for justification, and those who rely on their sanctification (religious performance, conversion experience, "relative infrequency of willful disobedience") for their justification. Keller uses Lovelace's framework in 1989 to diagnose both legalism and inspirationalism as forms of the same disease.

### The Early Church as Social Evidence

Keller's sermon "Christ Provides New Identity Papers" opens with the question: why did Christianity sweep through Rome, which was more jaded, sophisticated, and secular than New York City? The answer: Christians displayed a combination of virtues that broke all cultural molds — sexually chaste *and* extravagantly generous to the poor; committed to family *and* radically egalitarian across class and race. This stereotype-breaking combination was not the result of a social program but of a restructured identity. When you no longer need to use others to feel good about yourself, you can love them.

> "We're the most unhappy creatures in all of nature. Because what we see around ourselves... we're tormented atoms. Because we hate the idea that we're only atoms."

> "Your perfectionism is a fig leaf. Your work is a fig leaf. Your makeup is a fig leaf. Your holding onto your youth is a fig leaf."

> "If you uncover to me, I will cover you. But if you refuse to uncover to me, you will spend all your life desperately trying to patch up your fig leaves."

> "She says, and when God saw them, he was not ashamed to call us brothers. But Jesus Christ, at the cost of his life, said: I'm not afraid. I'm not ashamed to call you brothers."

### From "A New Humanity" (Eph 2:11–18, Nov 2011)

> "There's something in the human heart that takes what's good about you, lifts it up to an absolute value, then looks at everyone who doesn't have what you have, and causes you to look down on them. We get our identity by excluding others."

> "The gospel destroys the comparison apparatus, the pecking order apparatus, that every other human being has in how they develop their identity."

> "When you become a Christian, the very way you think of yourself, the very way you have an identity, is restructured in two ways: the gospel destroys the comparison apparatus, and it reshuffles the layers in your identity deck."

### From "Born of Him" (1 John 2:28–3:6, Mar 1995)

> "He can't get over it. He'd say it again if he could. He's amazed by the love of God. And until you're amazed by it, until you feel that this is a miracle, until you feel like you're awash in it, you have not been born again."

> "To the degree you behold the lavishness of God's adoptive love, to that degree you'll handle criticism, you'll handle worry, you'll handle everything and anything."

> "Behold. All of your problems come because you're not beholding. If you're a Christian, your problems will start to break apart when you actually behold that you are a child of God."

### From "A Promise of Hope" (Gen 3:11–24, Jul 1998)

> "The heart of sin is to put yourself on the throne. The minute you say, 'Should I obey?' you've already disobeyed. If you don't sin, but you did the right thing out of fear or pride — you sinned. Your goodness is rotten at the root."

> "Most people are nurturing within their moral life sin — underneath the goodness over the years. You're not dealing with fear and pride. You're nurturing them."
