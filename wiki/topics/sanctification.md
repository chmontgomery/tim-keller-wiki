# Sanctification

## Summary [coverage: high — 33 sources]

Sanctification — the lifelong process of becoming holy, of being conformed to the image of Christ — sits at the center of Keller's pastoral and theological vision. He teaches it not as a self-improvement program layered onto an otherwise unchanged life, but as the outward and gradual expression of a radical inward revolution: the new birth. From his earliest sermons at a small Virginia congregation in 1990 through his mature preaching at Redeemer Presbyterian in New York City, Keller returns again and again to a single governing conviction: you do not obey in order to become a Christian; you obey because you already are one. Growth in holiness is the natural, organic unfolding of a seed already planted — the seed of the divine nature itself, implanted at conversion by the Holy Spirit.

Keller insists that the greatest enemy of real sanctification is not obvious immorality but subtle moralism. Religious self-improvement — heaping up virtuous behaviors without a changed center — produces what he calls a life as beautiful as a snowflake: intricate, symmetrical, and freezing. He distinguishes sharply between being *new* and merely being *nice*, between organic growth from a transformed soul and mechanical growth that amounts to throwing more bricks on a pile. The former produces a person who is simultaneously tougher and tenderer, wiser and humbler, more deeply joyful and more capable of grief — all at once. The latter produces spiritual pride, coldness toward other people, and an inability to handle criticism or failure, because the self-righteousness underneath has never been touched.

The means by which sanctification advances are what the Reformed tradition calls the means of grace — the Word of God (studied intently and allowed to act as a mirror, exposing the self), prayer, the Lord's Supper, and the believing community — but Keller is careful to insist that these means work not by information transfer or willpower alone, but by showing us the beauty of what Jesus Christ has done. The gospel is not merely the door through which a person enters the Christian life; it is the engine that drives growth for the entire journey. To be filled with the Spirit, as Ephesians 5 commands, is to be saturated with this truth until it heightens and melts us, dealing with worry and sin and self-obsession from the inside out rather than from the outside in.

Holiness, Keller argues, is not merely moral uprightness but total devotion — being "wholly set apart" for God, with no area of life withheld. Just as the Holy Spirit is the agent of regeneration and sanctification alike, so the goal of the sanctified life is a community as well as a character: a holy people, distinct from the world by their integrity, sympathy, chastity, and generosity. And suffering — the "wilderness" — is not a detour from sanctification but one of its primary instruments. It is in the desert that you meet the rock, and in the rock that you find honey.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 33 sources]

### The Diagnosis: The Ultimate Human Problem

Keller's starting point is a diagnosis he borrows from the philosopher Jacob Needleman: we know how we ought to live, but we are mysteriously helpless to actually do it. This is not primarily a problem of information. Becky Pippert sat in a Harvard psychology seminar and asked, "How is the therapist going to help him forgive his mother?" The professor replied: "If you're looking for a forgiving heart, you're in the wrong department. We can't give you a new heart." That, says Keller, is the whole problem in one sentence. Moses looks down the corridors of time in Deuteronomy 30 and says to Israel: You will fail. You know the law; you cannot keep it. This is not Israel's distinctive failure — it is the entire human race writ large.

The deeper issue is not ignorance but the *heart*. In the Bible, the heart is not the seat of sentiment — it is the seat of the entire acting self, the place where the most fundamental commitments live. Whatever a person most hopes in, most lives for, most fears losing — that is the heart's true object. And the unregenerate heart, no matter how educated or disciplined or religiously observant, organizes itself around something other than God. It looks to career, relationships, money, independence, or reputation to give it what only God can supply: significance, security, and delight. Our pleasure and our duty remain opposed. We know we ought to love God with all our heart, but our heart is fixed on lesser things.

The result is what Keller calls slavery — a slavery more subtle and comprehensive than political tyranny. "Everyone who sins is a slave to sin" (John 8:34). You are controlled by whatever you live for. The person who has made reputation an idol cannot take criticism; the person who has made security an idol cannot be generous; the person who has made independence itself an idol cannot commit to anyone or anything. The self is fragmented, its desires at war with one another. There is no simple technique to fix this, because the problem goes all the way down to the root.

Keller illustrates this with his early Methodists' prayer checklist — questions that probe whether you have actually loved God with all your heart and neighbor as yourself. When you go through them with real honesty, they drop you in the dust. Anyone who reads the Sermon on the Mount and thinks it is achievable by effort has simply not understood what the Sermon on the Mount actually requires. The standard is not behavioral compliance but the comprehensive union of heart and will that only supernatural intervention can produce.

### The Solution: A New Heart, a New Birth, God's Workmanship

The gospel answer is not a new rule, a new discipline, or a new effort — it is a new heart. In Deuteronomy 30:6, Moses speaks the most astonishing promise in the book: "The Lord your God will circumcise your hearts." Not: *you* will circumcise your hearts through obedience. God will do it. The new heart is a gift, not an achievement.

What does a circumcised heart mean? Keller draws out the metaphor carefully. Circumcision in Israel represented external, covenantal commitment — obedience performed because one *had to*. A circumcised heart is one in which that external duty has become internal desire. It is when the thing you most *ought* to do becomes the thing you most *want* to do. The old mentor's maxim Keller quotes gets at this precisely: "The greatness of God demands a love that cannot merely be a response to a demand."

The new birth — described variously as regeneration, being born again (John 3), a circumcised heart (Deuteronomy 30), a new heart (Ezekiel 36), and being made partakers of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4) — is the seed from which sanctification grows. It is not the full tree, but the entire tree is within it, the way a redwood is within an acorn. All the growth that follows is the natural unfolding of this implanted life. You have not simply been given new instructions; you have been given a new nature. "For we are God's workmanship, created in Christ Jesus to do good works" (Ephesians 2:10). The Greek word *poema* — poem, work of art — shows that God is the sculptor and you are the marble. He is "persistent, exacting, and venerable" about the project. The troubles that knock pieces off you are not accidents; they are his chisel.

In John 3, the vine-and-branches image makes the intimacy even more vivid. The branch does not *influence* the vine from outside; it is *interpenetrated* by the vine's life. Our union with Christ is not that of a student to a teacher or a child to a parent; it is a participation in the divine nature itself. This is the engine of all genuine sanctification.

### The Pattern: Gospel-Motivated Effort in Community

Once the new heart is received, there is work to do — but it is a different kind of work. Keller refuses both poles of the classic error: passive quietism ("let go and let God") and moralistic activism ("try harder"). Both miss the gospel dynamic.

The tension Keller navigates most carefully is between the indicative and the imperative in Paul's letters — between what God has already done and what we are therefore called to do. The imperative never comes first and never stands alone. Martin Luther, studying the perfect law of God, felt not freedom but condemnation — the more intently he looked, the more condemned he felt. The breakthrough came when he saw not only himself in the law but the one who had *perfectly fulfilled* the law. Now his motivation for obedience changed entirely. He no longer kept the law to earn acceptance; he kept it to delight the one who had earned acceptance *for* him. That transformation of motive is what gospel-driven sanctification looks like.

Holiness, Keller argues from 1 Peter and Deuteronomy 7, is essentially *total devotion*. The Hebrew *kadosh* — to be cut off, set apart — means being wholly God's, with no rival. A pot in the temple was holy not because it was morally pure but because it was exclusively dedicated to God's use. The Pharisees were moral but not holy, because their morality served their self-esteem and social standing rather than God. David's mighty men illustrate the posture of total devotion perfectly: they overheard their king *sigh* a desire, and without a command or a request they went into enemy territory to fulfill it. That is what a holy heart looks like — so entirely given to God that his desires become "glad compulsions."

Sanctification never happens in isolation. Keller repeatedly insists that "babies have to live in families." Spiritual newborns need community. And it is in community that holiness becomes visible and counterculturally compelling: the holy people of God become a "royal priesthood," a community whose integrity, sympathy, chastity, and generosity make the world stare. The early church grew not through public meetings but through the supernatural attractiveness of its common life.

### The Role of Suffering: Honey from the Rock

One of the most distinctive threads in Keller's sanctification teaching is the role of suffering as a sanctifying instrument. Life, he teaches from Psalm 81, is a wilderness — a desert that cannot support the deepest needs of the human heart, a "down escalator" subject to entropy and decay. The second law of thermodynamics is a spiritual reality: if you do nothing, things go bad. Character, like trumpet-playing, erodes without active cultivation.

But the wilderness is also where you meet the rock. Moses met God in the burning bush in the wilderness. Elijah heard the still small voice in the wilderness. Jesus went into the wilderness to pray. The very things that strip away your false securities — health, career, relationships, reputation — are what position you to discover that God is actually the rock, the shelter, the light, and the water. The wilderness makes God existentially real rather than merely notionally true.

And in the rock, Keller argues from Psalm 81, there is *honey*. God does not merely sustain you through suffering; he transmutes it. The humiliation of the wilderness — discovering you are nowhere near as courageous or as patient or as spiritual as you thought — is precisely how you become forgiving, because you can no longer feel superior to others. You cannot get a genuinely forgiving spirit without first being humbled. Sweetness comes from the rock. The three disciplines that make the difference between sour and sweet are corporate worship (rejoicing on schedule, whether or not you feel like it), scriptural meditation (listening for God's voice in the Word), and self-examination for idols (naming and repenting of the magnified desires that intensify your suffering).

The ultimate ground of confidence in suffering is the cross: Jesus Christ is "the rock" who went before Israel in the wilderness (1 Corinthians 10:4), and at the cross he took the rod that fell on us. Which means you can face the wilderness without terror. The rod has already fallen on him. He can say, "I will be with thee thy troubles to bless, and sanctify to thee thy deepest distress."

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 33 sources]

Keller returns to a constellation of passages across his sanctification preaching. These are the texts he treats as foundational:

**Deuteronomy 7:6–11** — "The Lord did not set his affection on you because you were more numerous… but because the Lord loved you." The doctrine of election, Keller argues in *Grace – Therefore, Holy*, is the most radically humbling doctrine in the Bible: it produces not arrogance but deep security. Chosen by sheer grace, believers form a counterculture — a holy nation — whose sex, money, and power function differently from the world around them.

**Deuteronomy 30:6** — "The Lord your God will circumcise your hearts." The promise of a new heart is the hinge of the entire Deuteronomy series. Keller reads this as the Old Testament's most concentrated statement of the gospel's transforming power: not behavioral modification but the surgical transformation of the motivational center.

**Joshua 5:13–6:5** — The appearance of the pre-incarnate commander of the Lord's army before Jericho. In *The Holy One*, Keller draws three lessons: the nature of God's incomparable holiness, the nature of holy servanthood (total and unconditional devotion, like David's mighty men), and the identity of the one who "sanctified himself" for us — Jesus Christ, who was holy *for* us so that we could be holy *for* him.

**Psalm 51** — David's prayer of repentance after his sin with Bathsheba. In *Healing of Sin (Part 2)*, Keller shows that real repentance cuts down in four steps: seeing sin as God sees it ("in thy sight"), taking full responsibility without excuses (Jonathan Edwards: "circumstances shape your sin but never cause it"), melting the heart down through the grace of God rather than through fear of punishment, and then forsaking sin permanently — not in isolated crises of guilt, but through a whole new life of "continual repentance." The fire that melts the metal is not a hammer but a flame: the flame of God's unfailing love (*hesed*). David sinned because he lost the joy of his salvation, and he lost the joy of his salvation because he sinned. Only when that deeper sin-beneath-the-sin is named can lasting change occur.

**Psalm 81** — The Feast of Tabernacles psalm. In *Honey from the Rock*, Keller develops the wilderness paradigm: life is a desert that cannot sustain the deepest human longings; the rock (God himself) is present in the wilderness; and the honey — sweetness, joy, gratitude, character — comes only through passing the test of the wilderness by corporate worship, scriptural meditation, and repentance of idols. Jesus passed the test we fail, as our substitute, so the rod need not fall on us.

**Isaiah 11:1–10** — The messianic king who comes as a shoot from the stump of Jesse and yet also the root of Jesse. In *Healing From Decay*, Keller argues that this king's wisdom "turns the paradigms of the world on their head" — he comes as a poor carpenter's son and achieves world-historical impact through the upside-down logic of the cross. This is the wisdom of God that the world calls foolishness, and it is the same wisdom that underlies the gospel's approach to character transformation.

**Micah 6:6–8** — "What does the Lord require of you? To act justly, love mercy, and walk humbly with your God." In *Goodness*, Keller unpacks the context: the two wrong answers (sacrifices, self-inflicted pain) cannot atone for sin; only the sacrifice of God's own firstborn son can. The right answer — do justice, love mercy, walk with your God — is the *conduct* of a relationship already established by grace. Walking with God means exposure (total accountability), friendship (experiential prayer), and *progress* — actually growing in love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, and self-control.

**John 15:1–11** — The vine and the branches. In *Growth in Grace*, Keller argues that growth is both possible (because of union with Christ) and inevitable (because life without abiding is a down escalator). The two means of abiding are letting Christ's *words* remain in you (digesting scripture until it becomes part of your psyche) and remaining in Christ's *love* (living out of the gospel on every specific problem — jealousy, anxiety, touchiness — rather than treating them as free-standing issues).

**John 17:6–24** — Jesus's high-priestly prayer. In *Healing Truth*, Keller focuses on verse 17: "Sanctify them in the truth. Thy word is truth." The modus operandi of sanctification is the truth. God does not zap holiness from heaven; he works through truth engaged by the Spirit. The Christian is neither passive (waiting for power) nor moralistic (self-assaulting the will directly). The Spirit engages the will *through* truth: you see the truth, grasp it, and the power comes.

**Ephesians 2:8–10** — "We are God's workmanship (*poema*), created in Christ Jesus to do good works." In *God's Workmanship*, Keller argues that being comes before doing. You cannot do good works without first being made God's artwork. And God is "persistent, exacting, and venerable" in his love for his work — he bleeds for your splendor. The troubles that chip away at you are his chisel. Every hammer blow, even the bad things, is bringing out the beauty the artist always saw in the marble.

**Ephesians 5:3–7** — The negative side of Christian holiness. In *God's Holy People (Part 2)*, Keller argues there must be both a negative and a positive to authentic Christian faith — neither "Jesus for me" (all grace, no obedience) nor "me for Jesus" (all obedience, no grace) but both together, organically. The positive (the fire of grace softening the wax of the heart) is what makes the negative possible; and the negative (actually saying no to certain things) is irreplaceable. Archibald Alexander's metaphor: the Spirit is the flame, the heart is the wax, and the truth of the Word is the seal — when the Spirit softens the heart through meditation on grace, the seal permanently shapes the wax.

**1 Peter 1:13–21** — "Be holy because I am holy." In *Holiness: Overview*, Keller argues that holiness as total devotion means being "wholly set apart" for God — not a department of your life but its entire organizing principle. Like the Olympian who can no longer say "that's the training part of my week" because everything must now serve the goal, so holiness means all of life comes under God's claim. The reason this is possible: Jesus Christ, as the commander of the Lord's army, "sanctified himself" for us — he focused everything on going to the cross. Because he was holy *for* us, we can be holy *for* him.

**1 John 2:28–3:6** — "Everyone who does what is right has been born of him." In *Born of Him*, Keller distinguishes: doing right does not *produce* being a child of God; being a child of God *produces* doing right. The new birth — God's DNA, his imperishable seed — is acidic toward sin; it will eat through impurity over time. The question is not whether you sin (the text is not saying true Christians never sin) but whether God's seed is there, growing, changing the prevailing habit patterns of your character.

**1 John 3:1–10** — "God's seed remains in him." In *Growing in Grace*, Keller develops the same seed metaphor further: the seed of God comes in not as a tidal wave but as a seed — gradually, mysteriously, imperceptibly. Trees grow in winter even when no growth is visible. An oak tree will crack a concrete slab, though slowly. You are settling for far too little when you say you cannot change. The question for long-term Christians: is someone who knows you well able to say you are less irritable, less anxious, more honest than you were last year?

**Romans 8:5–17** — The Holy Spirit and Christian growth. In *Assurance and Witness of the Spirit*, Keller describes the Spirit's three main ministries: regeneration (the new birth), sanctification (gradually conforming us to Christ), and the anointing of power (the "high assurance" that comes in seasons and cannot be manufactured). The Spirit is not an electrical charge or a liquid but a person. To be filled with the Spirit means to have that person's desires influence yours. The Spirit never says "look at me" — he is the hidden floodlight trained on Jesus Christ.

**2 Peter 1:4** — Being made "partakers of the divine nature." Conversion plants the actual nature of God in seed form in the believer. The seed contains the whole tree — all that radiance and goodness and greatness — but it must be fertilized and watered and given proper soil.

**Galatians 5:22–23** — The fruit of the Spirit. The Christian life produces love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, gentleness, faithfulness, and self-control not by following a virtue list but as the organic expression of a Spirit-transformed heart.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 33 sources]

### The Technological Society and the Hunger for Authenticity

In *Be Filled with the Spirit*, Keller opens with an extended diagnosis of modernity's spiritual situation. Jacques Ellul's concept of "the technological society" — a culture that reduces everything to procedures and commodities — has created a hunger for authentic experience. But the church often responds by commoditizing spiritual experience in turn: "Three Easy Steps to Being Filled with the Spirit." This doubles the error. The Spirit is not a technique. Keller argues that in 1850, you knew who Abraham Lincoln was by his arguments; today, what you look like is everything, because appearance can be manufactured while character cannot.

The same technological culture has created, paradoxically, enormous spiritual hunger. People rushed from plastic culture into drugs, mysticism, and experience-seeking — anything that felt like reality. Keller addresses both sides of this hunger: yes, the Spirit-filled life is genuinely stimulating and experiential; but the stimulation comes from a *heightened* grasp of truth, not a narrowed one. Alcohol deals with worry by narrowing your field of vision so you can't see the monster. The Spirit deals with worry by expanding your field of vision so you see the sovereign Lord behind the monster.

### The Down Escalator: Life as Entropy

In *Growth in Grace* and *Honey from the Rock*, Keller uses the second law of thermodynamics as a cultural touchstone. Life is a down escalator. Your trumpet playing, your French, your tennis, your body — if you do nothing to them, they decay. The same is true of character. Time itself, says Keller, pulls you toward harder, more cynical, more addicted, more anxious. The bookstore is full of self-help books precisely because we all sense we are a bud that hasn't blossomed, an egg that needs to hatch. The Christian vision of union with Christ as the engine of growth against this entropy is genuinely counter-cultural: you can participate in the divine nature and "escape the decay that is in the world through lust" (2 Peter 1:4).

### New York City, Career, Identity, and the Question of Freedom

For the young professional class of New York, "freedom" is a sacred word — and the Bible's call to obedience sounds like its opposite. Keller engages this directly in *Active Discipline* with the image of "Dependence Day." Americans celebrate Independence Day as the throwing off of tyrants. But the Bible says your real liberation happens when you finally declare your *dependence* on God — not because God is a cosmic killjoy, but because you are most free when living in accordance with your deepest design, the way a fish is most free when it is in water rather than on hot pavement.

In *Grace – Therefore, Holy*, Keller turns to the intersection of identity and election. Manhattan is awash in cultural identities — racial, gender, professional — each one putting enormous pressure on its adherents. "If you're Asian, certain things have to be true of you for your culture to say you're a good man or good woman." But if you are chosen by sheer grace, your ultimate identity is settled not by what you achieve or how you perform but by the fact that God loves you just because he loves you — "because he loved you because he loved you." That identity relativizes every cultural identity without destroying it. Korean Christians are still Korean; they are just Korean second. The pressure comes off.

### The Brady Bunch, the Snowflake, and True Virtue

In *Growing in Grace*, Keller uses a review of the *Brady Bunch* movie as a cultural entry point into the difference between moralistic virtue and gospel-produced virtue. The Brady Bunch depicted a family with all the virtues that cultural traditionalists call for — and the reviewer found them "cold, one-dimensional, unappealing, unable to relate to the broken." This is the fundamental problem: religion in general can make you moral, but it can also produce a morality that is (in the famous image) as beautiful as a snowflake — "intricate, symmetrical, perfect, and freezing." What the Bible is after is not Brady Bunch tidiness but the full-grace-and-truth portrait that Jesus showed to the woman caught in adultery: "This is a sin, and I don't condemn you." That combination — neither cheap grace (who knows what sin is?) nor cold law (therefore I condemn you) — is what the Spirit produces, and it is genuinely compelling to broken people.

### Hope, Fairy Tales, and C.S. Lewis on the Inconsolable Longing

In *Hope That Transforms*, Keller develops an extended cultural argument through C.S. Lewis and J.R.R. Tolkien. Every human heart is "hopelessly hope-shaped." We long for escape from death, love without parting, good triumphing over evil, communion with something beyond the natural world. Every age produces fairy stories because these stories "wet the desire unbearably" — satisfying it momentarily while making it more acute. Tolkien's insight: when every myth achieves that "turn" — the darkest hour suddenly and gracefully reversed — you are glimpsing what Christianity calls the gospel. Jesus is not one more myth pointing to this underlying reality; he *is* the underlying reality to which all the myths point, and he is historically true. This "myth became fact," as Lewis wrote, and it answers the longing that realistic fiction can never scratch.

Keller applies this to sanctification: the Christian hope is not just a ticket to heaven but a vision of what you are being made into. C.S. Lewis's description — "pulsating all through with such energy and joy and wisdom and love as we cannot now imagine" — is not poetry but prophecy. That is what is coming out of you through the work of the Spirit. "Give up your small ambitions."

### The Idol of Ornaments (and the NYC Investment Portfolio)

In *A Leader's Passion*, Keller uses Israel's stripping off their ornaments in Exodus 33 to show how idolatry is diagnosed through money. In the ancient world, ornaments *were* your wealth — wearable capital. Where money flows most effortlessly, there is your heart. Keller then turns personal: he spends money on books "like that" — without even looking at the price. Why? Because his functional idol is being known as an expert, being the person people come to for answers. The books feed the idol. "The best way to know what your idols are and to starve them out is to check out your money."

### The Pyongyang Revival and the Spirit's Power

In *Assurance and Witness of the Spirit*, Keller reads extended excerpts from missionary William Blair's account of the 1907 Korean Pentecost. What arrested Keller was the cultural irony: the Spirit's first visible sign in an honor-shame culture was mass public confession of sin — the very thing that culture most fears. You cannot explain that humanly. Keller uses this to argue that when the Spirit of God falls in power, the consequences are unmistakably supernatural: not just tears but *reparation*, not just confession but restoration of stolen property to non-Christians across the city. "A power has come into our church that we could not control even if we wanted to." This anointing — what the old writers called *unction* — is different from the steady daily work of sanctification. It comes and goes; you cannot manufacture it. But you can build an altar.

> "Society says freedom is the absence of all restrictions. But freedom is not the absence of restrictions — it is finding the right restrictions, the restrictions that comport with your nature, with who you are. The Bible is your owner's manual." *(A People Under the Word)*

**On being filled with the Spirit:**
> "To be filled with the Spirit and to be filled with the Word is the same thing. The Spirit of God basically works through truth. Don't be drunk with wine, but be filled with the Spirit — meaning, get what the bottle promises, but get it rightly, from him." *(Be Filled with the Spirit Part 1)*

**On the Spirit-filled life and worry:**
> "How does alcohol deal with worry? By depressing your understanding of reality, by narrowing down your vision so you don't see the monster. How does the Spirit deal with worry? By heightening it, expanding it, showing you that the monster himself is just a small thing because there is a sovereign Lord of history who loves you." *(Be Filled with the Spirit Part 1)*

**On kindness and the gospel:**
> "Real spiritual kindness takes a radical humility and a radical confidence. Where are you going to get both? If we're doing well in life, we're confident but not humble. If we're not doing well, we're humble but not confident. It's the kindness of Jesus — nailed open, constant — that both humbles you (you were so bad he had to die for you) and affirms you (you're so valuable he wanted to die for you). When you put those two things together, then and only then will you be able to live a life of love." *(Befriending Grace)*

**On community and the new birth:**
> "Babies have to live in families. They cannot survive on their own. If you're spiritually newborn, you need to be completely committed to a particular body of believers." *(Born to Grow)*

**On the continual need for renewal:**
> "The fire of God's presence, unless you tend it — the second you stop tending it — burns down. An awful lot of us have had an experience of God, and it's been a long time since then. The spiritual entropy of your heart will always draw you down, away from him, toward cold." *(A Leader's Passion)*

**On the test of genuine change:**
> "Jonathan Edwards: I'm glad you like Tim Keller's sermons. Maybe they make you weep. But I don't care. Are the people who know you best saying that you are becoming more and more unselfish, more and more generous, more and more kind, more and more self-controlled as the weeks go by? If you're not changing in the way you actually live, then you don't have that new heart." *(A New Heart)*

**On dependence and freedom:**
> "The day you become a Christian is the day you finally become free. It's your Dependence Day. It's the exact opposite of what the world says. The Bible says that only when you're willing to be completely dependent on God do you begin to experience what freedom really is." *(Active Discipline)*

**On God as artist:**
> "I'm going to die, I'm going to bleed for your splendor. I'm out to turn you into something splendid, magnificent. I'm the artist. You're the art. I'm the painter. You're the canvas. I'm the sculptor. You're the marble. You don't look like much there in the quarry, but I can see." *(God's Workmanship)*

**On the seed of God and the will to change:**
> "You know what's in a poppy seed? A poppy. You know what's in a tree seed? A tree. You know what's in an apple seed? An apple. You know what's in God's seed? The glory of God. That means that though it may start small — do you know what kind of power you've got inside you?" *(Growing in Grace)*

**On the positive and negative in Christian life:**
> "There's the Jesus for me churches and there's the me for Jesus churches. The gospel is both — not in a mechanical, slapped-together way, but organically. The reason I am for Jesus is because he's for me." *(God's Holy People Part 2)*

**On repentance and the sin beneath the sin:**
> "He says, Restore to me the joy of my salvation. We think: he lost the joy of his salvation because he sinned. Yes. But also: he sinned because he lost the joy of his salvation. We only ever sin because we've lost the joy of our salvation." *(Healing of Sin Part 2)*

**On holiness as total devotion:**
> "Those of you who tend to be kind of irritable — you get compassionate. Those of you who tend to be very impatient — you get patient. Real righteousness is a supernatural thing that grows and breaks through the barriers of your personality and temperament." *(Born of Him)*

**On the wilderness and suffering:**
> "Make me your rock in the wilderness, because all of the rocks will crumble. Make me your light when all of the lights go out. Make me your water when all of the wells dry up. Don't you see now that in this life, all of the wells will dry up, all of the lights will go out, all of the rocks will crumble, but me." *(Honey from the Rock)*

**On sanctification through truth:**
> "Sanctify them in the truth. Thy word is truth. On the one hand, you can't be passive — you cannot stick the medical textbook under your pillow and sleep on it and have that be enough. On the other hand, God never simply assaults the will directly. He engages the will with the truth. The power comes as you see the truth." *(Healing Truth)*

**On goodness as newness:**
> "C.S. Lewis says: He can make the feeblest and filthiest of us into dazzling, radiant, immortal creatures, pulsating all through with such energy and joy and wisdom and love as we cannot now imagine. Get a vision for newness of life." *(Goodness)*

**On election and humility:**
> "God's people are his chosen people, but they're not his choice people. To be chosen is not to be choice. He didn't choose you because you were better. There's nothing in you that's different. The only reason we have a relationship is that he simply chose you. And that's radically humbling." *(Grace – Therefore, Holy)*
