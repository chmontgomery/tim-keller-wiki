# Spiritual Warfare

## Summary [coverage: high — 15 sources]

Tim Keller approaches spiritual warfare with the sobriety of the Reformed tradition and the pastoral realism of a pastor who has spent decades in one of the world's most secular cities. He does not sensationalize. He does not dismiss. He takes the biblical teaching on demonic powers with full seriousness, and he addresses head-on the modern Western embarrassment about the subject — treating the secular skeptic's discomfort not as a point of shame to be apologized for but as a cultural assumption to be examined and answered.

The architectonic text for Keller's spiritual warfare preaching is Ephesians 6:10-20, which he preached through in two overlapping series: first the 1991-1992 series "Spiritual Warfare — The War" (six sermons on the nature of the enemy and the reality of the conflict), then the companion series "Spiritual Warfare — The Armor of God" (ten sermons on each piece of the armor). He returned to the passage again in 1998 in his Ephesians series "God's New Society," and once more in 2012 in his Ephesians series "Who is the Church?" These returns show the passage's permanent centrality in his preaching.

The governing conviction that shapes everything Keller teaches on this topic is what theologians call *Christus Victor*: the cross of Jesus Christ has already defeated the spiritual powers. Satan and the demonic realm are not co-equal adversaries of God in an ongoing metaphysical battle. They are defeated enemies fighting a rearguard action. The Christian who understands this fights from a position of accomplished victory, not toward an uncertain outcome. This is why Keller insists that "the whole armor of God" is not a spiritual technology to be mastered but the gospel itself applied to battle conditions.

The most counterintuitive and pastorally important argument Keller makes about spiritual warfare is his treatment of Satan's primary weapon: not temptation to gross sin but **accusation**. Unpacking the Greek word *diabolos* (slanderer/accuser), Keller argues that the devil's most effective strategy against the Christian is to haunt them with their own guilt — to speak the truth about their failures and then rob them of the gospel answer. The breastplate of righteousness and the shield of faith are not willpower and positive thinking; they are the imputed righteousness of Christ and the faith that claims it. The answer to accusation is never to claim innocence but to plead the blood.

Keller is equally careful about what spiritual warfare is not. He distinguishes firmly between demonic oppression and mental illness, insisting that confusing the two is pastorally dangerous in both directions. He resists the tendency, common in some charismatic circles, to attribute every struggle to direct demonic attack — and equally resists the secular reduction that explains every spiritual struggle in purely psychological or neurological categories. The 1991 sermon "Spiritual Warfare (Part 1)" spends considerable time on this epistemological caution.

The cultural apologetic running through the entire series is this: Keller acknowledges that in the Western world, the idea of spiritual entities and supernatural evil is foreign and embarrassing in a way it is not in Africa, Asia, or Latin America. He then argues that this embarrassment is itself a culturally contingent prejudice, not a mark of intellectual sophistication. C.S. Lewis's *The Screwtape Letters* and the writings of Walter Wink appear alongside Scripture as resources for taking spiritual powers seriously without either naïveté or superstition.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 12 sources]

### The Reality of the Powers

Keller's first task in every spiritual warfare sermon is establishing that the powers are real. He does this not by demanding credulity but by showing that the alternative — a purely naturalistic account of evil — fails to explain the data. The 2012 sermon (Ephesians 6:10-13, "A Study of Ephesians") frames the issue this way: in most of the world, the existence of spiritual good and spiritual evil is not a strange idea. It is the Western post-Enlightenment framework that is the anomaly, and its anomalous status does not confer epistemic authority.

Paul's framing in Ephesians 6 is crucial for Keller: our struggle is "not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the powers of this dark world and against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly realms" (Eph 6:12). Keller emphasizes the word "not" — our struggle is *not primarily* horizontal, not primarily a conflict between individuals or institutions or cultures. It has a vertical dimension that secular analysis by definition cannot see.

The complexity of evil — its overdetermination, its tendency to exceed any purely human explanation — is for Keller a clue that something more than social forces and bad choices is at work. In "The Defeat of Evil" (Mark 5:1-20, 2006), preaching on the Gerasene demoniac, he identifies three features of evil that the passage illuminates: (1) the *complexity* of evil — the man is simultaneously a victim and a danger, both suffering and causing suffering; (2) the *pattern* of evil — how it takes over a life step by step, marginalizing and isolating the person until they are living among tombs; and (3) the *defeat* of evil — Jesus' authority over the Legion is absolute and immediate.

### The Nature of the Enemy

Keller teaches that Satan operates through two primary mechanisms, corresponding to two different kinds of vulnerability in the human soul.

**Temptation** is addressed most directly in the 1992 sermon "Temptation" (Genesis 3:1-15). The serpent's approach in Eden follows a pattern: question the goodness of God ("Did God really say?"), reframe God's command as arbitrary restriction, and offer a false promise of autonomy and transcendence ("you will be like God"). Keller notes that the temptation is not to something openly wicked — it is to something that sounds like wisdom, growth, and self-determination. The enemy does not typically approach with a sign that reads "sin." He approaches with a philosophy, a mood, a set of questions that make obedience seem naive and rebellion seem mature.

**Accusation** is the second and, in Keller's pastoral experience, the more pervasive strategy. The word *diabolos* means "accuser" or "slanderer," and this is the strategy the enemy uses against the person who has already fallen. In "The Accuser" (Ephesians 6:10-13, December 1991), Keller develops this at length. Satan is not primarily a tempter once a Christian has sinned; he becomes a prosecuting attorney. He brings the sin to the bar of the conscience with relentless force and says: "Look what you've done. You call yourself a Christian. You are unworthy of God's love. This proves you were never really saved." The answer to accusation is never innocence. It is the breastplate of righteousness — not the believer's own righteousness but the imputed righteousness of Christ. "I have no righteousness of my own. Here is his."

The 1 Peter 5 sermon "The Flesh and the Devil" (May 1994) adds another dimension: the enemy is also a *lion* (1 Pet 5:8), prowling for the person who is already isolated by pride or shame. Pride and shame are two sides of the same coin — both prevent the person from receiving help, from staying in fellowship, from remaining in the kind of community where they can be known and supported. This is why Peter's exhortation to "humble yourselves" and to "resist the devil" appear in the same breath: humility is the posture that defeats the lion's primary hunting strategy.

### The Armor Is the Gospel

Keller's most theologically distinctive contribution to spiritual warfare teaching is his insistence that the armor of God in Ephesians 6 is not a separate spiritual technology but the gospel applied to combat. Each piece of armor is a gospel reality appropriated by faith:

- **The Belt of Truth** (Eph 6:14) — The 1992 sermon "The Belt of Truth Part 1" argues that this is not primarily moral integrity (doing what you say) but *doctrinal* truth: the facts of who God is, who you are in Christ, and how the world really works. The belt holds everything together. Without a clear grasp of gospel truth, the rest of the armor is unsecured.

- **The Breastplate of Righteousness** (Eph 6:14) — In both "Breastplate" sermons (February 1992), Keller develops what has become one of his most characteristic arguments: every human being needs *some* righteousness to live by — some answer to the question "Why am I presentable?" The secular answer is performance-righteousness (I'm good enough). The religious answer is moral-righteousness (I keep the rules). Both are forms of self-righteousness, and both are vulnerable to accusation because they depend on a record that is never clean enough. The gospel answer is the alien righteousness of Christ — a righteousness received, not achieved, and therefore not subject to attack on the basis of the believer's record.

- **The Sandals of Peace** (Eph 6:15) — The 1992 "Sandals of Peace" sermons argue that the gospel of peace gives the Christian stability of footing: peace with God (justification) translates into the psychological stability to stand firm under assault without either capitulating in fear or fighting back in panic. A soldier without secure footing cannot fight. A Christian without peace with God — without the assurance of the Father's acceptance — cannot hold the line when the enemy presses.

- **The Shield of Faith** (Eph 6:16) — The "Shield of Faith" sermons (March 1992) argue that "fiery arrows" do not come randomly — they come when you commit to storming an enemy stronghold, when you take a costly stand, when you move to the front line. The shield is not passive endurance; it is the active act of faith that says "God's promise is more real than this attack." Faith is not a psychological act of positive thinking; it is a relational act of trust in a specific person's specific promise.

- **The Helmet of Salvation** (Eph 6:17) — The 1992 "Helmet of Salvation" sermon develops the three tenses of salvation (past justification, present sanctification, future glorification) and argues that the helmet — protecting the mind — requires knowing all three. The Christian who only has the past tense ("I've been saved") is vulnerable to the enemy's attacks on present experience. The Christian who grasps the future tense ("I *will* be saved, fully and finally") has a confident hope that enables endurance.

- **The Sword of the Spirit** (Eph 6:17) — The Word of God is the only offensive weapon in the list, and Keller argues that Jesus' use of Scripture in the wilderness temptation is the model: not abstract theological propositions but specific, pointed truths applied to specific attacks.

### Christus Victor: The Decisive Battle Has Been Won

The deepest theological note in Keller's spiritual warfare preaching is the claim from "The Defeat of Evil" (Mark 5, 2006): Jesus' confrontation with the demonic in the Gospels is not a skirmish but a sign. When Jesus drives out the Legion and they beg not to be destroyed but only to enter the pigs, this is the posture of a conquered enemy negotiating terms, not a co-equal power in a balanced contest. The cross is where this conquest is accomplished definitively: Colossians 2:15 says Jesus "disarmed the powers and authorities, made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them by the cross."

Keller uses this to guard against two errors simultaneously: the error of treating spiritual warfare as an ongoing metaphysical battle between equal forces (dualism), and the error of dismissing it as a primitive superstition that enlightened Christianity has outgrown. The powers are real and active. They are also defeated. This is the tension Paul holds in Ephesians 6: "Put on the whole armor of God" (take the battle seriously) — *because* Christ has already won (the battle's outcome is settled).

The Luke 11 sermon "Power to Change" (February 1993) extends this to the question of personal transformation. Jesus' parable of the strong man and the stronger man (Luke 11:14-26) makes a striking argument: it is possible to have evil driven out and not have it replaced with something better, in which case the end is worse than the beginning. The demons return to the "swept and garnished" house. What drives them out permanently is not moral effort or spiritual technique but the indwelling presence of Christ himself — the stronger man who evicts the strong man and takes up residence. This is why Keller insists that spiritual warfare is not fundamentally about spiritual disciplines or rebuking demons; it is about the gospel: bringing the person who has been set free into living, daily relationship with the one who freed them.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 7 texts]

### Ephesians 6:10-20 — The Armor of God

The commanding text. Paul's exhortation to "put on the whole armor of God" (Eph 6:11) is the framework for fifteen of Keller's seventeen sermons on this theme. Keller's governing interpretive key is that each piece of armor is a gospel reality — not a spiritual discipline or technique, but a dimension of what Christ has accomplished and the Christian is called to appropriate. The crucial interpretive note: verse 13 says "so that you may be able to *stand*" — not advance, not conquer, but stand. The Christian's calling in spiritual warfare is primarily defensive, because the offensive victory has already been accomplished by Christ. The Christian stands on won ground.

Keller returns to this passage across four different series spanning twenty-one years (1991, 1992, 1998, 2012), showing its permanent centrality.

### Mark 5:1-20 — The Gerasene Demoniac

The most vivid and extended exorcism account in the Gospels. Keller uses it in "The Defeat of Evil" (2006) to establish three things: (1) the complexity of evil — the man is simultaneously victim and perpetrator, suffering and dangerous; (2) the pattern of evil — isolation, loss of self, living among the dead; (3) the defeat of evil — the demons cannot withstand Jesus' presence and immediately recognize his authority. The detail that the demons beg Jesus not to destroy them but merely to enter the pigs is, for Keller, a window into the posture of the defeated: negotiating terms, not contesting the outcome.

### Genesis 3:1-15 — The Fall and the Serpent's Strategy

The foundational text for understanding temptation. Keller's 1992 sermon "Temptation" reads the serpent's approach to Eve as a template for how the enemy operates: (1) question God's command ("Did God really say?"); (2) deny God's judgment ("You will not certainly die"); (3) impugn God's motive ("God knows that when you eat... you will be like God"). The temptation is not to something visibly wicked but to something that presents itself as wisdom, autonomy, and growth. The pattern recurs in every temptation.

### 1 Peter 5:5-11 — Humility, Anxiety, and the Prowling Lion

The text behind "The Flesh and the Devil" (1994). Peter's sequence is theologically dense: humble yourself under God's hand → cast your anxiety on him → be sober and alert → resist the devil. Keller draws out the logic: pride and anxiety are the open doors through which the lion enters. The person who cannot humble themselves — who is too proud to admit need, too shame-driven to ask for help — is the person who ends up isolated, which is exactly where the prowling lion wants them.

### Luke 11:14-26 — The Strong Man and the Stronger Man

The text behind "Power to Change" (1993). Jesus' argument that a house swept clean but left empty is worse off than before (the seven worse spirits) is Keller's most direct treatment of why spiritual warfare is not ultimately about expelling evil but about welcoming the one who can permanently occupy the space. The strong man (Satan) has been bound by the stronger man (Christ), but the implication is that freedom from evil is only the beginning; the permanent solution is the presence of the one who freed you.

### Ephesians 6:12 — "Not Against Flesh and Blood"

Keller repeatedly singles out this verse as the diagnostic lens for understanding the true nature of human conflict. The failure to see the spiritual dimension of struggles — in personal life, in congregational conflict, in cultural battles — leads to fighting the wrong enemy by the wrong means. The horizontal conflicts that exhaust churches, marriages, and cities often have a vertical dimension that explains why they are so intractable.

### Colossians 2:13-15 — The Triumphal Procession

Keller cites this passage in conjunction with Ephesians 6 to establish the *Christus Victor* framework: at the cross, Christ "disarmed the powers and authorities, made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them by the cross." The present tense of spiritual warfare takes place under the sign of this past-tense victory. The Christian does not fight to win; the Christian fights from the position of one who has already won in Christ.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 8 sources]

### The Western World's Unique Embarrassment

Keller's 2012 sermon opens with an observation that sets the entire cultural frame: "In Africa, Latin America, Asia — most places in the world — the idea of spiritual warfare, of a conflict between spiritual good and spiritual evil, is not an unusual concept. Many people in many parts of the world think this helps make sense of reality. But here in the Western world, we find it a foreign concept." He then refuses to let this embarrassment go unchallenged. The assumption that Western skepticism about spiritual entities is intellectually superior is itself a culturally parochial assumption — the fruit of a particular post-Enlightenment philosophical tradition, not a timeless discovery.

This move is characteristic Keller: he does not argue from the assumption that the secular frame is neutral ground that the Christian must work from. He argues that both frames are faith commitments, and the secular frame's confident dismissal of spiritual realities is itself an act of faith, not of evidence.

### C.S. Lewis and the Two Errors

Keller frequently invokes C.S. Lewis's famous preface to *The Screwtape Letters*: "There are two equal and opposite errors into which our race can fall about the devils. One is to disbelieve in their existence. The other is to believe, and to feel an excessive and unhealthy interest in them. They themselves are equally pleased by both errors." This framing gives Keller's treatment its characteristic balance. He is arguing simultaneously against the secular dismissal and the charismatic sensationalism that he believes are both strategic victories for the enemy.

### The Skeptic's Problem with Radical Evil

One of Keller's consistent cultural arguments is that purely naturalistic accounts of evil cannot adequately explain the most extreme forms of human wickedness. The Holocaust, genocide, the abuse of children — these events have a quality that presses even secular thinkers beyond purely social-structural explanations. Hannah Arendt's phrase "the banality of evil" is itself a confession that evil has a momentum and a pattern that exceeds individual moral failure. Keller does not press this into proof of demonic forces, but he uses it to argue that the secular frame is insufficient — that the biblical category of principalities and powers illuminates something the purely psychological and sociological account does not.

### Manhattan, Identity, and the Subtle Battlefield

In the Armor of God sermons, Keller regularly grounds the teaching in New York City life. The breastplate of righteousness sermon speaks directly to the Manhattan professional's vulnerability: the person whose entire identity is organized around professional achievement is structurally exposed to a particular kind of accusation — from the enemy, but also from within — whenever they fail, are passed over, or are criticized. The secular solution is to build a more impressive performance record. The spiritual warfare insight is that this is exactly the wrong kind of armor: it can be pierced, because the record is never spotless. The only impenetrable breastplate is the righteousness of another.

The sandals of peace sermon addresses the Manhattan anxiety epidemic: what looks like a psychology of fear and overwork is also, in Keller's analysis, a symptom of spiritual displacement — the person without peace with God is structurally unstable, perpetually seeking footing in performance, relationship, or status. The spiritual stability that comes from justification — from knowing that one's standing before God is settled in Christ — produces a kind of peace that anxiety-management techniques cannot replicate.

### The Shield on the Front Line

The Shield of Faith sermon makes a cultural observation that has direct application to New York: "Fiery arrows do not come all throughout the battle. When do they come? Right when you've decided to storm the stronghold of the enemy." Keller applies this to the person who has made a costly commitment — to a neighborhood, to an act of justice, to a risky act of charity, to stepping out of a lucrative career to pursue a calling. The frontal attacks of doubt, discouragement, and attack come precisely at the moment of commitment, not in the comfortable middle. The person who has never put anything at risk for the kingdom has also never experienced the full force of the fiery arrows.

### Power to Change and the Limits of Self-Help

The "Power to Change" sermon (Luke 11, 1993) addresses one of the most resonant cultural questions for the Manhattan professional: why do self-improvement programs, therapy, and behavioral change often fail to produce lasting transformation? Jesus' strange parable of the seven worse spirits returning to the swept house is Keller's answer: you can achieve behavioral modification, moral reform, even significant psychological healing — and still be worse off than when you started, because the space has been swept but not occupied. The only change that sticks is the change produced by the indwelling presence of Christ. The sermon addresses multiple competing approaches to change — "white-knuckle it," "understand your past," "find a community," "change your environment" — and shows how each can be temporarily effective while failing at the root level.

### Walter Wink and the Powers in Social Structures

In the more theologically developed sermons, Keller references New Testament scholar Walter Wink's work on "the powers" — the biblical principalities and authorities — as not purely personal spiritual beings but as embedded in social structures, institutions, and cultural systems. This allows Keller to speak to the secular listener who is uncomfortable with the personification of evil: even if you are not prepared to believe in a personal devil, you can observe that institutions, nations, and cultural systems behave in ways that exceed the wickedness of any individual member. The church, the corporation, the nation — all can be in bondage to spiritual forces in ways that individual moral reform cannot address.

### Dostoevsky and the Complexity of Evil

In "The Defeat of Evil," Keller draws on Dostoevsky's *The Brothers Karamazov* — particularly Ivan Karamazov's argument that evil is too complex, too embedded in the suffering of the innocent, to be explained by simple moral categories. Ivan cannot believe in a God who permits children to suffer. Keller uses this not to answer Ivan's challenge by force but to note that Dostoevsky himself — through the character of Alyosha and the figure of Father Zosima — suggests that the answer to evil is not a philosophical solution but a presence: someone who enters into the midst of the suffering and bears it from the inside. The Gerasene demoniac episode is exactly this: Jesus crossing the lake to find one suffering man in a cemetery, driving out the Legion, and restoring him to his family and community.

- [Salvation and Justification](salvation-and-justification.md) — The breastplate of righteousness is imputed, forensic righteousness; the spiritual warfare teaching makes the doctrine of justification pastorally urgent
- [Death and Resurrection](death-and-resurrection.md) — The *Christus Victor* framework grounds spiritual warfare in the resurrection; the powers are defeated because death itself has been defeated
