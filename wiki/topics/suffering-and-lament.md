# Suffering and Lament

## Summary [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Tim Keller returns to the problem of suffering more consistently than almost any other subject in his preaching. Across more than three decades of sermons — spanning his early ministry in Hopewell, Virginia through decades at Redeemer Presbyterian Church in New York City — he refuses every easy answer while insisting that Christianity, rightly understood, gives resources that no other worldview can match.

His fundamental claim is that suffering is not an anomaly that disproves God's goodness, but a reality that God has entered into personally. The gospel answer to suffering is not first an explanation but a presence. Jesus Christ did not come to give lectures about pain from a safe distance; he descended into the groaning world, suffered socially (rejection, betrayal), physically (torture, death), and spiritually (forsakenness — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"). This means, Keller argues, that Christianity is the only major religion with a God who can say, "I know what you mean," when a sufferer comes to him.

Several interlocking themes run through Keller's sermons on this topic:

**Suffering is universal and inevitable.** He repeatedly pushes back against what he calls the secular assumption — that this life is all there is, and therefore any suffering is an unacceptable theft of everything. Every human being will lose everything they love. Job's situation is not exotic; it is simply what aging, death, and loss do to everyone. The question is not whether suffering will come, but what inner resources you will have when it does.

**Suffering is not meaningless punishment.** Keller distinguishes sharply between the fatalistic view (God is the author of evil; resign yourself), the humanistic view (God has nothing to do with evil; panic), and the moralistic view (God punishes bad people; if you suffer, you must be sinning). Against all three, the book of Job insists: God is not the author of evil, God is absolutely in control of evil, and suffering does not come to people on the basis of their moral merit. Keller calls these the only three "pat answers" — and insists the biblical view is the only one honest enough to hold the tension without collapsing it.

**Suffering is purposeful formation.** James, Paul, and Peter all agree: trials are a furnace. The fire does not destroy what is real; it destroys what is dross. The suffering shows you what you truly trust in, burns away the false anchors, and — if you stand fast in obedience — turns you into what Keller calls "pure gold." Humility, freedom, compassion, and faith cannot be developed, he argues, by any other means.

**Lament is biblical and holy.** Keller is insistent that there is a kind of stoicism masquerading as Christian faith — the tight-lipped "I'm trusting the Lord" while refusing to weep. But Job ripped his robe, shaved his head, and fell on the ground, and the text says he "did not sin." Jesus wept at Lazarus's tomb and "snorted with anger" at death. In Gethsemane, he was "overwhelmed to the point of death." Psalm 88 ends in darkness with no resolution. The lament psalms give the church permission to cry out honestly, even accusingly, before God — because the God of the Bible can handle it.

**The cross is the center.** Ultimately, Keller's answer to the problem of suffering is not philosophical but personal and historical: look at the cross. When you see Jesus Christ cry out from the cross, "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — you see the ultimate "why me." And because he was forsaken in his death-groan, you never will be. God charged himself with sin so that when we put God in the dock, he can forgive us. The resurrection does not explain suffering; it swallows it up.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

### The Diagnosis

Every culture has a way of processing suffering. Keller identifies three inadequate responses — all of which he calls "pat answers" — before offering the biblical alternative:

1. **Fatalism (the stoic response):** God is the author of evil; evil is inevitable; resign yourself to it. This fails because, as Job shows, God is *not* the author of evil.

2. **Humanism (the panic response):** God has nothing to do with evil — either because he doesn't exist or because he can't stop it (cf. Rabbi Kushner's *When Bad Things Happen to Good People*). This fails because it places God outside his own creation and leaves suffering completely random and meaningless. Suicide becomes rational. Life becomes a "stupid fraud" (Tolstoy).

3. **Moralism (the groveling response):** God punishes bad people and blesses good ones. If you're suffering, you must be sinning. This fails spectacularly in the face of Job — a blameless man whom God describes as the finest person on earth, who suffers most precisely *because* he is the best.

The biblical view, Keller argues, holds together what every other system tears apart:
- God is not the author of evil (it originates in the ill will of Satan and the fall of humanity)
- God is absolutely in control of evil (he says "this far, but no further")
- Evil does not fall on people according to their goodness or badness

This requires holding the mystery without resolving it. As C.S. Lewis put it, the ancients approached God as the accused approaches a judge. In modernity, the roles are reversed: God is in the dock. Job's radical move — and the Bible's radical move — is to let God be God, to let him be the questioner rather than the one required to give answers.

### The Gospel Answer

The gospel does not dissolve the mystery of suffering; it provides something better: a Savior who has entered into it.

Keller draws on Hebrews 2 for a three-part portrait of Jesus:

- **The King who gets involved.** Like the 38 bystanders who watched Kitty Genovese murdered in Kew Gardens (1964) and did nothing, the gods of every other religion look down but do not come down. Jesus comes down. He makes himself not merely at risk, but at cost.

- **The Captain who faces death for us.** Jesus is the *archegos* — the champion in representative combat. He sees us running from the enemy of death and puts himself between us and the foe. He entered death and "blew a hole through the back of it" (C.S. Lewis). The resurrection is not a general assurance about afterlife; it is the specific fact that someone has returned from the undiscovered country and bids us follow.

- **The Brother who is proud of us.** In the genealogy of Matthew 1, Jesus claims Tamar (incest survivor), Rahab (prostitute), Bathsheba (adulteress), Mary (unwed mother) as his family. "Not ashamed to call them brothers." No matter what anyone has said about you, no matter what you have done, his verdict is what counts.

The cross is where these threads converge. On the cross, Jesus — quoting Psalm 22 — cries out "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" That is the ultimate "why me." He was forsaken in his death-groan so that when you groan, the Father hears it the way a parent hears the cry of a child in pain. The cry of the cross is the question that answers all our questions — not by giving information, but by showing that God has taken the suffering with infinite seriousness.

And the resurrection promises that everything sad will come untrue. Not just a consolation for the life we've lost — resurrection is the *undoing* of everything that is wrong. Bodies redeemed, creation liberated, the whole groaning creation set free (Romans 8). As Keller quotes Ivan Karamazov (Dostoevsky), something will come to pass at the world's finale "so precious that it will suffice our hearts for the comforting of all resentments, for the atonement of all crimes of humanity."

### Especially Significant Sermons

**"A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break" (Jan 2010, Isa 42:1-9)** — Keller's most sustained treatment of Jesus's tenderness toward the suffering. Drawing on the servant songs of Isaiah, he shows that the Hebrew word translated "bruised" (*daka*) means a deep internal contusion, a death blow that doesn't show on the surface — "inside you're dying." The servant is attracted to hopeless cases: "A smoldering wick he will not snuff out." Keller draws on Richard Sibbes's classic Puritan work *The Bruised Reed* and Jonathan Edwards's "The Excellency of Jesus Christ." The sermon's central comfort: "He was bruised so he can deal with your bruises."

**"A Rich Man's Poverty" (Nov 1997, Job 1:8-22)** — The most philosophically thorough demolition of all alternative frameworks for suffering. Keller shows how the Job narrative uniquely refuses every pat answer. The only reason God doesn't condemn Job for putting him in the dock is that God charged himself with sin — "He charged himself so he wouldn't have to charge us."

**"Heman's Cry of Darkness" (Nov 2007, Ps 88)** — A close engagement with the only psalm in the psalter that ends without hope, in pure darkness, with no resolution. Keller's key argument: darkness can last a long time even for faithful people, and the very existence of this psalm in Scripture is God's permission to pray in desolation without pretending.

**"Groaning in the Spirit" (Dec 2006, Rom 8:13-27)** — A Christmas sermon on suffering. The whole creation groans. We groan. But Keller's stunning observation: the Spirit of God groans too (v. 26). How can an omnipotent God groan? Christmas. He entered the groaning world. He became subject to hunger, rejection, alienation, nature, death. The Spirit's intercession means God gives you "what you would have asked for if you knew everything he knows."

**"A Society of Suffering" (Nov 2009, Jas 1:1-18)** — Addresses the particular vulnerability of secular modern culture to suffering. "There has never been a culture that set its people up to be so vulnerable to the vicissitudes of life." The sermon's most striking image: the double-mindedness that Paul describes (*dipsychos*) as the fundamental obstacle to persevering through suffering.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

Keller returns to certain texts again and again as the load-bearing pillars of his theology of suffering:

### Job 1–42
Job is Keller's primary proving ground for the "why" question. He preaches it in at least three distinct full-sermon treatments (1997, 2001, 2008) plus numerous references. Key moves:
- Satan, God, and Job all agree: unless you serve God for nothing (for himself, not for benefits), you are not a servant — you are a customer.
- God overrules evil ("this far, no further") and overcomes evil (always working to thwart Satan's deepest desires), but is not the author of evil.
- God never gives Job answers; he gives Job questions. And those questions crush the self-righteousness that makes us either hate God or hate ourselves.
- The ultimate answer is Jesus's cry on the cross, echoing Job's "why me?" — but from the only truly innocent sufferer.

### Psalm 88
The "darkest psalm" — no light, no hope, darkness as the final word. Keller preaches this as permission for sustained desolation. Four lessons he draws: (1) spiritual darkness can last a long time regardless of your faithfulness; (2) darkness is often where God's grace most deeply forms us; (3) darkness can produce greatness of character; (4) darkness is not ultimate — it can be relativized by the resurrection.

### Isaiah 42:1-9 (and the Servant Songs broadly)
The bruised reed and smoldering wick passage. The Hebrew word for "bruised" (*daka*) appears in both verse 3 (the servant will not crush the bruised reed) and verse 4 (the servant himself will be bruised/*daka* but will not give up). The movement is transitive to intransitive: Jesus experienced the very same crushing so that he can heal ours. Keller traces this back to Genesis 3:15 — the serpent bruises the heel of the seed of the woman; that same death-blow-language anticipates Isaiah 53: "bruised for our iniquities."

### Romans 8:13-27
The groaning passage. Three forms of groaning: creation (under bondage to decay), believers (awaiting the redemption of their bodies), and the Spirit himself. Keller's three resources from this text: **prayer** (Abba prayer — God hears like a parent hears a child's cry of pain; and Spirit-intercession — God gives you what you would have asked if you knew everything he knows), **pattern** (sharing in Christ's sufferings so that weakness becomes strength, death becomes resurrection), and **perspective** (the eternal weight of glory so outweighs present suffering that the man working for $150 million and the man working for $15,000 in the same conditions experience those conditions completely differently).

### 2 Corinthians 4:7-18 and 12:7-10
Paul's "jars of clay" passage and the "thorn in the flesh." Keller uses these to show: (1) the inevitability of suffering, (2) the pattern of suffering (outwardly wasting away, inwardly being renewed), and (3) the future of suffering (an eternal weight of glory that outweighs all present affliction). On the thorn: Paul's arrogance would have undone his influence; the thorn was mercy disguised as cruelty — "strength is made perfect in weakness."

### James 1:1-18
Trials as furnace. "Consider it pure joy" — not masochism (trials are good!) nor hedonism (no joy until trials end), but a deliberate reframing: *consider* what the furnace can produce. Perseverance (*hypomonē* — hyper-standing, standing fast) is the key virtue. Keller traces this word through to Hebrews 12:2 — Jesus "endured the cross" using the same word. The reason you can hyper-stand is because he hyper-stood for you.

### Hebrews 2:5-18 and 12:1-13
Jesus as *archegos* — champion, captain — who in representative combat places himself between us and the enemy death. God made "the author of salvation perfect *through* suffering" (v. 10). Jesus's glory was not *despite* his suffering but *because* of it. Therefore our suffering is not a sign that God has abandoned us; it is us walking the same road the Captain walked.

### 1 Peter 1:6-9 and 4:12-19
The "fiery ordeal" (*pyrōsis*) — the refiner's fire. The point is not that the ore is bad; the point is that in normal atmospheric conditions, the pure metal and the dross can live together. Only in the furnace do they separate. That is what a trial does: it separates your true allegiances from your functional ones. The silversmith knows the silver is pure when he can see his own face reflected in it.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 8+ sources]

### The Secular Assumption and NYC Context

Keller's setting in Manhattan makes his diagnosis of secular culture particularly pointed. He argues that New York — and the West broadly — is probably "the worst society in history for helping its members get ready for suffering." The Latin word *saeculum* means "now": a secular society says this life is all there is, which means any suffering is a catastrophic theft of *everything*. Every other culture has said, "This life is a good place to try things out, but it's not all there is." Only modernity has told people that loss here is total loss.

This means, Keller observes, that modern culture produces what he calls "crybabies" — not because modern people are weak, but because their worldview gives them nothing to stand on when suffering hits. People sue at the drop of a hat because suffering is treated as a violation of what life owes them. They consider suicide when life becomes painful because there's no category for delayed gratification or future glory.

Post-9/11 New York is the implicit backdrop for many of these sermons. Keller planted Redeemer in 1989 and watched the city absorb enormous trauma in September 2001. His theology of suffering was not abstract; he was pastoring people whose colleagues died in the towers, whose city was remade by grief. The question of innocent suffering — children, firefighters, office workers — was not theoretical.

### Career Anxiety and Identity

A recurring NYC cultural hook: the career reversal as existential crisis. In "A Society of Suffering," Keller describes the person whose anchor is professional status — "I believe in God, but my career is what I'm really living for." When that career collapses, the person collapses, because their functional god has died. His pastoral observation: "Whenever life becomes meaningless, it's because something besides God that you put your functional trust in has died."

This connects to his treatment of the Kitty Genovese murder in "Brother, Captain, King" (1964, Kew Gardens, Queens): 38 neighbors watched from their windows and did not come down. They saw, heard, but didn't get involved. The gods of every other religion are like those 38 — they observe from a height but don't descend into the mess. Jesus is the one who comes down.

### Literature and Philosophy

Keller saturates his suffering sermons with literary and philosophical reference:

**Tolstoy** (*A Confession*): "Is there any meaning in life that my inevitable death does not destroy?" — Keller quotes Tolstoy's existential crisis at 50 as a window into the modern person's terror of death and the inability to suppress it indefinitely.

**Dostoevsky** (*The Brothers Karamazov*): Both Ivan Karamazov (in "Benefits of Facing Trouble") and Father Zosima hover in the background of Keller's theology of suffering. Ivan's famous rebellion against God because of children's suffering — "the ticket" — is taken seriously as the strongest secular objection. But Keller also quotes Ivan's own concession that something will come to pass at the world's finale so precious it will "suffice our hearts for the comforting of all resentments." The book answers itself.

**C.S. Lewis** (*A Grief Observed*, *The Weight of Glory*, *Mere Christianity*): Keller quotes Lewis frequently. "God is in the dock" — from *God in the Dock* — is a touchstone for the modern attitude toward suffering: we are the judge, God must explain himself. Lewis's essay "The Weight of Glory" appears in "Groaning in the Spirit" for its vision of the resurrection glory: "We are on the outside of the world, the wrong side of the door." Also: "Our great captain has opened a cleft in the pitiless walls of the world and bids us come through" — on the resurrection as breaking death open.

**J.R.R. Tolkien**: The story of Húrin the Steadfast from the *Silmarillion* — a warrior who stays in a mountain gap while his companions escape, swinging his axe 40 times saying "day will come again" — is Keller's favorite illustration of *hypomonē* (steadfast endurance). He uses it in both "Benefits of Facing Trouble" and "A Society of Suffering."

**Viktor Frankl** (*Man's Search for Meaning*): Quoted in "A Rich Man's Poverty." In the death camps, the people who survived were those who stopped asking "what is the meaning of life?" and began to realize that life was asking *them*, "what is the meaning of *you*?" Keller uses this to illuminate Job's turn: God doesn't give answers; he gives questions.

**Richard Sibbes** (*The Bruised Reed*, 1630): The 17th-century Puritan's classic is cited extensively in "A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break." Keller quotes Sibbes: "If Christ be so merciful as not to break me, I will not break myself by despair."

**Jonathan Edwards** ("The Excellency of Jesus Christ"): Used in "A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break" for the paradox of Jesus as both infinitely high and infinitely humble — "There is an admirable conjunction of diverse excellencies in Jesus Christ."

**Nicholas Wolterstorff** (*Lament for a Son*): The philosopher who lost his son in a mountain-climbing accident. Keller quotes him in "A Rich Man's Poverty": "I cannot fit it all together by saying he did it. But neither can I fit it all together by saying there was nothing God could do about it." Wolterstorff's refusal of every pat answer models the Job-like living-without-answers that Keller commends.

**Sigmund Freud**: Freud's death-wish and fear-of-death, cited in "Brother, Captain, King" — Freud believed we repress our terror of death, and yet it drives everything: the need for wealth, love, accomplishment. Our desperate insistence that we matter. Keller affirms this diagnosis while rejecting Freud's conclusion that death is simply the end.

**Hamlet**: "Death, the undiscovered country, from whose bourn no traveler returns, puzzles the will and makes us rather bear the ills that we have than fly to others we know not of. Thus conscience doth make cowards of us all." — Keller quotes this in "Brother, Captain, King" only to refute it. Hamlet was wrong. *Someone* has come back from death.

**Martin Luther King Jr.**: Referenced in "A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break" as someone who navigated the paradox of the servant king — wanting the results of the king (justice) without the methods of coercion (violence). King's strategy of civil disobedience plus nonviolence was a secular example of the servant pattern: change without raw power.

## Representative Quotes [coverage: high — 8+ sources]

### On God's tenderness toward the broken

> "A bruised reed he will not break. A smoldering wick he will not snuff out. What is this talking about? Jesus Christ, this servant, is attracted to hopeless cases. He loves the fragile. He loves people who are beaten and battered and bruised — and maybe don't show it on the outside, but inside they're dying."
> — *A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break* (Jan 2010)

> "If Christ be so merciful as not to break me, I will not break myself by despair."
> — Richard Sibbes, quoted in *A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break* (Jan 2010)

### On the uniqueness of Christianity's answer to suffering

> "Every other religion says: go to God. All the other gods, as depicted in other religions — they don't know. They don't know what it's like to be hurt. They don't know what it's like to be lonely. They don't know what it's like to be facing death. Christianity is the only religion in which you've got a God that suffers. And when you go to him and say, 'I'm suffering,' here's the only God in any religion that can say, 'I know what you mean.'"
> — *How to Handle Suffering* (May 1994)

### On God in the dock

> "The ancients approached God, or the gods, as the accused approaches a judge. For the modern person, the roles are reversed. We are the judge. God is in the dock... The meaning of Job is that God is not the ultimate answerer. He's the ultimate questioner. He is not the one who comes and gives you the big answers. He is the one who stands and asks you the questions."
> — *A Rich Man's Poverty* (Nov 1997), quoting C.S. Lewis

### On the cross as the ultimate answer to suffering

> "The answer to your questions is a question. But in his question, you receive the answer to all of yours. Because when you see Jesus Christ on the cross, saying 'My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?' — you know why. For you. That is the question that answers your questions."
> — *A Rich Man's Poverty* (Nov 1997)

> "God charged himself with sin. Jesus Christ came to be a condemned criminal. He charged himself so he wouldn't have to charge us. He put himself in the dock so that when we put him in the dock, he can forgive us."
> — *A Rich Man's Poverty* (Nov 1997)

### On lament and the permission to grieve

> "There is a kind of stoicism that masquerades as Christian faith. Job got up, ripped his clothes, tore his hair, fell on the ground, and began to cry and scream. And it says in verse 22: Job did not sin. Grief will never destroy you. Surprise will."
> — *A Rich Man's Poverty* (Nov 1997)

> "Jesus was always weeping. He was always crying. He was sweating blood in the Garden of Gethsemane. He didn't say, 'Well, I'm just rejoicing in everything the Lord is doing.' He was in the dust. He was weeping. He said, 'My soul is overwhelmed to the point of death.' He's not in denial. He's not repressing his emotions. That's not Christianity. But he's not surprised."
> — *How to Handle Suffering* (May 1994)

### On the furnace as mercy

> "Suffering can turbocharge the ordinary process of Christian growth. It can be a furnace that turns you to pure gold."
> — *A Society of Suffering* (Nov 2009)

> "There is no way to humility. There is no way to compassion. There is no way to freedom. There is no way to this kind of faith. There is no way to be complete — without suffering."
> — *Benefits of Facing Trouble* (Oct 1995)

> "An ounce of sin will hurt you far more than ten million tons of suffering."
> — Charles Spurgeon, quoted in *How to Handle Suffering* (May 1994)

### On groaning and prayer in suffering

> "When you suffer, you can know that God responds to your groaning the way a parent responds to the cry of pain of his or her child. There is an intense love, an intense care."
> — *Groaning in the Spirit* (Dec 2006)

> "Wouldn't it be great if God was so gracious that every time you prayed, he would give you — and only give you — what you would have asked for if you knew every single thing that he knew and you saw everything that he could see? We do have a God like that."
> — *Groaning in the Spirit* (Dec 2006)

### On standing fast (hypomonē)

> "When your life is going dark, getting dark, things are getting bad — I want you to stand fast out of love. Out of love for the one who stood fast for me, I will stand fast for him. No matter how dark it gets: day will come again. Either the darkness will lift now, or eventually it's going to lift because Jesus is going to make the whole world right."
> — *A Society of Suffering* (Nov 2009)

> "Jesus Christ persevered for me. Jesus stood there for me. He took it all for me. If Jesus' love was so great that it endured that for you, you think your little sins are going to scare him away or wear him out?"
> — *Benefits of Facing Trouble* (Oct 1995)

### On the fear of death and what the resurrection does

> "You don't realize the degree to which you are enslaved to the fear of death until the faith in the resurrection of Jesus Christ comes into your life and begins to release you from it."
> — *Brother, Captain, King* (Feb 2005)

> "The whole creation is waiting for our liberation. We are going to be so radiant, so cleansed, so great... In other words, this isn't just getting heaven. Because heaven, as great as it would be, would just be a consolation for the life we've lost. But resurrection is the *restoration* of it. It's the undoing of everything that's wrong. It'll make everything sad come untrue."
> — *Groaning in the Spirit* (Dec 2006)

### On perspective: the weight of glory

> "How you experience your present is completely shaped by what you believe your ultimate future to be. Completely. If you rest the deepest hopes of your heart in anything but God — if it's a political cause, a relationship, a career — suffering will take you out. But if you believe what you're in for is God's future... then the tediousness of your present circumstances is being absolutely overshadowed by the glory that will be revealed."
> — *Groaning in the Spirit* (Dec 2006)

- [Faith and Doubt](faith-and-doubt.md) — The bondage to the fear of death (Heb 2) as the root of many dysfunctions that suffering exposes; honest doubt as compatible with faith
- [Sanctification](sanctification.md) — *Hypomonē* (steadfast endurance) as a key fruit of suffering rightly received; the wilderness as where you find the rock, and in the rock is honey
- [Apologetics](apologetics.md) — Keller's cultural diagnosis that secular modernity is uniquely ill-equipped for suffering; suffering as the great defeater belief that apologetics must address
- [Spiritual Warfare](spiritual-warfare.md) — The cosmic dimension of suffering: affliction as a site of spiritual conflict, not merely providential trial; Keller on standing firm (Eph 6) when suffering is prolonged
- [Identity](identity.md) — The "cosmic disappointment" of reaching for things that cannot satisfy; suffering exposes the emptiness Keller reads as evidence for the God-shaped void
- [Marriage and Family](marriage-and-family.md) — Marriage is a site of profound suffering — infertility (Hannah's lament), marital breakdown, loneliness within marriage; Keller's pastoral treatment of these griefs draws on his broader theology of suffering
- [Salvation and Justification](salvation-and-justification.md) — Justification enables endurance of suffering: the foundation is not circumstances but the unchanging status of being accepted in Christ
- [Sin](sin.md) — Sin's cosmic effects — the alienation from God, self, neighbor, and nature — produce all human suffering; suffering is read against the backdrop of a fallen creation, not as arbitrary
- [The Church](the-church.md) — The church is the community of suffering alongside one another; Paul's imprisonment in Ephesians 3 is addressed pastorally to discouraged people, and the church is where that pastoral care is embodied
- [Work and Vocation](work-and-vocation.md) — Genesis 3 thorns and thistles explain why even the best work is frustrating; occupational suffering is connected to the cross and the new creation
