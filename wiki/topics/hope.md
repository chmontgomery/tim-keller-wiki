# Hope

## Summary [coverage: high — 25 sources]

For Tim Keller, Christian hope is categorically different from optimism, wishful thinking, or the secular belief that things will improve. Hope, in his preaching, is a confident expectation grounded not in circumstances but in the promises of God anchored in the resurrection of Jesus Christ. It is not an emotion to be cultivated but a conclusion to be reasoned out — what Hebrews calls "the certainty of things not seen."

Preaching from Romans 8, Hebrews 11, Revelation 21, 1 Corinthians 15, Ephesians 1, and 1 Peter 1, Keller argues that biblical hope rests on three pillars: our bad things turn out for good, our good things can never be lost, and the best things are yet to come. These are not inspirational platitudes — they are logical deductions from the resurrection. When you truly reckon them, they produce an impervious, relentless joy that suffering cannot extinguish. Jonathan Edwards first articulated this triad in his early sermon on Christian happiness; Keller returns to it repeatedly across four decades of preaching.

Keller insists that Christian hope is entirely physical and material in its ultimate orientation. The new creation is not escape from the world but the restoration and glorification of this world. The resurrection body of Jesus — still bearing his wounds, able to eat fish, walking through locked doors — is the template for what awaits. Matter matters. The body matters. The world matters. Heaven, rightly understood, is not an alternative to this life but its rehabilitation — the glory of God falling on a broken creation and renewing it. The city of God comes down to the earth; the redeemed do not float upward out of it.

Hope shapes everything: how we handle money (1 Timothy 6), how we relate across racial and cultural lines (Romans 14–15), how we build Christian community (Hebrews 10), how we face exile and homelessness (Isaiah 35), and how we worship (Psalm 96). We are, Keller says, "irreducibly hope-based creatures." What we believe about the ultimate future completely determines how we live now.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 20 sources]

Keller's central argument about hope begins with a diagnosis of the human condition: we are beings made for eternity, living in a world subject to decay. "Nature is subject to futility," Paul says in Romans 8 — everything falls apart. We fight this with diets, cosmetics, new experiences, New Year's resolutions. But we cannot outrun entropy. The world we inhabit is described by J.R.R. Tolkien (a favorite Keller reference) as a picture of Pikes Peak compared to the real thing — the same thing, but so diminished.

The gospel answer is not escape but resurrection. Keller distinguishes sharply between *kainos* (the Greek for qualitative newness — brightness, vividness, glory) and *neos* (mere chronological youth). In the new creation, God promises *kainos* without *neos*: things that get newer and more glorious forever, without decay. "Behold, I am making all things new" (Revelation 21:5) is present progressive — it is already beginning.

This hope is not wishful. The resurrection of Jesus is its historical anchor. If Christ is raised, then "we're going to get those eyes back. We're going to get those hands back. We're going to get love back. We're going to get the world back, and far better than it ever was before." The Maccabean mother, watching her sons tortured to death, said to each one: "from heaven I will get them back." That is not sentimentality — it is a conclusion from premises.

His three-part structure from Romans 8:28-30 (formulated by Jonathan Edwards, recovered by Keller):
1. **Bad things turn out for good** (v.28) — not that bad things are really good, but that God works them toward his purposes. Jesus wept at Lazarus's tomb — he hates death. But he works it.
2. **Good things cannot be lost** (v.29) — God's predestinating purpose is to conform us to the image of Christ. This is as good as done; Paul uses the past tense: "he glorified."
3. **The best is yet to come** (v.30) — glory. Paul *reckons* it (counts it, audits it). The sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory that shall be revealed.

Hope is also the engine of social engagement, not its enemy. Because Christians know this material world is headed for redemption — not destruction — they have the strongest possible grounds for caring about poverty, injustice, racial reconciliation, and the environment. When God enters a covenant with Noah that includes not just human beings but "every living creature" and "the earth itself," he is declaring himself not merely Creator but Savior of the creation. Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech draws explicitly from this material eschatology; Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.'s contempt for human dignity flows directly from his disbelief in any meaningful future.

The English word "hope" is dangerously weak. Biblical hope is life-shaping certainty about the future — so certain it changes how you do the most tedious present task, the way a guaranteed billion-dollar prize changes how someone assembles parts all day. Two people in identical rooms, doing identical work, experience it entirely differently because of what they believe is coming.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 15 sources]

Keller returns most frequently to these biblical passages in his preaching on hope:

- **Romans 8:18-30** — The foundational text: creation groaning, the golden chain of predestination, and the three pillars of Christian joy. "I reckon that the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory that shall be revealed in us."
- **Revelation 21:1-8; 21:21–22:5** — The new heaven and new earth; no more death, mourning, crying or pain; "Behold, I am making everything new." The distinction between *kainos* (qualitative newness) and *neos* (chronological youth). The tree of life and river of life from Genesis returned — the healing of the nations.
- **1 Corinthians 15:35-58** — The resurrection body: "What is sown a physical body is raised a spiritual body." The defeat of death. "Death, where is your sting?"
- **1 Corinthians 13:1-13** — Heaven as *telion*, the fulfillment of design. Heaven is not peace or serenity (as in Eastern religion) but face-to-face love — seeing God and being fully known. The last word of the chapter is *agape*, not *eirene*. "Heaven is a world of love."
- **Hebrews 11:32-40** — The "others" who trusted God without receiving miracles: faith that doesn't require success is the ultimate success. The Maccabean martyrs. "A better resurrection."
- **Hebrews 10:19-25** — Hope as the anchor of Christian community. Confidence (*parresia*) to enter the holy place through the blood of Jesus transforms our relationships — we spur one another, encourage one another, bear one another.
- **2 Corinthians 4:7-18** — "We do not lose heart... our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory that far outweighs them all."
- **Ephesians 1:11-14** — The personal hope: Christians are God's *inheritance*, his treasure, the bulk of his net worth. The material hope: the Holy Spirit is a *down payment* guaranteeing the redemption of our bodies and the whole created order.
- **1 Peter 1:3-13** — Born into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ. The paradox of simultaneous suffering and inexpressible joy. "Joy unspeakable and full of glory."
- **Isaiah 40:1-11, 27-31** — The king is coming. The warrior is a shepherd. "Those who wait on the Lord will renew their strength." Waiting means obeying, relaxing, and *expecting* — treating God as king by having high expectations of what he can do.
- **Isaiah 35:1-10** — The messianic homecoming: deserts bloom, the blind see, the lame leap. The highway of holiness. We are homeless exiles, and God came homeless — born in a stable, foxes have holes — so we could be brought home.
- **Psalm 96** — The whole creation called to sing and rejoice before God's judgment. The trees of the forest sing for joy. Judgment Day is good news because it means the healing of everything that is broken.
- **Genesis 9:1-17** — The Noahic covenant: God enters a covenant not merely with humans but with every living creature and "the earth" itself. The rainbow is God's war bow laid aside — mercy and judgment meeting. God is the Savior, not merely the Creator, of the natural world.
- **1 Samuel 2:6-10 (Hannah's Song)** — God works through weakness, not strength; through the barren, the excluded, the marginal. Hannah's suffering was not meaningless. The anointed king comes through the pattern of impossible births.
- **1 Timothy 6:6-19** — Godliness with contentment is great gain. Money is a trap because it blinds you to who you are and how much you have. The three Edwards pillars are the antidote: the bad will turn out for good, the good can never be taken away, the best is yet to come.
- **Psalm 126** — Tears sown, joy reaped. The captives who return to Zion like dreamers.
- **Isaiah 60:4-11, 17-21** — The wealth of nations flowing into the new Jerusalem; glory replacing bronze; the least becoming a mighty nation.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 15 sources]

**The entropy problem.** Modern Western culture is obsessed with newness — new phones, new fashions, new experiences — because we are subconsciously aware that we are fading. "I look in the mirror and I'm falling apart." Diets, cosmetics, formaldehyde — all attempts to delay the inevitable. The gospel names this impulse honestly and then answers it: not with delay, but with reversal.

**Optimism vs. hope.** Keller distinguishes Christian hope from secular optimism throughout his preaching. Optimism is based on circumstances; hope is based on promise. Optimism collapses when circumstances turn bad. Hope can coexist with — even overwhelm — grief, because it is not contingent on current conditions. "Your joy will be strong to the degree you understand and grasp these three things." The English word "hope" is too weak — biblical hope is certainty about the future that shapes the present.

**The two-rooms illustration.** Two people in identical rooms, doing identical tasks, experience the work entirely differently because one is told she will earn $15,000 and the other $15 million (or $1 billion). Same circumstances, different hopes, radically different experience. Keller uses this as his baseline illustration for how hope functions. We are unavoidably, irreducibly hope-shaped creatures.

**J.R.R. Tolkien and the eucatastrophe / On Fairy Stories.** Tolkien's concept of eucatastrophe — the sudden turn from catastrophe to joy — is a recurring Keller reference. The *Lord of the Rings* scene where Sam looks up from the slopes of Mount Doom and sees a star ("the shadow of evil in the long run was a passing thing") illustrates what Keller calls "inner poetry" — the discipline of imagining glory until the beauty smites your heart. Tolkien's essay "On Fairy Stories" provides Keller with a deeper argument: stories of escape from death, love without parting, and triumph of good over evil wet an "unbearable" longing that nothing in realistic fiction can scratch — because these longings are real and their fulfillment is coming. Tolkien persuaded C.S. Lewis that the story of Jesus is not one more myth pointing to an underlying reality but the underlying reality to which all the myths point: "myth became fact."

**The Maccabean martyrs.** This historical example (from 2 Maccabees 7) recurs throughout Keller's Easter preaching. A mother watches seven sons tortured to death under Antiochus Epiphanes rather than renounce God. She encourages each one by saying God will give their bodies back. She was not sentimental — she was reckoning. Keller uses this story to demonstrate what genuine hope looks like when it is stripped of all comfort and forced to stand alone.

**Somerset Maugham, Philip Carey, and the park bench.** In *The Summing Up*, Maugham concedes that if there is no afterlife, then "there is no meaning for life, and thus life has no meaning." His autobiographical character Philip Carey has an epiphany on a London park bench: freedom from all obligation, yes — but "he realized he could never be happy." Keller uses this to show that secularism cannot explain why we persist in believing life has meaning.

**Cynthia Heimel and the celebrities.** The Village Voice columnist observed that people who became famous found it did not satisfy: "the giant thing they were striving for had happened, and nothing changed... I think that when God really wants to play a rotten practical joke on us, he grants our deepest wishes and then giggles merrily." Keller cites this as secular confirmation of Ibsen's "life lie."

**The African American spiritual tradition.** Keller is deeply struck by how enslaved people developed a rich theology of heaven not as escapism but as resistance. They understood the resurrection more deeply than the comfortable because their circumstances forced them to reckon with it. "What African-American Spirituals Teach Us about Heaven and Hell" is a recurring lecture theme.

**Martin Luther King Jr. and the material hope.** David Chappell's *A Stone of Hope* argues that black activists succeeded where white liberals failed because they were shaped by a biblical understanding of sin and the kingdom of God. White liberals had hope in human nature and science; King had hope in divine intervention against human sin. The "I Have a Dream" speech draws directly from Isaiah ("every valley exalted, every mountain made low") — a material, eschatological hope, not a merely political one.

**Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. and the absence of hope.** Holmes, who disbelieved in any ultimate future, wrote: "I see no reason for attributing to a man a significance different in kind from that which belongs to a baboon or a grain of sand... doesn't all this squashy sentimentality about the dignity and sacredness of human life make you puke?" He then upheld Virginia's forced sterilization of a woman. Keller pairs this with King to show that what you believe about the ultimate future has catastrophic or redemptive social consequences.

**Rodney Stark and the Roman plagues.** In *The Rise of Christianity*, Stark documents how Christians stayed in plague-ridden Roman cities to care for the sick while pagan doctors fled — because Christians knew death could only translate them into glory, and this material world was worth redeeming. Their survival rates were three times higher. This is Keller's empirical case that "the most heavenly-minded people are the most earthly good."

**Homelessness and the park.** Keller uses New York's parks as an extended metaphor: parks are beautiful, but you cannot live in them. When homeless people try to, they destroy the park and the park destroys them. Career, romance, family, art — these are parks, not homes. God alone is home. Jesus was born homeless (on the road, in a stable), lived homeless ("foxes have holes"), and died homeless (crucified outside the gate) so that we could be brought home.

**Ivan Karamazov and Dostoevsky.** Keller regularly quotes Ivan Karamazov's concession from *The Brothers Karamazov*: "I believe that suffering will be healed and made up for, that in the world's finale, at the moment of eternal harmony, something so precious will come to pass that it will suffice for all hearts, for the comforting of all resentments." Even an unbeliever senses that only a transformed future can justify the suffering of the past.

**Epicurus on death.** Keller engages the Greek philosopher's argument: "I could die happy if I were totally sure that death was peaceful oblivion. But since no one can know what happens after death, no one can die happy." Christianity alone, among all worldviews, doesn't just offer a story about the afterlife — it offers a risen man.

**Sauron's ring of power.** Tolkien's reply to a reader who found the plot implausible: "The Ring of Sauron is only one of the various mythical ways of treating the placing of one's life or power in some external object which is thus exposed to capture or destruction with disastrous results to oneself." When you make anything other than God your ring of power — romance, career, beauty — its loss melts you down. Only God has *kavod* (glory, weight, substance). Everything else is weightless, passing, ephemeral.

From "Heaven, a World of Love" (1 Corinthians 13, 1996):

> "The love between the Father and the Son is the song of which all other songs are just hints and echoes. This is the waterfall of which all other waterfalls are just hints and echoes... And we're brought into the center of it."

> "Hell is a place where everybody's impatient... unkind, never forgets a grudge... utterly self-absorbed. Hell is lovelessness. Heaven is a world of love."

From "Hope for the World" (Ephesians 1, 2009):

This is the land I've been looking for all my life though I never knew it.'"
