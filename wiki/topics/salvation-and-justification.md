# Salvation and Justification

## Summary [coverage: high — 26 sources]

Tim Keller's preaching on salvation and justification forms the theological spine of his entire ministry. Across hundreds of sermons — from his earliest work at Redeemer in 1992 through his mature Romans, Ephesians, Galatians, and Christmas series — he returns obsessively to a single claim: the gospel is not a self-improvement program. Salvation is not something you contribute to by being better; it is something entirely done for you, and received entirely by faith.

The thread that runs through every Keller sermon on this theme is the diagnosis of the human heart as curved in on itself (Luther's *incurvitus in se*) — and the announcement that Jesus Christ has done the one thing that breaks that curvature. He came not to help us save ourselves but to save us in our helplessness, seating us — legally, actually — at the right hand of God, in the place of highest honor.

Keller's approach to justification is classically Reformed: God declares the sinner righteous on the basis of Christ's righteousness imputed to the believer. This is not merely a legal technicality — it is the answer to the deepest hunger of every human soul. "Every human heart is thirsty for the thunderous applause of some crowd," he says; justification means you receive instead the praise and delight of God himself, grounded not in what you have done but in what Christ did.

The new sermons in this compilation enrich the picture significantly. Through the lens of Colossians 1 (*God and Sinners Reconciled*), Keller explains reconciliation as the restoration of a broken relationship whose debt cannot be willed away — requiring the blood of Christ, who takes our uncleanness upon himself like the woman with the hemorrhage touching the holy. Through his Galatians sermons (*Grace to the Barren I and II*, *Hagar and the Son*), he shows how the Hagar–Sarah allegory reveals two modes of relating to God: earning by human effort versus receiving by supernatural grace. Through Luke 15 (*He Came to Himself*), he exposes the two wrong modes of repentance — self-pity masquerading as remorse versus the gospel repentance that looks at the Savior and is truly freed. Through his Christmas sermons (*His Name Shall be Called*, *Home from Exile*, *Fullness of His Grace Advent*), he frames the Incarnation itself as the ultimate act of justifying grace — the Word becoming flesh so that the homeless could be brought home, the guilty could be freed.

What distinguishes Keller's preaching from generic Reformed orthodoxy is his cultural acuity. He translates justification into terms that resonate with secular New Yorkers, showing how the need to justify oneself — to boast in career, beauty, morality, or achievement — explains workaholism, identity fragility, self-righteousness, racism, and the inability to forgive. The gospel does not merely fix a theological problem; it cures the sickness that makes us miserable, divided, and cruel.

His two-word formula: **"You are accepted, therefore perform"** (gospel) vs. **"Perform, therefore you are accepted"** (moralism/religion). This inversion — "the great reversal" — is the engine of every sermon he preaches on salvation.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 26 sources]

### The Diagnosis: Boasting and the Curved Heart

Keller's diagnosis of the human condition begins with Martin Luther's phrase: the human heart is *incurvitus in se* — curved in on itself. He quotes Luther: "Our nature is so deeply curved in on itself that it wickedly, curvy, and viciously seeks to use all things, even God, for its own sake."

This self-centeredness does not primarily produce crude evil. More often, it produces impressive religiosity, hard work, and careful morality — but all in service of the self. The man who obeys the Ten Commandments, goes to church, and reads his Bible may be doing all of it to purchase God's favor and earn a sense of superiority. When things go wrong and God does not deliver, such a person becomes angry, revealing that his religion was really a transaction: "I've lived well, so where's my payoff?"

Keller uses the ancient concept of "boasting" (Paul's *kauchaomai*) to frame this. Before a battle, soldiers would boast to fire up their courage: "We have iron chariots. We have the greatest king." Every soul does this with something — money, career, morality, relationships, ethnic identity, political cause. "Boasting" is what the heart does to generate confidence to face life. It says: *this is why I am love-worthy, this is why I matter, this is my glory.*

But boasting is also always a taunt. When your identity is grounded in one thing, you must look down on those who lack it. The self-righteous moralist despises the immoral. The hard worker despises the lazy. The proud racial nationalist demonizes the other group. This is why the human heart's boasting not only distorts the individual (puffing up when you succeed, destroying yourself when you fail) but divides the human race.

### The Need for Reconciliation

In *God and Sinners Reconciled*, Keller grounds the need for salvation in a careful analysis of divine law. What is the purpose of human law? Peace and fellowship. What is its content? To treat human beings as nothing less than what they are. What are the consequences of breaking it? An unreconciled relationship, a debt that must be paid.

Divine law works identically, but infinitely more so. To live as if you belong to yourself — as your own king — is to treat God as less than what he is. This is not arbitrary rule-breaking; it is a fractured relationship, like a citizen who has broken a law and owes a debt to the community. The difference is that our debt to God is infinite. Keller: "You can't sin against God, treat him as less than who he is, and then turn around and say, well, no problem." Voltaire's deathbed remark — "God will forgive me, that's his job" — is not humility but cosmic presumption. The judge wants to forgive, but justice requires payment. We are, as the Levitical clean laws made visually concrete, "unclean, unholy, blemished, unreconciled."

### The Antidote: Justification by Faith

Paul's answer in Romans 3–4 is justification — being declared righteous before God not on the basis of performance but on the basis of Christ's record, credited to the believer by faith. Keller unpacks the Greek word *logizdemi* ("credited"): an accounting term meaning that though you have not earned a million dollars, if it is placed in your account it is yours to spend. The righteousness Christ earned — his perfect obedience, his suffering of the divine judgment we deserved — is placed in the believer's account.

This is not merely a change in legal status. Keller insists it is the answer to the deepest psychological and spiritual need: the need for the applause of God. He quotes C.S. Lewis's *Weight of Glory* at length: the promise of glory is "fame with God" — the delight, the acclamation, the welcome of the one we were made to please. "The door on which we have been knocking all our lives will open at last." This is the praise that, unlike human acclaim, does not puff you up and does not rot your soul, because you know you did not earn it.

Keller draws on the kinsman redeemer (*goel*) concept from Ruth and Leviticus 25. A *goel* must be: (1) of the same flesh and blood, (2) acting voluntarily out of love, (3) bearing the entire cost himself. Jesus is the ultimate *goel*: he had to become human (the Incarnation), he went to the cross freely, and he bore the infinite debt — not just forgiving the debt but, like Boaz with Ruth, bringing the redeemed one into his own household, his own inheritance, flooding their life with honor.

The cross accomplishes three things Keller identifies from Romans 3:

1. **Redemption** — liberation from slavery to guilt, shame, and false masters (career, relationships, money, achievement)
2. **Propitiation** — bearing the wrath of God, paying the debt to justice himself (God does not demand our blood; he sheds his own)
3. **Demonstration** — publicly showing who God is: both just *and* justifier, more holy than any legalistic religion and more loving than any liberal secularism

### The Way of Reconciliation: Substitution and the Transfer of Blemish

Keller's most vivid account of how justification works comes through the Old Testament imagery of the sacrificial system. In *God and Sinners Reconciled* (Colossians 1), he explains the Levitical clean laws: everything from bodily purity to dietary rules was a visual aid saying, "spiritually, you are unfit for the presence of God." Then he moves to the Levitical sacrifice: you lay hands on the animal. Its blemishlessness becomes yours; your blemishes become its. The animal gets what your sins deserve.

The woman with the hemorrhage in Mark 5 crystallizes this. She was ritually unclean — anyone she touched became unclean. She reaches out in secret and touches Jesus. In the normal logic of holiness, she should have made him unclean. Instead, her uncleanness goes to him and his wholeness goes to her. Keller: "Her flow of blood stops because his flow of blood began. She did not make him dead. He got her blemishes, and we now stand before God, represented by Jesus Christ, holy, blameless, and free from accusation."

His most compressed statement of the gospel comes from John Stott, which he quotes repeatedly: "The essence of sin is us substituting ourselves for God, while the essence of salvation is God substituting himself for us." We put ourselves where only God deserves to be — at the center, on the throne. God puts himself where we deserve to be — on the cross.

On the cross, Paul says in Philippians 2, the eternal Son "made himself of no reputation" (*ekenosen* — emptied himself). He had the infinite thunderous applause of heaven — the adoration of the Father and all creation. He came into the world to receive mockery, jeering, rejection, and the ultimate condemnation: "Depart from me." He took the divine "boo" so that we could receive the divine "Well done, good and faithful servant." He was forsaken so that we might be welcomed.

### Grace vs. Works: The Hagar–Sarah Allegory

The Galatians sermons add a critical dimension. In *Hagar and the Son* (Genesis 16) and *Grace to the Barren I and II* (Galatians 4:21–31), Keller shows how Abraham's choice between Hagar and Sarah dramatizes the choice every soul faces: to obtain the blessing through human ability or to receive it through divine supernatural grace.

Hagar represents the covenant of law — earning salvation through moral and religious performance. Sarah represents the covenant of promise — receiving salvation through faith in what God accomplishes miraculously. Paul's point is not about the women themselves but about two entirely different orientations toward God. Keller: "The gospel is not that you develop a righteousness and give it to God. The answer of the gospel is that God develops a righteousness and gives it to you."

Both approaches produce slavery. If you rely on your own human ability, you will become a slave. "You'll need it. You'll have to do it. If anything gets in your way, you'll be filled with anger. Even if it doesn't get in your way, you'll be driven and driven. You'll be a slave." But if you understand that what Jesus Christ has done is sufficient — nothing to add — you'll be free. "You'll work but you won't have to work. You'll try to succeed but you won't have to succeed."

The Isaiah quotation Paul uses — "More are the children of the desolate woman than of her who has a husband" — announces an astonishing reversal: those who rely not on their own resources but entirely on God's grace will be more fruitful, not less. The barren woman who gives herself to God will have more children than the fertile one who relies on her own ability. Keller applies this to New York specifically: the person whose career, relationship, or social status is not their salvation can work with freedom and fruitfulness that the driven, anxious person never achieves.

### Salvation as Homecoming

The Christmas sermons introduce the theme of exile and homecoming as a lens for justification. In *Home From Exile* (Isaiah 35), Keller argues that the entire human race is spiritually homeless — we were made for the Garden of Eden, for communion with God, and our rebellion expelled us. "Every human being is actually in exile. Spiritually, we are homeless." Martin Heidegger's *Unheimlichkeit* (uncanniness, homelessness) names something the Bible teaches: we live in a world that doesn't fit our deepest desires because we were made for a world that was lost.

Jesus came homeless — born on the road, in a stable, laid in a feed trough. He lived homeless ("Foxes have holes... but the Son of Man has no place to lay his head"). And he was expelled from the community in death, crucified outside the gate — the scapegoat driven out so that those who believe in him can be brought home. "He was born outside the door, and now he's crucified outside the city, outside the community. He's being expelled. He's getting the cosmic homelessness that we deserve so we can be brought in."

In *His Name Shall Be Called* (Isaiah 9), Keller frames Christmas as gift-giving: "For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given." Gift, not wages. Not something earned but something given with infinite generosity. And what is the gift? Not power over enemies but a Savior who deals with sin from within: "He became your righteousness. His moral record, his personal righteousness is made over to you. It's written over. It's accounted to you." This is justification as Christmas gift.

### The Result: Freedom from Boasting and Genuine Repentance

When a person genuinely grasps that they have God's own delight — not earned but given, through Christ — they no longer need to ground their identity in anything else. This is what Paul means in Galatians 6:14: "May I never boast except in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ, through whom the world has been crucified to me and I to the world."

Keller calls this "the one boast that ends all boasting." When you boast in the cross, you can look at the things the world offers — career success, the love of another person, wealth, moral superiority — and say: "You're a good thing, but you're not an ultimate thing. You cannot control me."

The Prodigal Son parable (*He Came to Himself*, Luke 15) shows the difference between gospel repentance and moralistic self-improvement. The younger son's initial plan — to earn his way back as a hired worker, paying back every cent — is the religious mistake. It treats repentance as atonement through self-flagellation. But the father runs to meet him while he is "still a long way off." The father's love does not need to be earned or revived by repentance; the repentance is actually enabled by the father's love.

Keller: "It's not like our repentance gets God's love. God's love enables the repentance. God's pouncing aggressive love enables the repentance." Gospel repentance is not looking at yourself in self-pity but looking at the Savior in wonder. "I've been loved and his love will never change. Look at what he did for me. How can I treat him like this?" That kind of repentance — focused on Christ rather than on self — is the only repentance that actually changes the heart permanently.

The healing of Peter in John 21 (*Healing of Peter*) extends this: Jesus heals Peter not by demanding behavioral change but by surgery on the root sin — pride, self-reliance, the use of others to confirm one's own worth. True repentance always unmasks the sin as a form of "playing God," putting the self on the throne Christ deserves. When Peter sees that Christ is his foundation rather than his own integrity, he runs to Jesus rather than away from him.

### The 1992 Ephesians Series ("Salvation from the Outside In")

Keller's earliest systematic treatment of justification appears in the 1992 "Salvation from the Outside In" series at Redeemer Presbyterian, New York City. Preached when the church was just three years old to a congregation of young Manhattan professionals, these sermons address the human condition, sin, adoption, faith, and grace with unusual directness and pastoral warmth. The Ephesians 2 sermons in particular lay out what would become Keller's signature framework: sin as self-centeredness (*incurvitus in se*), salvation as the great exchange, and the contrast between the slave mentality and the son mentality. The *Gods Plan* sermons in this series add the macroscopic view: salvation is not just individual forgiveness but cosmic reconciliation, the reuniting of all things under Christ.

### The 2009 Romans Series ("Bible: The Whole Story")

The mature statement of Keller's soteriology appears in the 2009 Romans series. These sermons — on justification, the blood of Christ, and saving faith — synthesize decades of pastoral experience with careful exegesis. The three sermons on Romans 3 (The Failure of Religion, By the Blood of Jesus Christ, Boasting and Faith) form a sustained argument: religion fails because the human heart is too self-centered to obey from love; propitiation is necessary because justice is inseparable from love; and faith is the antidote to boasting because it grounds the identity in God's applause rather than performance.

### The Galatians Series (c. 1994–95)

Keller's Galatians sermons develop the Hagar–Sarah allegory (ch. 4) as the most powerful biblical illustration of the difference between saving faith and works-righteousness. *Grace to the Barren I* focuses on friendship and spiritual formation as the channels through which gospel truth penetrates the heart rather than merely bludgeoning it. *Grace to the Barren II* and *Hagar and the Son* argue that every culture assigns people a "salvation" — children, career, beauty, status — and that the gospel is the only force that can liberate people from being whipped between superiority (Hagar's arrogance) and inferiority (Sarah's despair).

### The Advent/Christmas Sermons

Keller's Christmas and Advent preaching consistently grounds the gift of salvation in the Incarnation itself. *God and Sinners Reconciled* (Colossians 1) frames Christmas as the answer to the universal human problem: we are unreconciled to God, blemished, unfit for his presence, and the blood of Christ is the only way of reconciliation. *Home from Exile* (Isaiah 35) frames salvation as homecoming — the spiritually homeless returning to God as their true dwelling place. *His Name Shall Be Called* (Isaiah 9) frames justification as a gift given to those who come as shepherds — those who cannot make themselves worthy — rather than wages earned by the deserving. *Fullness of His Grace Advent* (John 1:14–18) frames the Incarnation as the end of meaninglessness and the end of guilt: "From the fullness of his grace, we have all received one blessing after another."

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Keller returns to the following passages more than any others on salvation and justification:

**Ephesians 2:1–10** — "Dead in trespasses and sins... but God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ." Keller calls this "one of the richest passages in all the Bible, word for word, on what it means to be saved." He preached it in full series in 1992 and returned to it in 2011. The structure — saved *from* (vv. 1–3), saved *through* (vv. 4–7), saved *for* (vv. 8–10) — gives him his canonical account of the human condition, the mechanism of grace, and the life that results.

**Colossians 1:19–23** — The reconciliation passage. Keller uses the temple clean laws and the sacrificial system to unpack what it means to be "presented holy and blameless and free from accusation." Christ reconciles us by taking our blemishedness upon himself and giving us his cleanness. He is both personally beautiful and legally perfect in God's sight.

**Romans 3:21–31** — The heart of Paul's soteriology: righteousness from God, justification freely by grace, redemption through Christ Jesus, propitiation through faith in his blood. Keller unpacks the three key words (redemption, propitiation, demonstration) as the complete account of what the cross accomplishes.

**Romans 3:27–4:8** — The contrast of faith and boasting, with Abraham as case study. Genesis 15:6 ("Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness") interpreted through the covenant ceremony where God himself walks between the pieces, taking the curse upon himself.

**Galatians 4:21–31** — The Hagar–Sarah allegory. Two sons, two covenants, two ways of relating to God: human effort versus supernatural grace. Paul's "figurative" reading is not allegory in the loose sense but pastoral counseling — showing people that if they rely on their own religious performance, they will be slaves; if they receive grace, they will be free.

**Isaiah 9:6–7** — "For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given." Keller reads "given" as the heart of Christmas: Christianity is not a wage religion but a gift religion. Jesus is the gift of righteousness, wisdom, sanctification, and redemption.

**Isaiah 35:1–10** — The promise of homecoming for the exiled. Keller uses this to frame salvation as the end of spiritual homelessness — the redeemed returning to God as their true dwelling, their unheimlichkeit finally resolved.

**John 1:14–18** — The Word made flesh, full of grace and truth. The Logos becoming flesh is the end of meaninglessness (the ideal became real) and the end of guilt (the blood of God cleanses Lady Macbeth's spot). "From the fullness of his grace, we have all received one blessing after another."

**Luke 15:11–20** — The Prodigal Son. Keller uses the son's plan to "earn his way back" as the model of religious repentance (self-pity, self-atonement) and the father's running to meet him as the model of gospel grace. The son's repentance was enabled by the father's love, not the other way around.

**John 21:4–17** — The healing of Peter. Jesus heals through surgical repentance — taking Peter back to the fire (the scene of his denial), pressing past behavior into the root sin of pride and self-reliance. Gospel repentance: "Because you, Lord Jesus Christ, are my Savior and my righteousness, I put you in the center of my life."

**Galatians 2:4–16** — Freedom in Christ vs. slavery to law. Peter's racism as a failure to "walk in line with the truth of the gospel." The connection between justification and social ethics: if you know you're a sinner saved by grace, you cannot feel superior to anyone.

**Galatians 6:14** — "May I never boast except in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ, through whom the world has been crucified to me and I to the world." Keller's favorite summary of what faith looks like in practice.

**Ephesians 1:4–8** — Election and adoption: "He predestined us to be adopted as his sons through Jesus Christ." Keller treats adoption as the apex of salvation — higher than pardon, higher even than justification in the technical sense, because the Father loves the adopted child as much as he loves the Son.

**Genesis 15** — The covenant ceremony. God walks between the pieces of the slaughtered animals, taking on himself the curse of the covenant, the self-maledictory oath: *if I fail to bless you, let this happen to me.* Fulfilled in Christ on the cross.

**Genesis 32:22–32** — Jacob wrestling with God at the Jabbok. Keller reads this as the paradigm of how saving encounter with God works: personal, wrestling (contradictory, agonizing), and always "winning through losing." Jacob is smitten not in justice but in grace — because one of his seed (Jesus, the son of David) would be smitten in full justice on behalf of all who had wrestled with God.

**1 John 2:28–3:3** — Born of Him. No one can live the righteous life God requires unless they are born again; being born again means receiving the "divine DNA," the nature of God himself implanted in the soul; and this new birth flows from beholding the lavish love of God in adoption: "How great is the love the Father has lavished on us, that we should be called children of God — and that is what we are."

**Philippians 2:5–11** — The self-emptying of Christ (*kenosis*). He had infinite praise in heaven and emptied himself of it, taking "the form of a servant." Used by Keller to show the cost of propitiation and the love that drives it.

**Isaiah 56:1–8** — God's salvation extended to all who hold fast to the covenant. Used to show the breadth of justification across ethnic and social boundaries.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Keller's genius lies in translation — taking the technical language of Reformed soteriology and pressing it into contact with the lived experience of secular New Yorkers. The following entry points recur across sermons:

### Career and Identity in New York City

Manhattan in the 1990s–2000s was (and remains) a city organized around professional achievement. Keller addresses this directly: the person whose identity is in their career has turned their job into a slave master. "A slave master is someone who has no limits and who beats you if you fail." When your career is your *goel*, it will beat you when you fail — and you will beat yourself, consumed by self-loathing, shame, and anxiety. The asset manager who nearly became suicidal after financial loss illustrates what justification liberates you from.

### Hagar and Sarah: Every Culture Assigns You a Salvation

In *Hagar and the Son*, Keller makes a pointed cultural observation: "In traditional cultures, what a woman had to have in order to have value was children. In our culture, it's career and individual achievement. Different content, same structure." Every culture has a Peninnah who taunts you if you fail to meet its standards for significance. The gospel is "incredibly subversive" because it smashes every culture's idol system. Whether you're being told you need children to matter (ancient culture) or that you need professional success to matter (modern Manhattan), the gospel says: "In Christ, your worth is already settled."

### Kafka, Guilt, and the Modern Self

Keller uses Franz Kafka's diary entry to press secular objections: "The state in which we find ourselves today is sinful, quite independent of guilt." Kafka was a modern, secular person who intellectually rejected the category of moral guilt — yet he could not shake the sense of condemnation. Keller argues this is universal: Western culture tells people they are free from guilt because they write their own moral scripts, yet they are driven by anxiety, workaholism, obsession with appearance, and need for validation.

### The Walker Percy Illustration

From Andrew Delbanco's reading of Walker Percy's novel *Love in the Ruins*: a psychiatrist tells a patient that he should not feel guilty after an affair. The patient says: "That's what worries me — I don't feel guilty." Delbanco comments: "What the psychiatrist does not understand is the guilt he no longer feels had been his last reassurance that there existed something in the world that transcended him." Keller uses this to press the point that if there is no guilt, there is no meaning.

### Rocky and the Ego

Keller uses the first Rocky film repeatedly. Rocky tells Adrian he just wants to "go the distance" with the champion: "Then I'll know I'm not a bum." Keller: "Down deep inside, all of us feel like we're bums. And we're doing something out there to try to convince the world and ourselves we're not." This is the universal human project — what Paul calls "boasting."

### The Super Bowl Boast

Before a Super Bowl, one team's player boasts about his defense's dominance — it fires up his team but also fires up the opposing team. The coach pins the quote to the locker-room bulletin board: "Are you going to take that?" Boasting always produces division, conflict, bad blood. And when the soul's identity is grounded in a boast, the same dynamic plays out: pride puffs up, taunt divides, failure destroys.

### The Concert Hall Applause

Keller describes a recording of Daniel Barenboim conducting the Berlin Philharmonic in Beethoven's Choral Fantasy. One second after the last note, the audience erupts in thunderous applause for ten seconds. He asks: what would it feel like to be the musician receiving that? Then: "Paul says you need to walk around with that in your heart all the time. That's how you face the world. But it must be God's applause — because only that will not distort you, will not rot your soul, will not puff you up. Only that you can never lose."

### C.S. Lewis: *The Weight of Glory*

Keller quotes Lewis's essay at length in multiple sermons. Lewis discovers that Thomas Aquinas and John Milton understood heavenly glory as "fame with God" — the applause, the delight, the welcome of the Father. Lewis: "The door on which we have been knocking all our lives will open at last." And: "The moment that heals her old inferiority complex forever will also be the moment that drowns her pride deeper than Prospero's book." Keller treats this as the definitive psychological account of what justification does: it both heals the sense of worthlessness and humbles the pride, simultaneously, because it is entirely unearned.

### Billy Graham at Cambridge (1955)

Keller tells the story of Billy Graham invited to preach at Cambridge's Great St. Mary's, to skepticism and embarrassment from the academic establishment. Graham preached the blood of Christ for three-quarters of an hour. Four hundred students stayed to commit their lives to Christ. A young man who became a pastor remembered walking out thinking for the first time: "Christ really died for me." Keller's point: what intellectuals dismiss as primitive is, in fact, the one thing that actually transforms a person.

### The Woman in the Trailer Park

A woman in Keller's Virginia congregation had spent years in abusive relationships with men, always needing a man's love to feel she had worth. When she understood the gospel — that Christ is her life, not any man — she found freedom. Inwardly, meeting new men, she would say: "You're nice, but you're not my life." She could finally make good choices because her identity was no longer hostage to their approval. Keller presents this as the pastoral proof of justification: it actually liberates from the slave masters that ruin people's lives.

### The Slave vs. the Son (Galatians 4)

Keller's pastoral application of justification regularly uses the metaphor of the slave and the son in a great household. Both are busy, both work hard — but with entirely different inner orientations. The slave works under compulsion and fear, up and down emotionally based on performance, critical and gossipy (needing to feel superior because not secure), defensive and unable to take criticism. The son works out of love, is emotionally stable (neither puffed up by success nor crushed by failure), affirming and generous toward others, and able to repent with joy. "Which are you?" is the pastoral question Keller presses.

### Bob Dylan and the True Meaning of Christmas

In *God and Sinners Reconciled*, Keller cites Bob Dylan's discomfort at singing "We Are the World" at the 1985 Live Aid concert. When asked why, Dylan said: "Because man cannot save himself." Keller: "So we look today unto Bob Dylan for the true meaning of Christmas. Because he got it right. The Bible says Jesus Christ came because we cannot save ourselves." The public culture says Christmas means human solidarity producing a better world; the actual gospel says Jesus came to reconcile sinners to God because we cannot reconcile ourselves.

### The Fish in the Water

The fish on the grass has lost its freedom. Put it back in the water — that is the right restriction, the restriction that fits its nature — and its strength and freedom return. Freedom is not the absence of all constraint; it is living in accord with the truth of what you were made for. The gospel is the only truth that can set a human soul truly free.

### Angels with Dirty Faces: Heroism Through Weakness

In *Hero of Heroes* (Acts 5), Keller uses the 1938 Jimmy Cagney film. Rocky the gangster, on death row, is asked by his priest friend Jerry to die as a coward — to give up his last possession, his reputation for bravery — so that the delinquent boys who idolize him will be saved from the same path. Keller: "Only if you relinquish your glory, only if you give up everything, can you save these kids." This is the heroism of the cross: not power over enemies but giving up all power in order to save. Jesus' heroism is "not subject to abuse" precisely because it flows from self-emptying rather than self-assertion.

### Lady Macbeth and the Spot That Won't Come Out

In *Fullness of His Grace Advent*, Keller addresses the guilt that many New Yorkers cannot shake — guilt for specific betrayals and violations that no amount of therapy or self-talk will remove. He quotes Lady Macbeth's "Out, damn spot!" and then: "Look at the expense of this gift. This is the most utterly costly gift... The blood of God. What is that worth? I don't know what you've done. I don't care what you've done. Your debt can't be a match for that value."
