# Justice and Mercy

## Summary [coverage: high — 9+ sources]

No theme runs deeper through Tim Keller's preaching than the integration of justice, mercy, and the gospel of Jesus Christ. From his earliest sermons at Redeemer Presbyterian Church in New York City in the late 1980s through his mature theological work in the 2010s, Keller returned again and again to a single, unifying conviction: that the gospel does not merely provide motivation for social concern as an add-on to "real" Christianity — it is the foundation that makes genuine, durable justice possible at all.

Keller's treatment of justice is deliberately comprehensive. He refuses the twin temptations that have fragmented American Christianity: the conservative temptation to reduce faith to personal morality and private piety, and the liberal temptation to reduce it to social programming with religious vocabulary. Both, he argues, are distortions. Biblical justice is not a department in the church — it is a mark of the church. Jonathan Edwards, whom Keller invokes often, put it bluntly: "Where have we any command in the Bible laid down in stronger terms, and in a more peremptory, urgent manner, than the command of giving to the poor?" Keller takes that declaration at face value.

What makes Keller's approach distinctive is his insistence that the gospel simultaneously creates the *theological motivation* and the *psychological equipment* to pursue justice without burnout, paternalism, or ideology. The person who understands that they were spiritually bankrupt, that they came to God with nothing, that Jesus "got off his steed" and came to them in the road — that person no longer looks at the materially poor with condescension. They see their own story. They become, in Keller's phrase, "poor in spirit," and that poverty of spirit is the only source of genuinely humble, persistent, non-paternalistic service to the poor.

Keller also insists that biblical justice is broader than either charity or politics. It encompasses both *mercy* (responding to economic need with material generosity) and *justice* (addressing the structural conditions that produce and perpetuate poverty). The Hebrew words *mishpat* and *tzedakah/tzedek* — "maintain justice and do what is right" (Isa 56:1) — together define a life of social justice that is always on the lookout for the vulnerable, always treating one's goods as belonging in some sense to the wider community, always wearing justice "as a robe and turban" (Job 29). This is not occasional charity. It is a posture of life.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 8 sources]

### The Diagnosis: Poverty as Both Economic and Social Condition

Keller consistently insists that poverty must be understood before it can be addressed. Drawing heavily on the Old Testament's 200+ direct references to the poor, he identifies two interlocking dimensions:

**Economic poverty** is the condition of having "nothing the world values." To be poor is to be a city without walls — vulnerable to exploitation precisely because you lack the resources, skills, connections, and marketable abilities that the world rewards. Critically, Keller challenges the assumption that poverty is primarily the result of irresponsible behavior. Citing Proverbs and his reading of urban sociology, he argues that by and large the Bible presents irresponsible behavior as a *response* to poverty rather than its primary *cause*. The poor do not start poor because they failed; they fail because they started poor, because their neighbors fled, because their schools decayed, because everything that accumulates in flourishing communities was stripped away before they arrived.

**Social poverty** (oppression) is the condition of having whatever you do possess taken from you. The Hebrew word *anaw* — the oppressed — points to the structural dynamic: "A poor man's field may produce abundant food, but injustice sweeps it away" (Prov 13:23). The poor can be taken from without giving anything back, because they lack the social power to resist. Keller uses a contemporary example: a store owner who makes profit in a poor neighborhood but lives elsewhere takes money out without investing back in schools, banks, or businesses — not through malice, but through the ordinary logic of a broken world. We are all implicated.

### The Gospel Answer: Knowing, Becoming, and Loving the Poor

Keller's signature claim is that the gospel is not merely an incentive for social action — it is the *mechanism* that makes genuine social action possible. He organizes this in three movements, drawn from Luke 6 ("Blessed Are the Poor"):

**Knowing the poor** — Understanding poverty rightly, as the Bible describes it, rather than reducing it to personal failure or political talking points.

**Becoming the poor** — This is Keller's most distinctive move. The gospel requires that those who receive it become "poor in spirit." You cannot be a Christian unless you acknowledge that you came to God with absolutely nothing of value, no merits, no leverage, no claim — only the mercy of God in Jesus Christ. The two characteristics of material poverty (nothing of value; no power of self-determination) are exactly the two characteristics of spiritual poverty that the gospel demands. "Religion appeals to the able. The gospel only comes to the poor in spirit." When you truly grasp this, you look at the materially poor and see yourself. Not a project. Your own story. The condescension evaporates.

**Loving the poor** — The person who has been made poor in spirit — who has received mercy they did not earn — will give mercy they are not obligated to give. The Samaritan in Luke 10 had no legal, moral, or cultural obligation to help the wounded Jew. He was moved by *splagma* — bowels of compassion — which Keller notes is the word most often used for the emotional state of Jesus Christ in the New Testament. The Samaritan was moved by the love of Christ. That is the motor. Not guilt. Not obligation. Gospel-generated compassion.

### Justice is Not Optional

One of Keller's recurring pastoral arguments is against the church's habit of treating social concern as a spiritual elective. He drives this point home from Matthew 25 (the sheep and the goats), from James 2 (faith without deeds is dead), from 1 John 3, from Isaiah 58, and from the entire prophetic tradition. The Scottish minister Robert Murray McShane's words, which Keller quotes frequently, crystallize it: "You seem to be Christians, and yet you don't care for his poor. What a change will pass upon you as you enter the gates of heaven."

Keller is clear: caring for the poor does not earn salvation, any more than leaves give life to a tree. But leaves prove the tree is alive. "The way Jesus Christ can tell real faith from lip service is whether or not you don't just talk about love but actually give it expression in deeds to people who have physical and material needs."

### The Two Hebrew Words: Mishpat and Tzedakah

Keller devotes sustained attention to Isaiah 56:1 — "Maintain justice (*mishpat*) and do what is right (*tzedakah*)" — as the grammar of a just life:

- **Mishpat** (rectifying justice): Go after what is wrong. Punish perpetrators. But also — and this is the broader usage — watch out for the quartet of the vulnerable: widow, orphan, immigrant, and poor. Rectify the conditions that harm them.

- **Tzedakah** (primary justice): Live in such a way that if everyone lived this way, rectifying justice would rarely be needed — because all relationships would be right. The Old Testament scholar Bruce Waltke's definition: "The just person is one willing to *disadvantage themselves to advantage the community*." The wicked person is one who *disadvantages the community to advantage themselves.* This means an unwillingness to share your resources, connections, power, and talents with those who have less is not merely uncharitable — it is, biblically speaking, *unjust.*

Keller extends this through Job 29: Job wore justice "as a robe and a turban" — not an occasional act of charity, but a constant posture, an orientation of every part of life toward the vulnerable. The car-dealership illustration (a Christian owner eliminating negotiable pricing because price negotiation systematically disadvantaged older Black women) shows what this looks like in practice: examining every part of one's life and asking, "Is this just?"

### Gospel, Grace, and the Motivation Problem

A significant part of Keller's preaching on justice is addressed to a specific pastoral problem: guilt-driven justice work burns out. If the sum total of his message were "you should be doing more for the poor," he would only produce either despair or superficial compliance. The gospel resolves this.

The Samaritan story in Luke 10, Keller argues, was designed to show that the only motive for social compassion that will endure is gospel gratitude. "If you understand that Jesus Christ said that you were poor, and Jesus poured out his riches on you, and that you were spiritually bankrupt — you were the person in the road — and that Jesus came on by and got off and impoverished himself for you, until you see that Jesus Christ was your neighbor, you will not have the motivation to be a neighbor."

And McShane's paraphrase of the objections is devastating: "My money is my own." But Christ might have said, "My blood is my own." "The poor are undeserving." But Christ might have said, "These are wicked rebels — I'll die only for the deserving poor." "They might abuse my charity." But Christ knew thousands would trample his blood underfoot, and he died anyway. The logic is irreversible: the shape of our mercy must be patterned on the shape of his.

### Sermon Summaries

**Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern (1990)** — One of Keller's earliest and most foundational treatments. Working from the Good Samaritan (Luke 10), he argues that social compassion is not optional for churches — it is the inevitable expression of genuine faith and the primary motor of spiritual renewal. He invokes the Roman Emperor Julian's lament that nothing spread Christianity like its indiscriminate generosity toward the poor. Five "vitamins" of church health: worship, community, teaching, witness, and social compassion.

**Blessed Are the Poor (1998)** — A sustained theological account of poverty from 200+ Old Testament texts and Luke 6. Keller argues the gospel does three things: helps us *know* the poor (understand poverty accurately), makes us *become* poor in spirit (the prerequisite for salvation), and thereby enables us to *love* the poor without condescension. Includes Keller's economic analysis of how the materially poor lack what the world values and have their remaining resources stripped by structural injustice.

**A Community of Justice, Part 1 (2009)** — From James 2:1-17, Keller addresses partiality in the church toward the wealthy and neglect of the poor. He argues that favoritism reveals a misunderstanding of the gospel: if you believe you are accepted by grace rather than merit, you cannot evaluate others by merit. The church is to be a visible community of equal honor — a counter-cultural sign that the gospel is real.

**An Everlasting Name (2010)** — Working from Isaiah 56:1-8 and the story of the Ethiopian eunuch in Acts 8, Keller argues that the free grace of salvation (Isa 55) flows immediately into a new commitment to justice (Isa 56:1). He distinguishes *mishpat* and *tzedakah*, develops Waltke's definition of righteousness as "disadvantaging yourself to advantage the community," and shows how the gospel destroys the identity-by-superiority mechanism that drives both racial division and economic exploitation.

**A Strong Basis for Doing Justice (2014)** — Delivered as part of the "Questioning Christianity" seeker series, this sermon addresses secular objections to justice while arguing that Christianity provides uniquely strong grounds for it — stronger than secular humanism can sustain, because the gospel simultaneously humbles the doer and grounds the dignity of the recipient in the image of God and the redemptive love of Christ.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 8 sources]

Keller returns to the following passages most consistently when preaching on justice and mercy:

### Old Testament

**Deuteronomy 15:7-11** — "Be open-handed toward your poor and needy neighbor... Give generously to them and do so without a grudging heart... Give till their need is gone." Keller treats this as one of the most commanding texts in the entire Bible on the duty of care for the poor.

**Proverbs 13:23** — "A poor man's field may produce abundant food, but injustice sweeps it away." Keller's touchstone text for the structural, systemic dimension of poverty — what you have can be taken from you because you are poor and powerless.

**Proverbs 19:17** — "Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the Lord." Keller uses this to show that generosity toward the poor is not merely humanitarian — it is directed toward God himself.

**Isaiah 56:1-8** — "Maintain justice and do what is right, for my salvation is close at hand." The twin Hebrew words *mishpat* and *tzedakah* define the grammar of biblical social justice. The passage also breaks down the walls of exclusion for foreigners and eunuchs — a key text for Keller on racial reconciliation within the community of faith.

**Isaiah 58:6-10** — The Isaiah 58 definition of true fasting: loosing chains of injustice, freeing the oppressed, sharing food with the hungry, providing shelter, clothing the naked. Keller reads this as the prophetic standard against which all religious performance is measured. God rejects the worship of unjust people.

**Isaiah 61:1-2** — The Servant's inaugural proclamation: "good news to the poor, release to the captives, freedom for the oppressed." Jesus reads this in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4) and says: "Today this scripture is fulfilled in your hearing." Keller insists this cannot be spiritualized away. The Messianic kingdom is, among other things, concretely good news for the materially and socially poor.

**Job 29:12-17** — Job's description of a just life: eyes to the blind, feet to the lame, father to the needy, advocate for the immigrant, defender against the wicked. "I put on righteousness as my clothing; justice was my robe and my turban." Keller uses this as the picture of justice as a habitual orientation of life, not an occasional act.

**Amos 5:24** — "Let justice roll down like a river and righteousness like a never-failing stream." Keller references this in the tradition of Amos's polemic against religious performance disconnected from social concern.

### New Testament

**Luke 6:20-26** — Luke's version of the Beatitudes: "Blessed are you who are poor... woe to you who are rich." Keller argues this cannot be allegorized away as Matthew's "poor in spirit" sometimes is. Both are true and both demand response.

**Luke 10:25-37** — The Good Samaritan. Keller's most-used justice parable. Key elements: (1) Jesus sets the bar impossibly high — love neighbor as self — to expose self-justification; (2) the Samaritan had no obligation but was moved by *splagma*; (3) "your neighbor is anyone in your path that you have the power to help"; (4) the gospel is the only sustainable motor — you were the man in the road, Jesus was your Samaritan.

**Matthew 25:31-46** — The sheep and the goats. "I was hungry and you did not feed me." Keller does not read this as works-salvation but as the distinguishing mark of true faith: those who genuinely belong to Christ will inevitably express that in concrete acts of mercy toward the vulnerable.

**James 2:1-17** — Partiality and the royal law of love. Favoritism toward the wealthy violates the very gospel that the church exists to proclaim. Faith without deeds is dead — not a living tree but a dead one.

**James 4:13–5:6** — The "life control illusion" and its economic consequences: hoarding wealth, underpaying workers, self-indulgence. Keller connects the failure of justice with the deeper spiritual problem of treating one's resources as fully one's own rather than as a gift from God.

**1 John 3:14-18** — "If anyone has material possessions and sees a brother or sister in need but has no pity on them, how can the love of God be in that person?" Keller uses this to argue that care for the poor is not a secondary application of the gospel but a direct test of whether one has truly received it.

**Ephesians 2:11-18** — Christ has "destroyed the barrier, the dividing wall of hostility" between Jew and Gentile. Keller reads this as the foundational text for racial reconciliation: the gospel creates a "new humanity" in which the identity mechanism that drives racial division (lifting up your strengths, looking down at those who lack them) is dismantled and replaced by a shared identity as recipients of undeserved grace.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 6 sources]

### The New York City Context

Keller preached at Redeemer Presbyterian Church in Manhattan, and the city was never merely a backdrop. New York's extraordinary density of wealth and poverty — often blocks apart — gave his justice preaching a constant urgency and particularity:

- **Class anxiety and the meritocracy myth**: New Yorkers, Keller observed, are constantly looking two rungs up the social ladder, never down. They feel like victims even when they have more power than they realize. The gospel reorients this: "Look all the way up to Jesus Christ, and then look below you. You've got power in your hands."

- **Homelessness and street poverty**: Keller directly addressed the everyday ethical puzzle of panhandling. He argued against mere reactivity (throwing quarters at everyone who asks) in favor of intentional, strategic generosity — finding ministries you trust and investing in them so that your mercy is active rather than merely responsive.

- **Urban inequality and structural dynamics**: His analysis of how wealth extracts from poor neighborhoods without reinvesting — the store that profits in a poor area while the owner lives elsewhere — drew on the lived reality of every New Yorker who has watched neighborhoods deteriorate or gentrify.

- **Racial diversity at Redeemer**: Keller's sustained attention to racial reconciliation was not merely academic. He worked to build a racially diverse congregation in one of America's most racially polarized cities, and he understood that in a city like New York, a church that talked about the gospel but remained racially homogeneous was preaching a stunted version of it.

- **The Questioning Christianity series (2014)**: Keller designed "A Strong Basis for Doing Justice" for the explicitly secular New Yorker — the friend who cares about justice but suspects Christianity is indifferent to it. He argued that secular humanism cannot sustain justice long-term without the theological roots that the gospel provides.

### Secular Objections He Takes Seriously

Keller engages secularism on justice with genuine respect rather than dismissal:

**"Secular justice advocates care more than conservative Christians."** Keller takes this seriously. He acknowledges the historical failure of evangelical churches to pursue racial and economic justice, and he argues for a corrective not by relativizing the failure but by showing that the failure is a theological inconsistency — a failure to follow the implications of the gospel.

**"If you just believe in grace, you have no motivation to be good."** Keller's canonical response: "If when you lose all fear of being smacked by God, you then lose all incentive to live a good life, then the only incentive you ever had was fear. You need a better motive." Grace creates a deeper and more durable motivation than law.

**"Poverty is caused by irresponsible behavior."** Keller does not dismiss personal responsibility but challenges the assumption that it accounts for most poverty. He cites Proverbs, urban sociology, and the observation that "irresponsible behavior is largely a response to poverty rather than a cause of it" — noting that his own wife's middle-class position is partly the result of her grandfather's emigration luck, not just her hard work.

**"The church shouldn't get involved in politics."** Keller distinguishes political partisanship (which churches should avoid) from the call to justice (which is non-negotiable). He argues that the church's role is not to become a political actor but to be a visible community that embodies justice — a "counterculture of grace" that the city can look at and ask, "What makes those people so different?"

### Literature, Philosophy, and History

- **Jonathan Edwards** — Keller cites Edwards's treatise on the Christian duty to the poor as one of the clearest statements in the tradition: "nothing more clear and strong in the Bible than our duty for care and involvement with the poor."

- **Robert Murray McShane** (Scottish preacher, 1838) — Keller quotes McShane's remarkable passage on giving extensively, both for its pastoral frankness ("Enjoy your money... for I can tell you you'll be beggars throughout eternity") and for its gospel-centered reframing (Christ's blood was his own; he gave it for the undeserving).

- **Julian the Apostate** (Roman Emperor) — Keller's most striking historical illustration: the pagan emperor who tried to revive Roman religion complained that "nothing has contributed to the progress of the superstition of these Christians as their charity to strangers. The Empire's Galileans provide not only for their own poor, but for ours as well." When an opponent of the faith observes that generosity is Christianity's most powerful apologetic, that is worth heeding.

- **Malcolm Gladwell's *Outliers*** — Keller references Gladwell's argument that success depends far more on circumstances, timing, and social capital than on individual effort alone. He uses this to make the biblical case that what you have is a gift — which is the foundation of generosity. "If you had been born on a mountain in Tibet in the 13th century and you worked every bit as hard as you have in this life, I don't think you'd have much to show for it."

- **Bruce Waltke** (Old Testament scholar) — Keller frequently cites Waltke's lexical study of *tzaddik* (the just person) and *rasha* (the wicked): "The just are those willing to disadvantage themselves to advantage the community; the wicked are those willing to disadvantage the community to advantage themselves." This definition, Keller notes, makes un-generosity not merely uncharitable but actively unjust.

- **Robert Linthicum** — Keller tells Linthicum's story of Eva, a 14-year-old girl coerced into prostitution by a gang that also controlled the police — as a searing illustration of the structural dimension of poverty. Beautiful, talented, believing — and utterly unable to protect herself because she had nothing the world values and no power to resist those who took from her.

### On the Nature of Justice

> "The righteous care about justice for the poor — the Hebrew word here is *yada'*, the most deep and intimate word in the Hebrew language for knowledge. It's used for sexuality in Genesis. This proverb is saying: you're not wise unless you are living an intensely passionate life, committed to justice."
> — *Creation Care and Justice* (2005)

> "The just person is one willing to disadvantage themselves to advantage the community. The wicked person is one willing to disadvantage the community to advantage themselves. An unwillingness to share your resources, your power, your connections, your talents with people who have less is not just uncharitable. The Bible sees it as a lack of justice."
> — *An Everlasting Name* (2010), citing Bruce Waltke on Proverbs

You and I think that if you don't start a home for the blind, if you don't give a lot of your money away to the poor, well, that's up to you. Maybe you're non-charitable. But no — Job saw that as a lack of justice."
> — *An Everlasting Name* (2010)

> "If you understand that Jesus Christ said that you were poor, and Jesus poured out his riches on you, that you were the person in the road and Jesus came on by and got off and impoverished himself for you — until you see that Jesus Christ was your neighbor, you will not have the motivation to be a neighbor."
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

> "If you're a Christian who understands the gospel, you will know that even when you were dying in your blood in the road, Jesus Christ with no obligation came and poured out his riches so that you could be made whole."
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

> "When you look at a poor person, you see your own story. You see exactly how you look apart from Jesus Christ. Your righteousness is a filthy rag. If they are in a filthy rag, you just see yourself."

### On the Motivation Problem

> "If I stopped here, all I would be leaving all of you with is guilt. Lots of guilt. Tons of guilt. And if you go out from here saying, 'I'm going to have to do loving deeds,' you're going to have to do loving deeds — with that as your motivation, you will burn out so fast."
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

> "The Samaritan had no legal obligation. He had no moral obligation. He had no cultural obligation. Nobody would have expected him to stop. Why did he stop? He was moved with *splagma* — bowels of pity — the word most often used for the emotional state of Jesus Christ in the New Testament."
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

### On Poverty and Structural Injustice

> "The Bible says, by and large, the vast majority of people in absolute misery are people who come into the world and find they've got nothing. The frequent irresponsible behavior, the frequent cynicism and hopelessness — these are largely a *response* to poverty rather than a *cause* of poverty."
> — *Blessed Are the Poor* (1998)

> "The wealthy are like a fortified city, but the destruction of the poor is their poverty. To be poor is to be a city without walls."
> — *Blessed Are the Poor* (1998), from Proverbs 10:15

> "A poor man's field may produce abundant food, but injustice sweeps it away. What the poor do have is taken from them because they are poor, because they are powerless, because they cannot control their world."
> — *Blessed Are the Poor* (1998)

### On the Church's Failure and Opportunity

> "Robert Murray McShane said: 'I am concerned for the poor, but more for you. I don't know what Christ will say to you on the great day. You seem to be Christians, and yet you don't care for his poor.' That's what he's trying to say. And the reason I usually read that quote is because I don't really have the guts to say it myself."
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

> "Julian [the Apostate] wrote: 'Nothing has contributed to the progress of the superstition of these Christians as their charity to strangers. The Empire's Galileans provide not only for their own poor, but for ours as well.' If you ask Julian what gave Christianity its power, he didn't see the worship, the fellowship, any of the other things. The only thing he saw was: these people are promiscuous with their generosity. What's with these people?"
> — *Blueprint for Revival: Social Concern* (1990)

### On Race, Class, and the Gospel

> "I'm Christian first, and I'm white second. I'm Christian first, and I'm an American second. When I meet a poor single mother in Soweto who is also a believer, there is a closer tie and a more profound connection than I have with people I grew up with — same street, same school, same race, same generation. Do you understand that?"
> — *A New Humanity* (2011)

> "God made Jesus sin who knew no sin — he put him in the place of the inhumanity of man, all the things we should be destroyed for in our hostility. We've ruined this world. God should slay us for our hostility. Instead, he slew the hostility."
> — *A New Humanity* (2011)

### On Wealth and the "Life Control Illusion"

> "If you really believe that where you are today is mainly because of forces beyond your control — divine help, the gift of God — then what assets you have, you will not treat as fully yours. You'll know they're there because of a gift. And that makes you radically generous."
> — *A Community of Justice, Part 2* (2010)

> "The future is whatever you make it — so make it a good one. James says that is exactly what it means to be out of touch with reality."
> — *A Community of Justice, Part 2* (2010)
