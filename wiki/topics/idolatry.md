# Idolatry

## Summary [coverage: high — 12 sources]

Idolatry is not a relic of the ancient world. It is the fundamental disorder of every human heart in every age. Keller argues — drawing relentlessly from Jeremiah, Romans, Acts 17, and the Pauline letters — that what the Bible calls idol worship is simply what every secular person does when they place career, romance, family, status, or comfort at the center of their life and demand from it what only God can give.

The definition Keller returns to most often is this: **idolatry is making a good thing into an ultimate thing.** Art is good. Music is good. Success is good. Family is good. Romance is good. The question is never whether the object is good or bad in itself — the question is whether it has been elevated to the functional center of your identity and security. When that happens, even the most innocent thing becomes a slave-master.

Keller insists that this is not a metaphor. It is precisely what Paul describes in Romans 1:18-25, where he says that human beings "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images." The exchange is not literal stone-carving in most modern cases, but the structure is identical: we take something from the created order, place it on the throne of our hearts, and reorganize our entire lives around keeping it, acquiring it, or protecting it. Jeremiah's God puts it most starkly: "My people have committed two sins: they have forsaken me, the spring of living water, and dug their own cisterns — broken cisterns that cannot hold water" (Jer 2:13).

The broken-cistern image is central to Keller's diagnosis. The cistern doesn't leak because the water is bad. It leaks because the cistern is cracked. Every idol — no matter how wonderful — is a broken cistern. It will disappoint you if you lose it, terrify you if it's threatened, and enslave you even when you have it. The only non-broken source is God himself.

The gospel answer follows directly: only when Jesus Christ becomes your deepest treasure — when he is your meaning, your approval, your security, your beauty — do idols lose their power. Not because they are suppressed by willpower, but because they are displaced by something greater.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 10 sources]

### The Universal Diagnosis

Keller's central theological claim is that every human being is already worshiping something. There is no such thing as a neutral, un-worshiping self. The question is never *whether* you worship, but *what* you worship. Drawing from Bob Dylan's line — "you've got to serve somebody" — and from Paul's argument in Romans 1, Keller argues that the human heart is constitutionally oriented toward worship. We cannot exist without a center, without something that functions as our source of meaning, identity, and security.

When God is displaced from that center, something else fills it immediately. In the ancient world, people carved wooden idols. In the modern world, the idols are subtler and more powerful precisely because they are invisible. Career. Physical attractiveness. Romantic love. The approval of your parents. The cohesion of your family. Intellectual achievement. Political power. These are the gods of Manhattan, every bit as real as Aphrodite and Ares were to Athens.

Paul's encounter with Athens in Acts 17 is the controlling narrative for Keller's cultural analysis. Paul "theorized" — the Greek *theoreo* means to see underneath — the idols at work beneath the art, the philosophy, the commerce, and the civic life of one of the world's great cities. He didn't see idols only in the temples. He saw them everywhere, under everything. This is the capacity the gospel gives: to see the spiritual architecture beneath cultural phenomena.

### The Two Sins of Jeremiah 2

Jeremiah chapter 2 is Keller's primary Old Testament diagnosis of idolatry, and he returns to it across many sermons over three decades. The passage identifies a double failure:

1. **Forsaking God** — abandoning the spring of living water.
2. **Digging broken cisterns** — constructing our own sources of meaning that cannot hold water.

What makes this passage so devastating, Keller argues, is that the second sin is not a retreat into pure emptiness. People don't worship nothing. They go to enormous trouble constructing elaborate alternative sources of life. The idol doesn't feel like a choice for nothing — it feels like a choice for something. But every alternative to God is structurally broken. It cannot deliver the blessings it seems to promise, because only God was built to be God.

The prophet's language — "consider then and realize how evil and bitter it is for you when you forsake the Lord your God" — is the language of intervention. God is not thundering at a distant enemy. He is the concerned father, the jealous spouse, the heartbroken lover crying: *Can't you see what you're doing to yourself?*

### How Idols Enslave

Keller draws here from the biblical covenant structure, especially as mediated through Paul's letter to the Galatians. Paul tells the Galatian church that by adding law-keeping to faith, they are not becoming more religious — they are falling back under "the slavery of the non-gods" (Gal 4:8). This is the structure of every idol:

- You make a **covenant** with it: "If I achieve X, then I will have worth. If I have Y, then I will be safe."
- You **serve** it: your entire emotional and behavioral life is reorganized around keeping the covenant.
- It **blesses** you when you succeed and **curses** you when you fail — producing the characteristic emotional swings of idolatry: elation when the idol is satisfied, despair when it is threatened or lost.

This is why Keller calls sin an **addiction**. Like every addiction, idolatry is not primarily about pleasure. It is about a spiritual hunger that cannot be satisfied by anything other than God. When you feed it with an idol, the hunger temporarily quiets, but the idol's structural inability to deliver permanent satisfaction means the hunger always returns — more demanding than before.

Keller describes this dynamic using the CoDependency metaphor: a codependent person has made a covenant with a rescue-and-help idol. Their entire sense of self depends on being needed. When the person they "help" gets better, they lose their meaning rather than gaining it. The idol has turned a genuinely good impulse — caring for others — into a chain.

### The Gospel Answer

The solution is not willpower directed against idols. You cannot dislodge an ultimate thing by deciding not to want it. The only way to displace an idol is to find something more beautiful, more real, more satisfying. This is what Keller means when he says the gospel is the answer to idolatry: not as a rule to follow, but as an encounter with a person whose beauty and grace exceeds everything the idol promised.

Paul's method in Acts 17 was to "preach Jesus and the resurrection" — bringing not just an abstract truth but a person, the only person whose love is sacrificial rather than transactional, whose acceptance is not conditioned on performance, and who gives himself rather than demanding you give yourself to him.

Only the cross achieves this. The cross shows simultaneously the relentless holiness of God (who would not overlook evil even when it fell on his own Son) and the relentless love of God (who accepted that cost rather than lose us). Nothing else in any religion achieves both. And when both are burned into the heart, the idol's power begins to dissolve — not because you suppressed your desire, but because a greater desire has replaced it.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 8 texts]

Keller returns to a consistent set of passages across the entire corpus of idolatry preaching:

### Jeremiah 2 — The Broken Cisterns
The single most-cited passage in Keller's idolatry teaching. Verse 13 ("They have forsaken me, the spring of living water, and dug their own cisterns — broken cisterns that cannot hold water") provides the governing image. Verse 19 ("Consider then and realize how evil and bitter it is for you when you forsake the Lord your God") supplies the interventionist tone. Verse 11 ("Has a nation ever changed its gods? Yet they are not gods at all. But my people have exchanged their glory for worthless idols") supplies the shock of the diagnosis.

Keller uses this passage in at least four major sermons spanning 1995–1999 (Anatomy of Sin 1 & 2, How Sin Makes Us Addicts, How Sin Makes Us Vandals).

### Romans 1:18-25 — The Great Exchange
Paul's diagnosis of universal human sin as the worship of created things rather than the Creator. Keller reads this as the foundational New Testament account of how idolatry works: (1) humanity knows God, (2) refuses to honor him as God, (3) the heart becomes darkened, (4) the glory of God is exchanged for images. The word "exchanged" is crucial — idolatry is not ignorance but substitution. It is an active trade of the real for the counterfeit.

Used in The Freedom of Service and Co-Dependency: A Biblical Guide.

### Acts 17:16-34 — Paul in Athens
The narrative and methodological text. Paul's *paroxysmos* (translated "greatly distressed") when he saw Athens full of idols shows the right emotional posture: a mix of indignation and compassion, not just anger. His *theoreo* — seeing underneath the surface of culture to its idols — becomes Keller's model for Christian cultural analysis. His proclamation of "Jesus and the resurrection" in the Areopagus is the gospel answer.

Used as the primary text in A World of Idols.

### Galatians 4:8-20 — Slavery to Non-gods
Paul's shocking claim that the Galatians, by adding law to faith, would fall back under "the weak and miserable forces" (v. 9) they served before their conversion. Keller reads this as Paul saying: moralism and performance-religion are *structurally identical* to pagan idol worship. Both are forms of self-salvation. The non-gods are non-gods whether they wear a toga or a suit.

Used as the primary text in Enslaved to Non-gods.

### 1 John 5:18-21 — Dear Children, Keep Yourselves from Idols
The closing verse of John's letter — "Dear children, keep yourselves from idols" — is Keller's surprising summary of what the new birth means. It is not an afterthought but a summary: the whole letter has been building to this. To be born again is to have the ultimate allegiance of the heart moved from idols to God.

Used as the primary text in Born of God.

### Exodus 20:1-6 — The First and Second Commandments
"You shall have no other gods before me" and the prohibition of images (Exod 20:3-4) are the foundation of Keller's theological account of idolatry. The jealousy of God in verse 5 — "for I, the LORD your God, am a jealous God" — is not an expression of insecurity but of love: the jealousy of a spouse who refuses to watch their beloved destroy themselves with a false lover.

Used in Only True God: His Supremacy and The Fire of God.

### Deuteronomy 4:15-24 — The Fire of God
Moses warns Israel against making any image of God, grounded in the fact that "the LORD your God is a consuming fire, a jealous God" (v. 24). Keller uses the fire-of-God imagery to show that God's jealousy is not a character flaw but a covenantal necessity: the covenant made in love demands fidelity, and breaking it is not merely disobedience but betrayal.

### Jonah 1–4 — A Case Study in Divided Hearts
Jonah functions across multiple sermons as the primary narrative case study. A prophet of God who nonetheless has a divided heart — he serves Yahweh when it is convenient but serves his own nationalism when God's agenda conflicts with his. The storm, the whale, the withered vine, and the anger enough to die all diagnose the same idolatry: a conditional allegiance that is really no allegiance at all.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 11 sources]

### The Invisible Idols of Secular Manhattan

Keller's primary cultural move is to take the concept of idol worship, which most secular listeners dismiss as ancient superstition, and show it operating in the most sophisticated precincts of New York City life. The pantheon of modern Manhattan idols includes:

- **Career and achievement** — the deep driver of the professional class; the thing that if lost would make them feel they have no identity
- **Romantic love** — in secular society, Keller follows Ernest Becker in arguing that romance has become a "divine solution": people now load onto their partner all the hopes for transcendence, meaning, and justification that were once directed at God
- **Physical attractiveness** — particularly as an idol for women whose identity is organized around being desired; Keller narrates a real counseling case in which a woman could say "God forgiven me" but "I can't forgive myself" — because her real functional god was the pleasure of her parents, not the God of the Bible
- **Family honor** — the ethnic community's tendency to make family name an ultimate; when shame is more feared than God, the family has become an idol
- **Moral superiority** — the educated class's substitute god; Keller returns repeatedly to the elder brother in the prodigal son parable as a portrait of someone whose idol is their own righteousness
- **Approval and belonging** — codependency as a modern form of idol slavery; the person who can only feel worth when needed by someone else has made rescue itself a god

### The Apollos, Ares, Aphrodite, and Bacchus of Today

In A World of Idols, Keller playfully walks through the Greek pantheon in Athens and maps each to a modern New York equivalent:

- Aphrodite (beauty, sexual attractiveness) → the idol of those who base identity on being desired
- Ares (power, war, competition) → the idol of the ambitious, the driven, the competitive
- Apollos (art, music, culture) → the idol of the creative class who stake identity on critical acclaim
- Bacchus (wine, parties, social belonging) → the idol of those who must always be in the in-crowd

He concludes: "You couldn't be living without putting your faith in something. It's not Jesus, that's all. What is it? It's your career or it's your sexual attractiveness or it's partying, it's Bacchus or it's Ares or it's Aphrodite or it's Apollos or it's something. And all I can tell you is those gods, if you fail them, will never forgive you and if you get them, they'll never satisfy you."

### Kierkegaard, Becker, and the Secular Corroboration

Keller frequently enlists secular intellectuals to confirm the biblical diagnosis:

**Ernest Becker** (atheist social scientist, Pulitzer Prize winner): In *Denial of Death*, Becker argues that because modern secular people have largely abandoned God, they redirect their need for transcendence and meaning into romance and romance partnerships. "The love partner becomes the divine ideal within which to fulfill one's life. All spiritual and moral needs, which used to be focused on God, now become focused on the individual. But the failure of romantic love as a solution to human problems is a big part of modern humanity's frustration. No human relationship can bear the burden of Godhood." Keller uses Becker to show that this is not a Christian diagnosis but an atheist intellectual's diagnosis, which only validates the biblical account.

In *Anatomy of Sin 1*, Keller notes that Becker's *Escape from Evil* — published posthumously — represented a complete reversal from his earlier *The Structure of Evil*. Where he once blamed evil on oppressive social structures, by the end of his life he wrote: "I am now in this book looking at humanity full in the face for the first time. In my previous works I had failed to see how truly vicious human behavior is." This movement from denial to recognition mirrors the biblical call to "consider and realize" in Jeremiah 2:19.

**Søren Kierkegaard**: In *The Sickness Unto Death*, Kierkegaard defines sin as "building your identity on anything but God." Keller uses this as a secular philosophical corroboration of the biblical definition — the problem is not any particular misdeed but a structural misalignment of the self.

### Great Expectations: Idols Beget Idols

In A World of Idols, Keller reads Dickens' *Great Expectations* (the 1946 film version) as a case study in cascading idolatry: "Here's Mrs. Havisham and she is miserable. Why? Because she's made an idol out of marriage. And here's Pip and he's miserable. Why? Because he made an idol out of Estella. And Estella is miserable. Why? Because she's made an idol out of her emotional independence. She will not give her heart to anybody. And why has she got that as an idol? Because Mrs. Havisham raised her up that way because she hates men because of her idol of marriage to wreak vengeance on the male gender... Idols beget idols beget idols."

### The Codependency Movement and Idol Slavery

In the CoDependency: A Biblical Guide evening series, Keller engages the popular 1980s-90s codependency literature to show that its accurate description of certain patterns of bondage actually points toward a biblical diagnosis. The codependency literature could describe the patterns but not adequately explain or cure them because it missed the root: "A codependent is someone who has made a covenant with an idol." The cure is not self-improvement or learning to love yourself more — it is the displacement of the idol by a greater love.

### The Bob Dylan Observation

Keller frequently invokes Bob Dylan's lyric "you've got to serve somebody" as a secular statement of the biblical truth that no one is truly autonomous. Everyone is functionally worshiping something. The only question is whether the object of worship is capable of delivering what you have asked it to deliver.
