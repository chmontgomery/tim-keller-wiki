# Sin

## Summary [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Tim Keller's teaching on sin is one of the most sustained and developed threads in his entire preaching ministry. From his 1992 series at Redeemer's earliest days through the "Anatomy of Sin" series (1995), the "Faces of Sin" series, the "How Sin Makes Us..." series, and individual sermons drawn from Genesis, Jeremiah, Isaiah, the Psalms, and Paul's letters, Keller returns again and again to what he considers the central diagnosis of the human condition — and the one insight that makes sense of everything else.

His approach is deliberately counterintuitive. In a secular, therapeutic culture that has "airbrushed the word sin out" of public discourse (as he observed in *Alive With Christ Part 1*, noting the Wall Street Journal's report on how mega-churches dropped the language), Keller argues that sin is not a primitive or pessimistic category but the only intellectually honest one. Without sin, human history — its genocides, its broken marriages, its political corruption, its existential despair — remains inexplicable. The biblical doctrine of sin is not the bad news that makes people feel terrible; it is the indispensable prerequisite for understanding the gospel. You cannot be saved unless you know you are in peril.

Keller's definition of sin moves on multiple levels simultaneously. At the most surface level, sins are violations of God's law. But Keller insists this is 10% of the picture. The deeper reality is that sin is a **disposition** — what Dorothy Sayers called "a deep interior dislocation of the soul" — in which the self, which was made to center on God, curves inward on itself. This is Augustine's *incurvatus in se* (the soul curved in on itself), and Keller returns to it constantly. Sin is not primarily about bad deeds but about a fundamental misdirection of the whole person: the demand of the heart that everything, including God, revolve around me.

From this root, Keller develops a three-part taxonomy of how sin operates:

1. **Sin as idolatry** — We replace God with "lover gods," good things (achievement, relationships, approval, comfort, power) that we have elevated into ultimate things. These idols enslave us and leave us empty.

2. **Sin as self-deception** — Sin always puts out a "force field of denial." It crouches and hides, presenting itself either as a virtue or as something minor. The most dangerous sins are the ones we don't think are that bad.

3. **Sin as addiction** — Sin begins as a choice and ends as a power. Once we give ourselves to the craving — to the idol, to the inordinate desire — we find that sin "does us" rather than the other way around. The biblical image of an animal in heat who "cannot be restrained" (Jer 2:24) is Keller's vivid icon for this slavery.

Crucially, Keller refuses moralism. The problem is not simply bad behavior that needs correcting; it is a disordered heart that needs regenerating. The gospel answer is not "try harder" but "be born again" — a radical renovation from outside, God making dead souls alive by grace.

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Keller's central theological claim about sin can be stated in two movements — the diagnosis and the answer.

**The Diagnosis: A Dislocated Soul Without Awe**

The hinge text for Keller's doctrine of sin is Jeremiah 2:19: *"Consider then and realize how evil and bitter it is for you when you forsake the Lord your God and have no awe of me."* God addresses Israel using what Keller calls "the language of intervention" — *consider, realize, don't you see?* — the language you use with a deluded loved one who cannot perceive the destruction their habit is causing. This is not the language of condemnation but of pleading.

The diagnosis has three layers:

**First, sin is denial.** The root of human suffering is not that people are sinners — it is that sinners will not admit they are sinners. "It is not fatal to be an alcoholic. It is fatal to be an alcoholic in denial." Sin puts out a force field that keeps the self from seeing itself clearly. Keller marshals historical witnesses: H.G. Wells and W.H. Auden began life confident in human progress and ended their lives confessing they had been wrong; Beatrice Webb wrote in 1890 that she had "staked everything on the essential goodness of human nature" and 35 years later wrote that she had come to see "how permanent are the evil impulses and instincts in us that mere social machinery will never change." Ernest Becker won the Pulitzer for *The Structure of Evil*, arguing that social science could solve poverty and war; his posthumous final book confessed he had "failed to see how truly vicious human behavior is" and called for some "third alternative beyond bureaucratic science or despair." These thinkers had to be pulled kicking and screaming to conclusions the Bible offered at the start.

**Second, the essence of sin is the absence of awe before God.** God's diagnosis in Jeremiah is not merely that Israel broke rules; it is that they had "no awe of me" (the Hebrew word *yir'ah*, the fear of the Lord). Keller distinguishes two kinds of fear: a negative fear that obsesses and paralyzes, and the positive "fear of the Lord" that liberates — not by filling you with dread, but by filling you with wonder. In positive fear, God becomes the center around which everything revolves; you can do nothing without reference to his glory. The absence of this awe is the root cause of every personal, intellectual, and moral failure. "When you worry, you are in more awe of the power of some factor than of the power of God. When you're guilty, you're in more awe of the power of your sin than the magnitude of his grace."

**Third, sin is irrational.** The image God uses in Jeremiah 2:13 is devastating in its logic: "My people have committed two sins — they have forsaken me, the spring of living water, and have dug their own cisterns, broken cisterns that cannot hold water." Who does that? Who throws away an inexhaustible spring to work at digging holes in the ground that will be empty by evening? Sin is not just morally wrong — it is stupid. It is against one's own interest. The idol gods leave their worshippers enslaved and empty, but their worshippers keep going after them anyway. This is why God says: "Consider and realize." It should be self-evident. You're not thinking.

**The Answer: The Gospel Confronts the Anatomy of Sin**

Because the problem is not a bad record but a bad nature, the solution is not improvement but regeneration. Because the problem is misdirected awe (worshipping created things rather than the Creator), the solution is not moral effort but a renewed vision of God's beauty and grace. Because the idols despise us and seek our life, the solution is a God who gives his life for us.

Keller's signature move is to show that Jesus is the only god who doesn't demand performance. "Every other god says, do this and you will live. Fail and you will die. We have the only God who says: fail and I will die for you." The lover gods — achievement, relationships, power, beauty — will never say "well done, good and faithful servant." Only Jesus says that, and says it not on the basis of performance but on the basis of grace. This is why the gospel is not just the solution to sin but the only power that can actually dissolve it: not fear (which produces moralism and temporary external change) but love — the love of a God who was stripped naked so we could be clothed, who was cast out so we could be welcomed in.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

Across 168 sermons tagged to sin topics, the most-preached books are Genesis (92 sermons), Romans (90), Luke (51), Psalms (50), Ephesians (50), Isaiah (46), John (42), Matthew (42), and Galatians (30). The passages listed below are those Keller returns to most consistently when teaching directly on sin:

**Jeremiah 2:13** — The broken cisterns image. The most concentrated single verse in Keller's sin preaching. Israel has committed two sins: forsaking the living spring and digging broken cisterns. Keller uses this to demonstrate sin's irrationality, self-defeat, and the presence of better alternatives.

**Jeremiah 2:19** — *"How evil and bitter it is when you forsake the Lord your God and have no awe of me."* The root definition: sin is the absence of God-centered awe, the refusal to let God be the center around which everything orbits.

**Jeremiah 2:23–25** — The animal-in-heat imagery. The progression from "I am not defiled" (denial) to "It's no use, I must go after them" (despair at one's own enslavement). Sin's enslaving power exposed.

**Genesis 3** — The Fall narrative. Three images dominate Keller's reading: (1) *the reach* — the ongoing human reach for the tree of life even after losing God, the cosmic disillusionment underlying all earthly longing; (2) *the covering* — the fig leaves of self-justification, the Sartrean nakedness of knowing something is wrong; (3) *the sword* — the justice that must be satisfied, which points toward the cross.

**Genesis 4:1–16** — Cain and Abel. The first case study of sin outside the Garden. Sin's secrecy: it crouches, hides, presents itself as a virtue or a minor flaw, then uncoils. Cain's identity built on being better than Abel; when God favors Abel, Cain's entire self-construction collapses, and violence follows.

**Ephesians 2:1–3** — Dead in trespasses and sins. The medical severity of the diagnosis: not sick, not sinking, but *dead*. Spiritual death parallels physical death: no sensation of spiritual reality, no strength to seek God, no capacity to respond to spiritual truth.

**Isaiah 64:6** — *"All our righteous acts are like filthy rags."* Keller's key proof text for the deep sinfulness of even our good deeds: we do good things out of fear (I don't want to get caught) and pride (I'm not like those people), which are the very same roots from which all evil grows. Common virtue is rotten at the root.

**Romans 1:21–25** — The exchange: refusing to glorify God, worshipping and serving created things rather than the Creator. Paul's universal diagnosis of idolatry as the engine of all sin.

**Psalm 51:1–4** — David's repentance after adultery and murder. The fourfold structure: seeing sin in God's sight (not one's own), confessing without excuse, mourning its offense against God (not just its consequences), and hating it as God hates it.

**Romans 7:19** — *"The good that I would, I do not; and that which I would not, I do."* Paul's testimony to the power of sin even in the regenerate. Keller: "Are you more compassionate than Paul? You should know the power of sin in your life."

**Luke 15** — The Two Sons. Both younger brother (open rebellion, squandering grace) and elder brother (Pharisaic self-righteousness, conditional love, joylessness) are sinners. Keller returns to this repeatedly: the elder brother is Keller's paradigm for religious sin.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

Keller is a master at making "sin" — a word his culture finds "narrow and shallow" — comprehensible to secular New Yorkers through multiple points of cultural entry.

### The Secular Admission of Evil

Keller consistently notes that secular culture has been forced, despite itself, to rediscover the reality of human evil. The optimism of 19th-century humanism predicted that education, legislation, and social reform would eliminate poverty and war; the 20th century's genocides, gulags, and world wars exploded that faith. He quotes H.G. Wells, Beatrice Webb, and Ernest Becker as intellectuals who started with confidence in human goodness and ended in admission that something much darker is at work. The great irony: secular thinkers arrive, kicking and screaming, at the place the Bible offered at the start. "You'll never find thinkers who start out saying 'people are scumbags' and end up saying 'now I realize how wonderful people are.' It's always the other way."

### Hannibal Lecter and the Modern Dilemma

In *East of Eden* (Nov 2000), Keller reads the scene from *Silence of the Lambs* where Agent Starling asks Lecter, "What happened to you?" Lecter's response — "Nothing happened to me, Officer Starling. *I* happened. Can you stand to say I am evil?" — is Keller's sharpest cultural hook. The modern worldview, committed to explaining evil through external causes (upbringing, trauma, social structures), has no category for irreducible wickedness. Lecter "slam dunks the modern worldview." Genesis does not. Genesis has always had categories for Lecter.

### The NYC Overworker and the Fig Leaf

Keller's Manhattan context shapes his application constantly. Why do people move to New York and work 80-hour weeks? Why can't some people bear the look of disappointment on another's face? Why does pornography exist? Why do people desperately hold onto their youth? Keller's answer is always the same: these are fig leaves. We have a deep, ineradicable sense of inadequacy (what Genesis calls nakedness) and we spend our lives either covering ourselves (perfectionism, achievement, moralism, beauty) or uncovering others (gossip, judgment, class prejudice) to feel temporarily better. The entire economy of social life in a city like New York can be read, Keller argues, through the lens of Genesis 3's covering and uncovering.

### Sartre and the Keyhole

In *East of Eden*, Keller deploys Jean-Paul Sartre's famous analysis in *Being and Nothingness* ("The Look") — the man at the keyhole who is horrified to discover someone is watching him watch. Sartre uses this to argue against God: there's nothing more dehumanizing than being the object of an unviewed viewer. Keller's counter: Sartre's argument collapses because he is doing to the person in the room precisely what he refuses to let God do to him. "Nakedness is a deep sense that there's something wrong with me that I can't keep the eyes of anyone else out." The doctrine of original sin explains Sartre's anxiety better than Sartre can.

### Fatal Attraction and Lover Gods

Keller uses Michael Douglas's *Fatal Attraction* as an image for idolatry. A fatal attraction is when someone becomes so obsessed with an object — a person, a goal, a substance — that they will do anything to have it. Keller's claim: every human being has at least one fatal attraction. Every human being has "lover gods" (Jer 2) — something about which they say "I must have this, I cannot receive life joyfully without it." These are not exotic pathologies but ordinary idols: achievement, approval, financial security, sexual satisfaction, the admiration of one's peer group, one's children's success. "Anything that you cannot part with at need is your ruler" (George MacDonald).

### Jonathan Edwards on Common Virtue vs. True Virtue

In *A Promise of Hope*, Keller deploys Edwards's distinction from *The Nature of True Virtue*. Common virtue — doing good out of fear (I don't want to get caught) or pride (I'm not like those people) — is still sin at the root. The same motivations (fear and pride) that produce good behavior also produce all evil. This means that people who consider themselves moral and decent are "nurturing sin" beneath their goodness. Their virtue is external; the heart is unchanged. Only true virtue — doing right out of love for God's beauty and grace — reaches the root. This is why Jesus' ethical demands are so severe: not to make us feel terrible, but to show us that we have a bad *nature*, not just a bad *record*, and that we need regeneration, not just improvement.

### Irving Berlin and the Achievement God

In *Anatomy of Sin, Part 2*, Keller cites a book review of Irving Berlin's life: his daughter said that no matter how much he achieved, Berlin would immediately fall into discouragement — "I'll never be able to write a song like that again." The achievement idol never says "well done." Neither does the thinness idol (supermodels who hate the bump on their shoulder blade), the parenting idol (teenage girls who have babies to "have somebody to love"), or the morality idol (people who say "I know God forgives me, but I can't forgive myself" — which means they have a god higher than God). The pattern is universal.

### Miroslav Volf and the Necessity of Wrath

In *How Sin Makes Us Convicts*, Keller makes the politically counterintuitive argument (drawing on Croatian theologian Miroslav Volf's *Exclusion and Embrace*) that the practice of nonviolence requires a belief in divine vengeance. "If you don't believe in a God who settles accounts, you will either be sucked into the cycle of payback yourself, or you will die in despair." The angry God is not the primitive, vengeful deity of stereotypes; God's anger is "a settled, fixed, implacable, incorruptible opposition to injustice and evil" — the only possible foundation for mercy and forgiveness. You need an angry God to hope, to be humble, and to know how loved you are: the greater the wrath poured out on the Son, the greater the love demonstrated to you.

### C.S. Lewis on the Longings That Nothing Satisfies

Keller regularly draws on Lewis's chapter on Hope in *Mere Christianity* and *The Problem of Pain*: "If you really understood your own heart, you will eventually come to know that you want something most acutely that cannot be had in this world." The cosmic disillusionment we feel when even the best marriage, the best job, the best travel cannot fully satisfy is not psychological — it is cosmic. It is the reach of Genesis 3:22: we have lost God, but we cannot forget what we were made for. We are, in Voltaire's phrase, "tormented atoms in a bed of mud" — atoms who hate the idea that they are only atoms. Sin explains this better than secularism can.

### Dostoevsky

Keller alludes to Dostoevsky in his preaching on sin, particularly on the theme of self-deception and the interiority of moral corruption. Characters like Raskolnikov in *Crime and Punishment* and the Grand Inquisitor in *The Brothers Karamazov* are used to illustrate how sophisticated intellectual self-justification can overlay deep moral disorder — and how confession and suffering are the only paths to restoration.

## Representative Quotes [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

### On Denial as the Essence of Sin

> "It is not fatal to be an alcoholic. What is fatal is to be an alcoholic in denial. And we also know that there's something about the very nature of alcoholism that puts out a force field of denial. In many ways that's just a small metaphor, a dim reflection of what God is talking about here. God is saying: the root problem of our society and of humanity is that we will not admit that we're sinners."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 1* (Jan 1995)

> "Sin is the shadow, the real shadow. By its nature, sin entails denial, and you will not see the extent and the magnitude. 'I err, I'm flawed' — that's not to admit you're a helpless sinner. That's not to admit that, as Dorothy Sayers says, there's a radical interior dislocation in the heart."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 1* (Jan 1995)

### On Sin as Loss of Awe

> "What does fear mean? In positive fear, you're blessedly free from thinking about yourself. In negative fear, you're paralyzed. But here's what they have in common: the fear of the Lord means He is absolutely central. You can do nothing without reference to Him. No matter what you do, you say, how does this relate to God? Now, God says that's the essence of sin. Whenever you sin in a particular way, at that point you are holding something in more awe than you are holding God in."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 1* (Jan 1995)

### On Idolatry as Fatal Attraction

> "A fatal attraction is when a man or a woman gets so obsessed with someone that you'll do anything — you'll lie, you'll steal, you'll kill, anything. And God says that's not true of only some people. Every human being has a fatal attraction to something. Every human being has lover gods. Every human being has a bottom line. Whatever you say 'I must have that' about — that's your lover. That's your God."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 2* (Jan 1995)

> "Anything that you cannot part with at need is your ruler." (quoting George MacDonald)
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 2* (Jan 1995)

### On the Broken Cisterns

> "Who does that? Who throws away a living spring to dig holes in the ground that will be empty by evening? God says, consider and realize how evil and bitter it is for you. Sin is irrational. It's not the economy, stupid. It's sin."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 1* (Jan 1995)

### On Sin Crouching at the Door

> "Sin always presents itself to you as either a virtue — 'I'm not a workaholic, I'm just productive' — or else as something minor: 'Yes, I probably shouldn't nurse that grudge, but it's not all that bad.' It's crouching down, always staying just off your radar. But he says: right now, you still have some control. When you give in — to the selfishness, to the prejudice, to the pride — it's not long before it will do you. By definition, the things in your life that can most destroy you are the things you don't think are that bad."
> — *East of Eden: Sin and Grace* (Nov 2000)

### On Spiritual Death

> "The Bible doesn't say you're sick in your sins. You're dead in your sins. It's not that you're sinking — you're sunk. Not drowning — drowned. Not calling out to Jesus from the surface, but at the bottom of the sea, not even knowing he's there, not even calling, because you're dead."
> — *Dead in Sin* (Eph 2 series)

### On the Flesh as Inordinate Desire

> "The flesh is not the body. The flesh is the bent of the entire person to be God — to want to be your own King and your own Savior. And therefore, the flesh takes a good desire — for food, for sex, for competence, for love, for power — and turns it into a strategy for life independent of God. The minute it becomes the thing you must have, the thing that would make life worth living, it's enslaved you."
> — *Alive With Christ, Part 1* (Nov 1992)

### On Idols Never Forgiving

> "If you cannot forgive yourself, it's because you've got a God higher than God. And no God but God will ever forgive you. Idols never forgive. Never. They despise you. They seek your life."
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 2* (Jan 1995)

### On Covering and Nakedness

> "You can understand all of your life doing two things. You're going to spend all of your life trying to uncover other people to get power over them. Because you know that if I can catch people being themselves, I'll get power over them — I know they're not what they purport to be. Meanwhile, I've got to cover myself. These are fig leaves. Your perfectionism is a fig leaf. Your work is a fig leaf. Your makeup is a fig leaf. Your holding onto your youth is a fig leaf."
> — *East of Eden* (Nov 2000)

### On the Gospel as the Only Cure

> "Every other god says, do this and you will live. Fail and you will die. We have the only God who says: fail and I will die for you. There is no other religion that even claims to have a God like this. He was stripped so you could be clothed. He was naked in the dark so you could be clothed in the light. He was disfigured so you could be beautiful. He was cast out so the Father could say, 'Well done, good and faithful servant.'"
> — *Anatomy of Sin, Part 2* (Jan 1995)

### On Sin and the Human Condition

> "Voltaire called us tormented atoms in a bed of mud. And here's what he meant: from what we can tell, this life is all there is, which means we're atoms. But here's how we're different — we're tormented atoms. Because we hate the idea that we're only atoms. We can't stand it. Why? Where did that come from? We know there's a tree. We know there's a garden. We know we've lost."
> — *East of Eden* (Nov 2000)

### On Common Virtue Being Rotten at the Root

> "Most people's goodness is rotten at the root. The reason that most people are virtuous on the surface is fear ('I don't want to be caught') and pride ('I'm not like those people'). But what is the cause of any lie at all? Fear and pride. So most people are actually, through their virtuous life, nurturing the very thing that produces sin — and sooner or later it blows up."
> — *A Promise of Hope* (Jul 1998), drawing on Jonathan Edwards

### On Why You Need an Angry God

> "The practice of nonviolence requires a belief in divine vengeance. In a scorched land soaked in the blood of the innocent, the idea that God refuses to take the sword will die like other pleasant captivities of a liberal mind. If God were not angry at injustice and did not take final end to violence, that God would not be worthy of worship."
> — *How Sin Makes Us Convicts*, drawing on Miroslav Volf
