# The Bible

## Summary [coverage: high — 12+ sources]

Tim Keller returns to the question of Scripture again and again across three decades of preaching — not as a doctrinal footnote, but as the animating nerve of his entire ministry. His teaching on the Bible holds together two things that many people instinctively separate: a rigorous, high view of the Bible's divine authority and an insistence that the right view of Scripture is not enough. You can believe every word of the Bible is God-breathed and still be "as deaf to what God is saying as if you didn't believe it at all."

His central conviction is that the Bible is the living word of a God who speaks — not a record of human beings reaching upward toward God, but God reaching downward to us. When Jesus argues with the Pharisees in John 5, he is not quarreling with their doctrine of Scripture. Their doctrine is essentially correct. What he quarrels with is their *use* of Scripture: they study it to possess eternal life as a spiritual achievement, rather than following its witness to him. The Bible, Keller insists, is not an end in itself. It is a means — the great witness pointing beyond itself to Christ, exactly as John the Baptist pointed beyond himself and refused the title of Messiah.

This christological reading of the whole Bible is perhaps Keller's most characteristic contribution. Every story, every law, every poem in Scripture is, in his reading, a form of John the Baptist: "I am not the one. Look at him." Joseph sold into slavery and raised to the right hand of the throne is the true Joseph — Jesus. David facing Goliath is the true David — Jesus, who went out against the ultimate giant not at the risk of his life but at the cost of it, and whose victory is imputed to all his people who did not raise a stone. The golden rule in Leviticus 19, the Psalms, the Ten Commandments, the sacrificial system — all of it is testimony, all of it is John the Baptist, all of it is confessing: I am not the one.

When Keller addresses secular New Yorkers who find parts of the Bible offensive or primitive, he does not soften the Bible's claims. He points out that if the Bible really is the word of a God who transcends every culture, it would have to offend every culture at some point. He also challenges the assumption that modern readers occupy a neutral, universal standpoint: their preferences are as culturally conditioned as any other era's. The real question is not whether the Bible offends you but whether Jesus Christ rose from the dead. If he did, you are committed to the same authority over Scripture that he practiced — the gegraptai ("it is written") that governed every moment of his life, right up to his last words on the cross.

Keller also develops a sustained account of how the Bible actually changes people. Drawing on James 1, he traces four movements: looking *intently* into the text (rational engagement), letting it show you who you are (the personal and convicting), persevering in practice (the volitional), and arriving at freedom — the freedom of the fish in water, the owner's manual rightly used. None of this works, he insists, unless the reader sees not just himself in the mirror of the law, but the one who perfectly fulfilled the law. The gospel is the hinge. Without it, the Bible is either a crushing moralism or a comfortable idol. With it, the Bible becomes — in Luther's phrase that Keller loves to cite — "greater than heaven and earth, yea, greater than death and hell."

## Core Argument [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

Keller's diagnosis: Human beings approach the Bible in one of two destructive ways. The first is the *Pharisee's error* — treating Scripture as a spiritual performance metric, studying it to accumulate righteousness before God, using it to feel superior rather than to meet the living Christ. The second is the *liberal error* — treating Scripture as a human production, sorting through it for acceptable ideas while sitting in judgment over the parts that offend. Both errors, Keller argues, leave the reader functionally deaf to what God is saying.

**The human condition:** We are not fundamentally ignorant people who need more information. We are disordered people whose loves are mis-ordered, and the Bible is a surgical instrument — a "two-edged sword" (Heb 4:12) — designed to expose and heal what no other book can reach. The problem is that we instinctively pick up Scripture as a mirror in which to admire ourselves rather than as a mirror in which to see ourselves truly. We select the verses we like and dismiss the ones that challenge our autonomy. The result is a god we have constructed, a Jesus who cannot contradict us and therefore cannot help us.

**The gospel answer:** Jesus Christ is both the subject and the key to all of Scripture. He is the one to whom every law, every story, every prophet, and every psalm points. When you read the Bible without this christological center, you are reading it "for information, not for formation" — accumulating facts about a book rather than meeting the person to whom the book testifies. The transforming power of Scripture is released only when the reader sees, through every passage, the one who came down to live the life we should have lived and die the death we should have died. Only then does the perfect law become a delight rather than a despair.

**On authority:** Keller holds a high view of Scripture — inerrancy, infallibility, plenary inspiration — but he does not argue for it primarily on the basis of philosophical proofs. He argues from Jesus: if you want Jesus, you must take his view of Scripture. Jesus lived entirely by gegraptai. He used Scripture to resist temptation in the desert, to correct the religious establishment, to interpret his own suffering, and to cry out from the cross (quoting Psalm 22). You cannot want the real Jesus and dismiss the Bible he staked his life on. The authority of Scripture is not a doctrine imposed on top of the gospel; it is internally bound to it.

**On the Bible as story, not instruction manual:** Against both fundamentalist and liberal misreadings, Keller insists that the Bible is primarily a *story* with instruction in it, not an instruction manual with illustrative stories. All other religions are essentially instructions sprinkled with stories to illustrate. Christianity is a true story — creation, fall, redemption, restoration — that carries moral instruction within it. When you read the Bible as instruction, the hero of every story becomes you. When you read it as story, the hero of every story is Christ, and you become someone who can actually change because you have been met and loved rather than merely informed and commanded.

## Scripture Texts [coverage: high — 10+ sources]

These are the passages Keller returns to most frequently when teaching on the nature and use of Scripture:

**John 5:39–40** — *"You diligently study the scriptures because you think that by them you possess eternal life. These are the scriptures that testify about me, yet you refuse to come to me to have life."* Keller's single most-quoted verse on this topic. The Pharisees' error in miniature.

**2 Peter 1:19–21** — *"We have the word of the prophets made more certain... prophecy never came about by the prophet's own interpretation... but prophets spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."* Supports the claim that Scripture is divine testimony, not human opinion — and that it is more sure than even a vision or a voice from heaven.

**Hebrews 4:12** — *"The word of God is alive and active, sharper than any double-edged sword, penetrating even to the dividing of soul and spirit."* Keller uses this to distinguish the Bible from any other body of human words — it is seed, not stone; it has life-giving power that no other book possesses.

**James 1:18–27** — *"He chose to give us birth through the word of truth."* The new birth happens through the word. The word planted in us is meant to be received (rationally), to mirror us to ourselves (personally), and to free us (liberally).

**1 Peter 1:23–25** — *"You have been born again... through the living and enduring word of God... the word of the Lord stands forever."* The imperishable seed metaphor: the word of God has a life-giving power that no other book, system, or religion can match.

**Psalm 1:1–3** — *"His delight is in the law of the Lord, and on his law he meditates day and night. He is like a tree planted by streams of water."* The gateway to the Psalter; for Keller, a text about meditation as the bridge to prayer and the key to stability amidst circumstance.

**Psalm 19:7–11** — *"The law of the Lord is perfect, refreshing the soul... sweeter than honey from the honeycomb."* Used in multiple sermons to describe the Bible as the only book that fully understands us, that moves with us rather than staying fixed.

**Luke 24:27** — *"Beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself."* Jesus on the road to Emmaus, establishing that the whole Old Testament is christologically structured.

**Galatians 1:8** — *"Even if an angel from heaven should preach a gospel other than the one we preached to you, let them be under God's curse."* Used to show that Scripture sits in judgment over visions, voices, and feelings — not the other way around.

**Matthew 5:17–20** / Sermon on the Mount — Jesus's meditation on the Ten Commandments, showing that the law, rightly understood, exposes our radical need for a savior rather than confirming our moral adequacy.

## Cultural Hooks [coverage: high — 8+ sources]

### The Sophisticated Skeptic's Objection

Keller addresses New Yorkers who say: *"I like some parts of the Bible, but there are parts that offend me — I can't accept a book that teaches X."* His response is two-pronged. First, he notes that almost every "offensive" text is being misread: people think the Bible teaches polygamy because Abraham had two wives, but the Bible is not commending everything it records. Second, and more importantly, he points out that every person's preferences are culturally located. A contemporary New Yorker finds the Bible's teaching on sex regressive and its teaching on forgiveness beautiful — but that split is not universal wisdom, it is the particular moral grammar of a specific time and place. One hundred years from now, your great-grandchildren will find many of your "enlightened" views outrageous. If the Bible is the word of God, it would have to critique every culture, including yours. The offense is a sign of authenticity, not a disqualification.

### "The Bible Was Written by Men"

Keller does not deny this. Moses wrote it. David wrote it. Luke wrote it. But he draws on Jesus's own argument in John 5 and the language of 2 Peter 1: what Moses wrote was nevertheless the Father's testimony. The question is not whether human hands were involved but whether those humans were carried along by the Holy Spirit. He compares the alternative — the Bible as a human record of spiritual search — to the distinction between God reaching down to us and us reaching up to God. Every religion that treats its scriptures as human wisdom is, at bottom, a religion of human self-improvement. Christianity says God spoke.

### Conversation vs. Monologue

In "Bible: End or Means?" Keller uses a *Star Trek* illustration: Harry Mudd on a planet of robots who always say "yes, dear." Mudd can turn off any robot who says something he doesn't want to hear. But Keller observes that there is no intimacy, no conversation, no relationship on that planet — only an echo chamber. When people say they want a "personal relationship with God" while reserving the right to dismiss any part of Scripture that offends them, they are doing the same thing. They have built a God they can turn off. "If you don't let God have the authority over you to tell you the bad things, how will he ever heal you?"

### The Modern Epistemological Shift

Keller engages directly with the Enlightenment's repositioning of religious truth from the "objective" category (like protein — true regardless of whether you believe it) to the "subjective" category (like Baroque music — true only for those who prefer it). He traces this shift as an arbitrary historical decision, not a philosophical necessity, and argues that Jesus and the early church treated religious truth as the most objective truth there is — more certain than scientific truth, which is always provisional and revisable.

### Eastern vs. Christian Meditation

Preaching on Psalm 1, Keller distinguishes biblical meditation from Transcendental Meditation. Eastern meditation tries to empty the mind — to block rationality and achieve pure intuition. Biblical meditation does the opposite: it becomes "furiously rational" about a specific text and then tries to inflame the intuitions with what has been carefully understood. The object of biblical meditation is always Scripture; the goal is not to dissolve the self but to make the word flesh — to connect what you know in your head to everything "within you."

### The Unbearable Lightness of Being

Keller cites Milan Kundera's novel (and title) as the secular horizon against which Psalm 1's metaphors of tree and chaff become urgent. If there is no God, then everything is chaff — literally. The sun will die, the earth will rot, and nothing anyone has ever done or loved will survive. Against this, the person who has put their roots into the living word of a living God has access to "underground streams" — nourishment that is there "no matter the season, no matter whether it's drought or flood." The stability is not from circumstance; it is from a source that outlasts matter.

### NYC Career Anxiety and the Misuse of Scripture

Keller consistently observes that many New Yorkers who are not religious still have an implicit scripture — a set of authoritative texts (career success manuals, self-help books, therapeutic frameworks) that tell them how to live. These texts have the form of wisdom literature without the content of grace. They can inform but not transform; they can describe the human condition but cannot heal it. The Bible, by contrast, is not primarily instruction but story — and it is a story whose hero has done for us what no self-help system can do.

That's not how you reason. Here's how you reason. Is Jesus the Son of God? Did Jesus Christ rise from the dead? Then you have to submit to the full authority of the Word of God because even he did."

> "Freedom is not the absence of restrictions. Freedom defined positively is the ability to fulfill what you're built to do, fulfill your true nature... The Bible is the owner's manual."

> "There was only one person who ever really did the whole word of God. And only when you look into the perfect law and see the only one who perfectly kept the law — will the word of God not be an everlasting despair."

> "All other religions are basically instructions sprinkled with stories to illustrate. But the Bible, Christianity, is basically a story sprinkled with instruction. And when you see the difference, the word has begun to penetrate."

> "Jesus is the true and better Adam who also faced a test in the garden, but he didn't fail it and drag us down, but he succeeded and brought us up. He's the true and better Abel, the innocent, slain one whose blood cries out, but not for condemnation as it did against Cain, but for grace and acquittal."

> "Until you see every story as really telling one story, unless you see every single story as pointing to Jesus, the Bible will be a crushing weight that might coerce you and might reform you, but it will not be a transforming, joy-inflicting power."

From **"Blueprint for Revival: Truth"** (John 17, Aug 1990):

> "A man's word is a little sound that flies into the air and is gone in a moment. The word of God is greater than heaven and earth, yea, greater than death and hell, for it forms part of the power of God and endures everlastingly." *(Luther, quoted by Keller)*

> "The word of God is not just something that you've got to study because you believe Jesus is the Son of God and now you've got to do whatever he says. But rather, the word of God is alive and active. It's come in and become part of you... You will find that you will hunger and thirst for this thing and it will just have a wonderful ministry in your life."

From **"Bridge to Prayer"** (Psalm 1, Apr 2008):

> "There is no god, then everything is chaff — literally. The sun is going to die and the world is going to rot... The unbearable lightness of being is what it means to actually think these things through without blinking."

> "The law of God, instead of being an everlasting despair — which it will be apart from Jesus Christ — is a delight. And there it is."
