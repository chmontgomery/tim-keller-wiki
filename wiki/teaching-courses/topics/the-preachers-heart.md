# The Preacher's Heart — Spiritual Reality and Communion with God

## Summary [coverage: high — 2 sources]

For Keller, the goal of every sermon is worship — leading hearers, on the spot, to adore Christ. He distinguishes this from the more common aims of edification (training Christians) and evangelism (winning non-Christians), arguing that if you aim at worship you will accomplish both, but if you aim only at edification or evangelism you will lose one audience or the other. The way you change a person's life is to change what they worship — what their heart finds most beautiful, most "delectable." The sermon is not information transfer; it is the public lifting up of Christ so that affections move.

But this work is impossible if the preacher has not been there first. Keller's central claim, drawn out across Sessions 16 and 18, is that there is "probably nothing that more leads people to worship as you're preaching than if they see you're worshipping." The preacher must "taste the food he is feeding to the children." This is hard because the preacher's own subtext — what he is really after underneath the words — is often something else: reinforcement of the tribe, performance, or teaching. Worship-subtext is the most spiritually mature and the rarest.

The deepest danger Keller names is the confusion of gifts and grace. A preacher's gifts (communication, leadership, exegesis) can carry him for years while his actual communion with God collapses. The result is "dead orthodox" preaching — doctrinally correct, technically able, untouched by the sweetness it describes. The corrective is not better technique but a real prayer life: Bible reading that descends into meditation, meditation that warms into prayer, and prayer that occasionally opens into contemplation — a spiritual sense on the heart of the reality of God.

## Sub-Texts of Preaching [coverage: high — 1 source]

In Lecture 16 Keller distinguishes the **text** (what Scripture teaches about Christ), the **context** (how to apply it to these people in this culture), and the **subtext** (what the preacher is really, underneath, trying to say). Subtext leaks through tone, manner, and emphasis; if it contradicts the words, it will undermine them. He enumerates four subtexts.

1. **Reinforcement / "Gatekeeping" — Aren't we great?** Ritual, stylized communication used to fortify the tribe. The church does not want to be challenged or stretched; it wants the familiar jargon, the tribal dialect, the reassurance that "we are the kind of people who say these sorts of things." Requires almost no skill — only fluency in the in-group vocabulary. Keller **warns against** it: "the motive is really just to strengthen the ghetto."
2. **Performance / "Selling" — Am I okay? Don't you think I'm good?** The preacher is exhibiting his skill and promoting the church. The audience eventually senses that the speaker is not actually concerned about them, and the sermon cannot change lives. Keller says this is nearly unavoidable for new preachers fresh from seminary: "It took me five years to convince myself that I was in the right job." He **warns against** it but recognizes you mostly have to outgrow it rather than simply repent of it.
3. **Training / "Teaching" — Aren't these truths great?** The goal is to inform: to give people things they didn't know and could use. Keller says this is where "the average, secure, spiritually mature preacher in America at least is still probably aiming." He is gentler here — this is real work and not dishonorable — but it is still not the goal.
4. **Worship / "Preaching" — Isn't Christ great?** Aims past information and even past behavior change toward "capturing the imagination and a change in what our heart sets its affections on." It is the hardest subtext, requires the most skill, and is the only one Keller fully **commends**. "Churches should be committed to the worship subtext, and I'll call it preaching... it's certainly different than teaching."

The pastoral counsel is diagnostic: "If you can name the demon, you have got control over it, to some degree." You will not get past performance-subtext by trying; you get past it by recognizing it is there and then by years of growing into a different center.

## Edwards-Inspired Tips [coverage: high — 1 source]

Lecture 16 derives five practices for preaching that creates "sensible ideas" — the sense on the heart, not merely the notion in the head — from Edwards (especially *Religious Affections* and the sermon on Genesis 19:14).

1. **Rational clarity.** Engage the understanding. Edwards refuses to oppose head and heart, so being lucid, careful, and exegetically rigorous is not the *enemy* of affection but the *first step* toward it. "If you're not affected by who Jesus is... you don't really know who he is."
2. **Analogical illustration / metaphor.** Bring two universes of discourse together so the truth is felt across the senses. Keller's example: God does not tell Cain "sin will get you in trouble"; he says "sin is crouching at your door" — sin pictured as a predator in the shadows. Metaphor engages the "sense of the heart" because there is "some kind of cross-modal relationship between the five senses and the sense of the heart."
3. **Narrative / story.** Move from abstract metaphor to concrete story. "Is it more evocative to say, 'we will always be disillusioned in this life,' or to tell a story and say, 'in the morning it's always Leah'?" Edwards himself, Keller notes, did not use narrative much — Keller is extending Edwards here.
4. **Evoke the existing idols of the hearers on the spot, then transfer.** Don't illustrate Christ in the abstract; describe what the hearers' hearts are already lusting for — friendship that never fails, a true spouse, security, status — until their affections are engaged, *then* show its inadequacy and turn immediately to Christ as the true Friend, true Bridegroom, true Wealth. "Transfer the affections to him as their new object kind of right there." Keller flags this as the one he says least well in print; it cannot be a soundbite — it has to be worked.
5. **A worshipping preacher.** "Maybe the most basic way to move from information to sensation is by the preacher actually worshipping in the act of preaching... Are you sensing Christ on the heart as you preach?" Keller cites a Puritan's advice he could not later relocate: "Make sure you taste the food you're feeding to the children."

Keller flags **(5)** as the most counterintuitive and load-bearing — and the hardest for a young preacher whose subtext is still performance. **(4)** is the one he finds most homiletically surprising and admits he has had the hardest time codifying.

## Spiritual Reality vs. Notional Knowledge [coverage: high — 1 source]

The thesis of Lecture 16 is Edwards's distinction, from *A Divine and Supernatural Light*, between two kinds of knowledge: "that which is merely notional, and the other... which consists in the sense of the heart as when the heart is sensible of pleasure and delight in the presence of the idea of it." Edwards's illustration: "There is a difference between having a rational judgment that honey is sweet and having a sense of its sweetness. A man may have the former that knows not how honey tastes." Many Christians know all the doctrine — Jesus is Savior, Lord, beautiful — but have no sensible apprehension of it on the heart.

Keller defines *spiritual reality* as the state in which a truth is not merely affirmed but felt as real — affecting the affections and therefore the life. Edwards's diagnostic, which Keller adopts: if someone says "I know God cares for me but I'm still paralyzed by fear," what that actually means is "you don't really know God cares for you." The knowledge has not touched the heart.

**Practical signs in a preacher's delivery:**

- *Notional preaching* sounds correct but cool; it lays out the doctrine without seeming moved by it. The preacher functions as a lecturer or an executor of points. Listeners receive information; their hearts are not warmed; behavior change, if attempted, must be ginned up by guilt or willpower ("I just have to beat them into doing it").
- *Spiritually real preaching* presents the same doctrine but with the preacher visibly tasting it. Edwards's reformed remedy is not less doctrine but more — "the truth more clearly, more brilliantly, more lovingly, more firmly expounded." This route avoids both rationalism (truth without affection) and emotionalism (affection without truth).

Keller ties this to the biblical category of *remembering*. Hebrews 3:13, Joshua 4:21–24, 2 Peter 1:8–9, and the Lord's Supper all assume that the basic spiritual problem is forgetting — not intellectual amnesia but spiritual disengagement. The preacher's job is to fan the flame so that what is known notionally becomes real again.

## Gifts vs. Grace [coverage: high — 1 source]

Lecture 18 is the explicit "driver-training film." Keller, citing Edwards's *Charity and Its Fruits* on 1 Corinthians 13, presses the Puritan distinction between *gifts* (the operation of God's Spirit through a person's abilities) and *grace* (the actual saving, sanctifying work of God in the person's heart). Edwards goes so far as to say that God can gift even an unconverted man — "Many will say in that day, Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name...?" The gifts are "as it were precious jewels which a man carries about him, but true grace in the heart is, as it were, the preciousness of the heart itself."

**Keller's diagnosis** — how a preacher tells which is operative:

- *Symptoms of running on gifts alone:* fruitful public ministry coexisting with a stagnant prayer life, hidden anger at one's spouse, lustful thoughts, distance from God in private. The retreat goes well, the sermon lands, people come to Christ, and the preacher concludes "God is with me" — when in fact what he saw was his gift operating, not God's pleasure with his walk.
- *Peter Wagner's image Keller quotes:* "Spiritual gifts without spiritual fruit is like a tire without air." You can keep moving for a while; something carries you; but when you stop, you collapse. Many moral falls among "blessed" ministers happen exactly this way — "their gift operations mask the lack of grace in their life until it was too late."
- *Why this danger is acute now:* we live in "the age of technique" — results, skills, surfaces — and de-emphasize character, reflection, and depth. The temptation to mistake numbers ("nickels and noses") for God's favor is greater than in Edwards's or Whitefield's day.

**Warning signs:** You are very busy, very fruitful, and have not really prayed today. You teach others spiritual practices you barely use. You are a "spy in Canaan" who has tasted the fruit and described it but never settled the land. Keller confesses this about himself: "I'm acting like I've actually done... when I've only visited."

**The corrective:** real prayer — meditation, communion, the disciplines below — not better preaching technique. Keller relays John Gerstner's chapel sermon on Psalm 66:18 ("If I cherish iniquity in my heart, the Lord will not hear me"): an entire Gordon-Conwell student body skipped class and went back to pray. (He adds, ruefully, "It wore off in a week.") Lecture 18's about.md points to a parallel Keller conversation with Sinclair Ferguson at Westminster Theological Seminary that makes the same case at greater length.

## Communion with God [coverage: high — 1 source]

Lecture 18's main framework is a **four-fold devotional outline**: Bible reading → meditation → prayer → contemplation. Keller's innovation, drawn from looking outside his own evangelical tradition into Puritan, Lutheran, and even Catholic contemplative sources, is the insertion of *meditation* between Bible study and prayer, and the addition of *contemplation* as the hoped-for fruit.

1. **Bible reading.** Slow, repeated reading — not the kind you do to prepare a sermon, but listening for things "not seen or appreciated or enjoyed before," stopping to taste the truth as it goes by. Keller advises that new Christians cannot do this directly; they must first do study to know what a passage is about, then return to it for appreciation.
2. **Meditation.** "To descend with the mind into the heart" (Theophan the Recluse). Keller adopts Luther's *garland meditation* from the letter to Peter the barber: take a phrase, even a single word, and run it through a tripartite (Keller adds a fourth) set of questions: *What does this teach me about God to praise him for? What does it show me about myself to confess? What does it tell me I need to ask for? (Keller's addition:) How is Jesus Christ the ultimate example of this attribute, the ultimate antidote to this sin, the ultimate source of this grace?* Meditate "until your heart gets hot" or, in Luther's phrase, "until the Holy Spirit starts preaching to you, then stop your discipline and write everything down."
3. **Prayer.** With heart warmed by meditation, move into adoration, confession, thanksgiving, supplication, and intercession — the prayer list, but engaged.
4. **Contemplation.** The final, *receptive* practice — not something you can schedule. "A spiritual sense in the heart of the reality of God... a kind of gaze at him. At bottom, it's a gift." Keller equates it with Romans 8's "the Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit." Lloyd-Jones's experience over an open Arthur Pink book — catching the word "glory" and being given a sense of glory that lasted hours and returned over weeks — is the paradigm. Edwards's 1737 woods experience is the classical example.

**Why it is load-bearing for the preacher.** Without meditation, Bible study and prayer become two circles that merely "kiss" — cognitive intake followed by a list of requests, with no heart-engagement between them. The preacher who lives in that mode cannot lead his people into worship because he is not worshipping himself. Meditation is precisely the practice that converts notional knowledge into spiritual reality on the preacher's own heart first, before he attempts it on the congregation's.

**What it looks like daily.** A devotional event roughly twice as long as the seminary "quiet time"; familiar passages worked over slowly; a fixed set of meditation questions held in memory; willingness to learn from Puritan, sacramental, charismatic, and contemplative streams without abandoning evangelical theology; the Puritan counsel — "mind your work more than your wages" — that you do not chase the experience, you seek God himself and let the experience come.

## Pitfalls to Avoid [coverage: high — 2 sources]

- **Mistaking gifts for grace.** Treating fruitful public ministry as proof of God's pleasure with one's heart. Keller's repeated diagnostic: numbers, ease, results, applause — none of these prove communion. They can coexist with a soul "stranger to him."
- **Teaching what you have barely tasted.** "I've taken three steps but I'm acting like I've gone a mile." Public ministry outruns private devotion the moment a preacher finds something useful and turns it into content before he has lived it.
- **Treating doctrine as content without taste.** Preaching Jesus is good, holy, faithful, beautiful without ever giving evidence on the face or in the voice that the preacher has *sensed* it. The sermon becomes a lecture; affection in the hearer is impossible.
- **Performance subtext.** Especially fresh from seminary: the unstated question "Don't you think I'm good?" running underneath the words. Audiences eventually detect it.
- **Gatekeeping subtext.** Speaking the tribal dialect so the in-group can feel reassured. No skill required; no Christ proclaimed.
- **Stopping at intellectual conviction.** "They need the truth more clearly, more brilliantly, more lovingly, more firmly expounded" — but only if the goal is to move the affections, not merely to win the argument.
- **Letting prayer collapse into a list.** Skipping meditation and going straight from Bible study to intercession leaves the heart cold; the result is a preacher whose own heart is not moved by the text he is about to preach.
- **Chasing experience for its own sake.** "If you go after goosebumps in your prayer life, you're just being selfish." Seek God; the experience comes as fruit.
- **Refusing to learn outside one's tradition.** Reformed evangelicals stalled at a wall because they will not read Teresa of Avila, the contemplatives, the sacramentalists, or even the deeper Puritans on experience. Lloyd-Jones is Keller's example of a Reformed mystic recovered from the tradition itself.

## Worked Examples [coverage: high — 2 sources]

- **Jonathan Edwards, *Religious Affections* and *A Divine and Supernatural Light*.** Source of the two-faculties view (understanding and inclination, both grounded in the heart), the honey-tasting analogy, and the category of *excellency* — beauty appreciated and rested in for its own sake. Edwards's distinction between the "essentially Christian" (who finds Christ useful as a means to other beautiful things) and the "true Christian" (who finds Christ beautiful in himself) is Keller's diagnostic for affections.
- **Edwards's sermon on Genesis 19:14** (Lot's sons-in-law who thought Lot was joking). Edwards's line: "the reason why men no more regard warnings of future punishment is because it don't seem real to them." Christian Northampton believed in hell notionally; it was not real to them; therefore it did not affect them.
- **Edwards's 1737 woods experience** (*Personal Narrative*). The hour-long flood of tears at "the glory of the Son of God as Mediator... his wonderful, great, full, pure, and sweet grace and love, and meek and gentle condescension." Keller's paradigm of *contemplation*.
- **Edwards, *Charity and Its Fruits* on 1 Corinthians 13.** Source of the gifts-vs-grace argument and the line about gifts as "precious jewels which a man carries about him" versus grace as "the preciousness of the heart itself."
- **Genesis 4 ("sin is crouching at your door")** as Keller's example of God using sense-evocative metaphor rather than abstract warning.
- **Genesis 29 — Leah** ("in the morning, behold, it was Leah") as Keller's narrative illustration of disillusionment, and Leah's eventual "this time I will praise the Lord" as the trajectory of redirected affection.
- **Joshua 4:21–24, Hebrews 3:13, 2 Peter 1:8–9** on remembering as spiritual re-engagement, not intellectual recall.
- **John 2 — the wedding at Cana.** Keller's worked semi-sermon in Lecture 18. Exegetical key: "my hour is not yet come" everywhere in John refers to the cross, so Mary's request about wine pulls Jesus's mind immediately to his own wedding feast and the death required to bring it about. Jesus's calling-card miracle is therefore *festal joy*, *taste*, *sensation* — and it is purchased by his death to make us his bride. Four applications to prayer: petitionary, patience, perspective, presence. Keller credits Edmund Clowney's old sermon for the exegesis and quotes Clowney's line: "Jesus Christ sat amidst all the joy, sipping the coming sorrow, so that today we can sit amidst all this world's sorrow, sipping the coming joy."
- **Martin Luther's letter to Peter the barber.** Source of garland meditation — the word-by-word working of a text through praise, confession, and petition until the Spirit begins to preach.
- **Theophan the Recluse and Peter Toon** on meditation as "descent of the mind into the heart."
- **Richard Baxter** on meditation as distinct from both study (whose aim is to learn or declare) and prayer (whose direct object is God): meditation is "studying right in front of God," affecting the heart with love and delight.
- **Teresa of Ávila and John of the Cross** on discursive meditation leading to contemplation. Keller notes that Edwards, Luther, and the Spanish mystics describe the same phenomenon in different vocabularies.
- **D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones** in Wales, 1949 — burned out, unable to pray, caught by the printed word "glory" in an open Arthur Pink book, and given an experience that recurred over weeks. Keller uses this as a Reformed instance of contemplative experience and gently mocks Iain Murray's biographical squirming around it ("well, you know, the doctor was Welsh").
- **Spurgeon's sermon on the father's kiss in Luke 15.** Keller cites Spurgeon's line: "Some of us know what it's like to be too happy to live... we would have died for joy." Spurgeon's whole sermon takes off from three words and ignores its context — "a wonderful biblical sermon that has nothing to do with the text it's based on."
- **Jack Miller** as a counter-example to "good sermon = good preaching." Miller's sermons broke every homiletical rule; his preaching was "vastly better than the sermon" because spiritual reality shone through.
- **John Gerstner's Gordon-Conwell chapel** on Psalm 66:18, which sent a campus of seminarians back to pray.
- **Ephesians 1:17–19** ("the eyes of your heart enlightened") and **Psalm 34:8** ("taste and see that the Lord is good") as biblical warrant for "sense on the heart" language.

## Connection to Keller's Own Preaching

This teaching is the structural reason Keller's preaching feels the way it does. The sermons are exegetically careful and doctrinally precise — he believes Edwards: you cannot move affections by bypassing the understanding. But they refuse to stop at intellectual conviction. Almost every Keller sermon ends with a turn toward Christ as the true Friend, true Bridegroom, true Treasure — the practice he labels "evoke the idol and transfer the affections." The emotional warmth in his delivery is not stylistic; it is the visible operation of tip #5, a worshipping preacher tasting the food he is feeding the children. The recurring move at the end of his sermons — "do you see how beautiful he is?" — is the worship subtext made explicit.
