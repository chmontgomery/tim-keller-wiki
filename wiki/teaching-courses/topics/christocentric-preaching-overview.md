# Christocentric Preaching — Course Overview

## Summary [coverage: high — 3 sources]

"Preaching Christ in a Postmodern World" is an 18-session Doctor of Ministry course co-taught by Tim Keller and Edmund Clowney at Reformed Theological Seminary. Its single, organizing question is the one Keller asks at the opening of session 1: "Is Jesus made visible or only talked about?" The course's thesis is that a sermon has not done its work merely by exegeting a passage correctly, applying it practically, or stirring an emotional response — it has done its work when the hearer sees Christ through the text and is moved to worship.

Keller and Clowney organize the model around a three-part frame: **Expounding Christ → Applying Christ → Adoring Christ**. Keller maps these onto Christ's threefold office: the prophetic aspect of preaching is to *expound* the text so people understand Christ; the kingly aspect is to *apply* and counsel so they put on Christ; the priestly/existential aspect is to lead them to *adore* and worship Christ. The three are perspectives on one goal, not three separate tasks — "each one well understood includes all the rest," as Clowney puts it.

The audience is working pastors, and the tone is diagnostic. The course's core critique is that the two dominant streams in evangelical preaching — older doctrinal/expository preaching and newer practical, user-friendly preaching — "often fail to preach the gospel," and that a third stream, devotional/narrative preaching, fails in the opposite direction by going straight for the emotions. Keller's claim is stronger than a stylistic preference: he argues that preaching a text without getting to Christ *changes the meaning of the text*, turning every passage into "a moralistic exhortation to try harder rather than a call to live by faith in the work of Jesus."

## The Christocentric Frame [coverage: high — 2 sources]

The three aspects, in Keller's own structure:

- **Expounding Christ (prophetic)** — The preacher asks: "What does this text tell me about the person, the work, and the teaching of Jesus?" A text has not been expounded until its particular message has been integrated with the overall message of the Bible, which climaxes in Jesus. The alternative — and the failure mode — is "expounding biblical principles": treating the text as a repository of timeless truths or moral examples disconnected from redemptive history. Clowney's lectures in the course are largely devoted to this aspect: how every genre of Scripture (narrative, law, psalm, wisdom, parable) reveals Christ.

- **Applying Christ (kingly)** — The preacher asks: "How am I failing to rejoice in and live as if this text is true about who Jesus is and what he did?" The diagnostic move is to show that the listener's problems are, to some degree, a reflection of unbelief in this particular truth about Christ. If the text says Jesus is our Sabbath rest, the application is not "rest more" but "show how the problems in your life reflect that you don't believe this." The alternative is exhortation to try harder — telling people to be like David rather than showing them that the greater David has already won the battle for them.

- **Adoring Christ (priestly/existential)** — The preacher asks: "How can I adore and rejoice in Christ from what I've learned?" The aim of every sermon is for the hearer to *experience* Christ through the text — to have "a sense of God on their hearts." A sermon is part of worship, not a prelude to it. The alternative is information transfer — a lecture. Keller: "In a lecture, you're doing information... In a sermon, the goal is to get people to worship on the spot."

Keller offers a nine-question diagnostic (from page 6 of the course notes) that maps onto the three aspects: **Truth** (Was it sound? Was the point clear? Was Jesus the climax or tacked on?), **Life** (Was it fresh? Practical? Was Jesus preached as the solution to people's problems, or were they just told to try harder?), and **Power** (Was there a sense of God? Was the central metaphor gripping? Was Jesus made visible or only talked about?).

## What This Course Is Not [coverage: high — 1 source]

Keller diagnoses three failure modes, each of which gets one aspect right and the others wrong:

- **Pure doctrinal preaching** concentrates on exposition at the expense of application and adoration. "It hits the intellect rather than the heart. The result's more of a lecture." Keller specifically names a tendency in the Reformed evangelical world to promote this approach under the banner of "expository preaching" out of fear of "therapy, pietism, and emotionalism." He is careful to distinguish: what he and Clowney are teaching is genuinely expository and genuinely redemptive-historical, but some who use those labels are actually preaching the failure mode he is critiquing.

- **Pure practical / user-friendly preaching** puts almost all the emphasis on application. "It hits the will rather than the heart or the intellect." The church-growth-movement version is "user-friendly motivational sermons" on how to set priorities, handle stress, raise a family, spend quality time with kids — "all the emphasis is on how-tos." The mainline version is "liberal activist preaching" that does cultural commentary and exhorts to social justice. The fundamentalist version is "moral exhortations to live the right way." All three are how-to preaching with the gospel removed.

- **Pure devotional / narrative preaching** "goes right for the emotions." Keller has in mind the recent surge in narrative preaching that tells stories that "get to the heart" but is "not very theological and it's not very practical." It aims at affect rather than worship of Christ.

The deeper objection is not that these are stylistically thin but that they tell the listener — by structural implication — that "the Bible's about them." Keller's worked example is David and Goliath: a sermon that ends "be like David and fight the giants in your life" makes the text about the hearer's effort, while a sermon that ends with the greater David who fought the only giants that can really kill us (sin and death) and whose victory is imputed to his people makes the text about Christ for the hearer. The first produces try-harder moralism; the second produces faith and worship.

## Course Structure [coverage: high — 1 source]

The 18 sessions interleave Clowney (on exposition) and Keller (on application and adoration):

1. Introduction to the Christ-Centered Model — Keller & Clowney
2. Expounding Christ: Structure of Redemptive History, Part 1 — Clowney
3. Expounding Christ: Structure of Redemptive History, Part 2 — Clowney
4. Applying Christ: Introduction to Christ-Centered Application — Keller
5. Applying Christ: Getting to Christ — Keller
6. Expounding Christ: Structure of Redemptive History, Part 3 — Clowney
7. Expounding Christ: Telling God's Story, Narrative Analysis — Clowney
8. Applying Christ: Getting Down to Earth, Part 1 — Keller
9. Applying Christ: Getting Down to Earth, Part 2 — Keller
10. Expounding Christ: The Parable of the Prodigal Son — Clowney
11. Expounding Christ: Christ and the Law — Clowney
12. Applying Christ: Getting Inside Their World, Part 1 — Keller
13. Adoring Christ: Getting Inside Their World, Part 2 — Keller
14. Expounding Christ: Christ in the Psalms — Clowney
15. Expounding Christ: Christ in Wisdom Literature — Clowney
16. Adoring Christ: Spiritual Reality — Keller
17. Expounding Christ: Asking Questions, Discourse Analysis — Clowney
18. Adoring Christ: Communion With God — Keller

Clowney's nine sessions cover *expounding Christ* across every major genre of Scripture. Keller's nine sessions cover *applying Christ* (sessions 4, 5, 8, 9, 12), *getting inside the listener's world* (12–13, bridging application and adoration), and *adoring Christ* (13, 16, 18). **Only Keller's sessions are transcribed in this wiki.** For Clowney's expositional material, consult the original recordings at The Gospel Coalition.

## How to Use This Section

This section of the wiki is for **preachers** — pastors preparing sermons, teachers reviewing their own preaching, or anyone trying to understand Keller's method of homiletics. When the question is "how should I preach this text?" or "why does my sermon feel like a lecture?" or "how do I apply this passage without moralizing?", start here. The nine-question diagnostic on the heading above is itself a useful tool for sermon review.

For Keller's actual theological *content* — what he taught about idolatry, marriage, suffering, the gospel, justification, and so on — see the sibling wiki at `wiki/topics/`. The teaching-courses sub-wiki is meta-content about how Keller preached; the main wiki is the substance of what he preached. The major method topics in this sub-wiki are:
