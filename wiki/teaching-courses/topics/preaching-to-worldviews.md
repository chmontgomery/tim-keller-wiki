# Preaching to Worldviews — Getting Inside Their World

## Summary [coverage: high — 2 sources]

For Keller, "getting inside their world" is a form of application that goes deeper than tips for daily living. He calls it "worldview change," and he distinguishes it sharply from what he labels "NIF-NARFI application" — the merely pragmatic "here's a practical way to deal with worry / raise your children" mode. The reason application has to operate at the worldview level now, he argues, is that the country is no longer roughly homogeneous in its assumptions. His own in-laws (non-Christian) and parents (evangelical) shared a basic worldview a generation ago; today the non-Christian listener increasingly holds "a whole different view of human nature, whole different view of reality." If you preach to that listener as if they only need to be warned about specific sins or persuaded on specific moral issues, you will miss them at the level where they actually live.

The goal is therefore not just to persuade the skeptic but to reframe the believer's imagination. Keller's case studies (the Temple sermon from John 2; the Leah sermon from Genesis 29) are explicitly "combinations of evangelism and edification for Christians and non-Christians." The Christian sitting in the pew is being trained to see how the gospel critiques the culture's idols (career, family, romance, the empty bench of meaning) — and is also being equipped to hear their non-Christian friends, because they begin to notice the friends' objections being taken seriously from the pulpit.

This is also not "felt-needs preaching." Keller is emphatic that the listener's stated desires (acceptance, freedom, love) are both affirmed and confronted. The method requires entering the worldview in order to detonate it, not to pander to it. As he puts it, if you only stay outside and "blast" the worldview ("you hedonistic, secular, therapized, individualistic, selfish people"), nothing moves. If you only enter and never challenge, the listener "will hear the things I say still in terms of their own worldview" and be unchanged.

## Subversive Fulfillment [coverage: high — 2 sources]

The course materials describe the central concept as "subversive fulfillment, that is, showing how the culture's desires are both fulfilled and confronted by the Gospel" (lecture 12 about.md). In the lectures Keller works it as a three-move sequence: (1) **identify a commitment** the non-Christian holds that you can affirm biblically — justice for the poor, peacemaking, personal intimacy with God, humility, love, the magnificence of sexuality; (2) **destabilize** by showing them that, on their own premises, they cannot consistently hold that commitment — "the inadequacy or incompatibility of their existing commitments… for their principles"; (3) **re-stabilize with Christ** by providing the biblical view of God that grounds the commitment, while honestly naming the cost of changing and the more-than-commensurate reward.

He frames it metaphorically as drilling and detonation: "If I want to move this rock… I just sort of stick the dynamite on the outside… it'll just shear off a little piece. I have to drill a hole, deep, deep, deep, deep. But if all I ever do is drill, I'm never going to get my highway going either." Subversive fulfillment is the drilling-then-detonating motion together.

The course's wider framing (lecture 13 explicitly calls this "presuppositional apologetics, at least the way you preach it") sits in the same stream of thought that Dan Strange has developed under the label "subversive fulfillment" and that Christopher Watkin later articulates as biblical "diagonalization" — affirming the genuine truth in a cultural commitment while refusing both of the false alternatives the culture poses. Keller himself in these lectures attributes the basic Romans 1 logic to Van Til-style presuppositionalism: every non-Christian is "living as if the biblical God exists" and "stealing from the biblical worldview" at the level of their deepest commitments, even while denying him at the level of their stated beliefs. Subversive fulfillment is the homiletical form of that argument: it tells the listener that the gospel does not destroy what they most want — it grounds it.

## Preaching Apologetically Without Apologetics [coverage: high — 2 sources]

Keller is blunt that the older evidentialist style — "here's the evidence for the resurrection," "here are the proofs of God" — "doesn't fit into a sermon to preaching at all. As soon as you try to do those things in a sermon you're into a lecture and everybody's going ho-hum." Instead, apologetics should be **woven in**: presuppositional moves dropped into the body of an exposition.

Concrete examples he works in the lectures:

- **Biblical infallibility / authority** to postmodern listeners. Don't argue from prophecy fulfillment or F.F. Bruce-style historical reliability. Argue: "You can't have a personal relationship with God unless God can contradict you and outrage you. If you pick and choose what you'll accept, you have a Stepford God" (the Star Trek "I, Mudd" / *Stepford Wives* illustration recurs in both lectures).
- **Historicity of the resurrection.** Don't rehearse John Warwick Montgomery. Ask: would you like a relationship with God based on grace rather than performance? Then it has to have actually happened — otherwise the Gospels are "one more moral paradigm to crush you."
- **Hell.** Two entry points. Lewis's freedom-of-the-will argument works for some. Keller's preferred move: enter through the listener's commitment to a loving God, then show that removing hell makes God *less* loving, not more — "what did it cost your God then to love us and embrace us? Where was his agony? Where was the nails and the thorns?" The Cross only amazes if there is wrath to absorb.
- **Uniqueness of Christ.** Enter through the commitment to humility. Then the Polanyi/Newbigin elephant-and-blind-men move: "the only way you could say all the religions of the world are like blind men only seeing part of the elephant is if you assume that you can see the whole elephant." Religious pluralism is the more exclusivist position, dishonestly held.
- **God's existence / moral realism.** Annie Dillard's *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek* — nature is "red in tooth and claw," yet she cannot stop saying it is *wrong* for the strong to eat the weak. "How could we know that nature is abnormal" unless there is a supernatural standard? If your premise leads to a conclusion you know is false, change the premise.
- **Meaning.** Arthur Miller's *After the Fall* — "the bench was empty… the pointless litigation of existence before an empty bench." If you live as if your choices matter, you live as if there is a Judge.
- **Justice and non-violence.** Miroslav Volf, *Exclusion and Embrace* — "the practice of non-violence requires a belief in divine vengeance." The objection that wrath fuels violence "is a naive view held by people who themselves have not suffered violence."

His method-level advice for doing this without it sounding like apologetics: never make an orthodox Christian statement and walk past it. The example he gives is the wrath of God: "If you just say, the wrath God we deserve he took it, and don't add, now I know a lot of you feel that's outrageous… I beg you to consider this — as soon as you say that, they're in." The skeptic feels respected and the Christian in the seat next to them thinks, "I wish my non-Christian friend would have been here to hear this."

## Biblical-Theological Themes That Land Today [coverage: medium — 2 sources]

Two of Keller's worked sermons in lecture 13 are case studies in BT themes that he finds carry disproportionate weight in a postmodern audience.

**Temple (John 2:15–21).** Keller chooses this precisely because it seems remote: "for modern people today, temple means almost nothing." But that gap is the point. Ancient cultures all had temples because they shared two convictions: there is an "ultimate, impenetrable, divine mystery" and "we are cut off from it" and need mediation. The Enlightenment's "just-really" reductionism (everything is "just really" a natural cause) tried to abolish temples but is collapsing on both **pragmatic** grounds (our social and psychological problems have not yielded to scientific reduction) and **logical** grounds (Lewis's *Abolition of Man*: "you cannot go on seeing through things forever… to see through all things is the same as not to see"; Nietzsche's, Freud's, the sociobiologist's reductions all reduce themselves). Once the temple-shape of human longing is recovered, Jesus's claim — "I am the God on the other side of the gap" *and* "I am the temple, the bridge over the gap" — lands as the hardest saying in the Gospels, the one that got him killed.

**Marriage (Genesis 29, Leah and Rachel).** The chosen text because "there is no book less sentimental about marriage than the Bible." The text refuses both "Bush country" (family is what life is about) and "Gore country" (family as oppressive structure). Jacob makes Rachel his ultimate hope; Leah makes her husband's love hers; both are destroyed. Kidner: "in the morning, behold, it was Leah" — "a miniature of our disappointment from Eden onwards." Lewis on the argument from desire seals it. The text then opens into Christ as the true bridegroom who comes to the unloved Leah and through Leah, not Rachel, brings the Messianic seed.

The method for **finding** such themes: pick BT trajectories where the cultural translation gap is itself the homiletical leverage — concepts the modern listener thinks they've outgrown (temple, sacrifice, judgment) or thinks they understand sentimentally (marriage, family, love), where exposing the actual biblical shape destabilizes the listener's settled categories. The BT theme has to do double duty: it must both expose the modern idol and offer the gospel as the deeper fulfillment of what the idol was reaching for.

## The Preacher's Imagination [coverage: medium — 2 sources]

Keller's argument is that worldview-engagement is impossible if the preacher has not first been deprovincialized. Lecture 12's "vicious cycle" passage is the heart of this: "you tend to preach to the people you listen to during the week." The grid those people install in your head determines what you see in the text. If you read only other evangelicals, you preach to evangelical-sub-culture questions. If you talk only to seminarians, you preach seminary lunch arguments. If you spend all your appointment hours with leaders and movers, "after a while your sermons will get stupid."

His prescriptions are concrete. Read magazines published by people who differ wildly from you (he names the *Utne Reader*, *The Nation*, *The New Yorker*, *Wired* — each calibrated to a specific kind of non-Christian he wants in his mind as he opens the text). Diversify your appointment schedule. "Discipline who you picture": while exegeting, run lists — mature Christians, immature Christians, non-Christians, conscious skeptics, nominal Christians, divided Christians, mature Christians (his Mark 4 four-soils grid). Anticipate objections aloud, in the sermon, in the second person: "now some of you are likely saying, yes, that's great for you, but you don't have my husband." That move comes "from who you're spending time with." It cannot be faked.

Self-knowledge is the other half. He uses his own example bluntly elsewhere in the course material — "I was a white person. I looked down my nose at people…" — to make the point that the preacher's own captivity to context (race, class, region, temperament) shapes the grid before any worldview-work on the listener can happen. Lecture 12 names temperament specifically: "let the biblical text control you, not your temperament." Some preachers are natively forceful, others natively affectionate; the text contains both, and unless you are willing to grow into the side that is not native to you, your application will be monochromatic regardless of the passage. "The great preachers have this combination of forcefulness and affection. They weep as they're beating you to death."

## Pitfalls to Avoid [coverage: high — 2 sources]

- **Caricaturing the skeptic.** "It's very, very lazy to put up the objections to Christianity in a way that are very, very easy to knock down." The straw-man objection will never convince the listener who actually holds it.
- **Dismissive sneer.** "Some people say things like this, but we know…" — said with a smirk. "If there's somebody in that service that really feels that's why I can't be a Christian, to feel dismissed like that, they're never going to come back."
- **The "this is just the way it is" tone.** Preaching difficult doctrines as if they are not painful to anyone — Keller's *Little Shop of Horrors* "Be a Dentist" image of the preacher who hits the cavity with the drill without flinching.
- **Apologetics-as-preaching rather than apologetics-woven-into-preaching.** Doing a lecture on the empty tomb in the middle of a sermon. The sermon stops being a sermon and starts being a defence brief.
- **Pandering to felt needs.** Keller's "NIF-NARFI" warning: applying the text purely pragmatically (how to deal with worry, how to raise your kids) without confronting the worldview underneath those felt needs.
- **Monochromatic congregation, monochromatic application.** The vicious cycle: the people you talk to all week become the only people you preach to, who become the only people who keep coming, who become the only people you talk to. Application narrows until the sermons "get stupid."
- **Defensive posture.** The pre-emptive evangelical crouch — preaching as if outraged that the world finds you outrageous. Keller's counter: assume the outrage and step toward it ("I know some of you feel this is outrageous, but I beg you to consider…").
- **Climaxing where the outline says, not where the Spirit does.** Lecture 12's "pliable moment" warning: the sermon crescendos ten minutes in, but the outline schedules the climax at thirty, so the preacher plows past the moment of yielding and the people lose it.

## Worked Examples [coverage: high — 2 sources]

- **Annie Dillard, *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek*** — the giant water bug and the frog-suit, "evolution loves death more than it loves you or me," used to argue that moral outrage about nature requires a supernatural standard.
- **Arthur Miller, *After the Fall*** — "the bench was empty," used for the argument that lived meaning presupposes a Judge.
- **Miroslav Volf, *Exclusion and Embrace*** — "the practice of non-violence requires a belief in divine vengeance," used to invert the standard objection that wrath fuels violence.
- **Michael Polanyi / Lesslie Newbigin, *The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*** — the elephant-and-blind-men move on religious pluralism.
- **C.S. Lewis, *The Abolition of Man* and "The Weight of Glory"** — "to see through all things is the same as not to see," and the inconsolable secret / argument from desire.
- **Stanley Hauerwas** on singleness as a permanent valid lifestyle — Christianity as the first religion to refuse to make family ultimate.
- **Derek Kidner** on Genesis 29:25 — "in the morning, behold, it was Leah… a miniature of our disappointment from Eden onwards."
- **John 2:15–21 (the Temple sermon)** — full worked example: Enlightenment "just-really" reductionism cracking on pragmatic and logical grounds; Jesus as both the God-on-the-other-side and the bridge across; application to seekers (you cannot buy in; the house of God is not a market) and to Christians (zeal for the temple now consumes him for *your* life).
- **Genesis 29 (the Leah sermon)** — full worked example: Jacob's and Leah's idolatries of romance and recognition; "in the morning it's always Leah"; the Lord becomes Leah's husband; the Messianic seed comes through the unloved one.
- **Doctrinal worked examples** — wrath of God, hell, biblical authority, uniqueness of Christ, miracles — each shown with an "entry point" (a commitment the listener already holds) and a "challenge point" (the internal incoherence on their own premises).

## Connection to Keller's Own Preaching

To spot this in a Keller sermon, listen for the rhythm: a non-Christian objection is named in the second person and taken seriously; a commitment the listener already holds (love, justice, freedom, intimacy, humility) is affirmed and shown to be biblical; the listener's own premises are then turned to show they cannot sustain that commitment; finally Christ is offered as the ground the commitment was reaching for, with an honest naming of the cost. The literary or cultural quotation early in the sermon (Lewis, Dillard, a novel, a film) is almost always doing entry-work, gaining plausibility before the destabilizing move.

The conceptual substrate of this method lives in `wiki/topics/apologetics.md` (Keller's broader Romans 1 / presuppositional case for skeptical engagement) and `wiki/topics/identity.md` (the "what you build your life on" diagnostic that he uses again and again as the entry-point commitment for both Christians and non-Christians).
