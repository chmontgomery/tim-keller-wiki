# Christ-Centered Application

## Summary [coverage: high — 2 sources]

"Christ-centered application" in Keller's teaching is the practice of making Christ visible in every sermon by showing how the text genuinely points to him, rather than tacking him on at the end of an otherwise moralistic message. The aim is to get from the text to Christ *organically* — through types, themes, narrative patterns, and the logic of redemption — never by forced allegory or by squeezing a Christological moral out of a passage that is really about something else. Keller is explicit that you do not need a character-type to preach Christ: "all of Scripture is about Christ," and types are only one of several pathways from a text to him.

The reason this matters is pastoral, not just hermeneutical. If preachers cannot get to Christ from the text in front of them, they will inevitably preach moralism — Bible-character-as-life-coach, "try harder to be like Joseph," "be brave like David" — which Keller argues actually nurtures the roots of sin (pride and fear) underneath a thin veneer of moral behavior. Christ-centered application replaces moralism (conservative version) and "God-loves-everybody" flattening (liberal version) with the cross, which alone resolves the tensions the text raises. As Keller summarizes Alec Motyer: rather than only looking for types, you should "look for the questions the text raises to which Jesus is the only answer" — the "answer in the back of the book."

This wiki section is meta-content for preachers: it tells you how Keller himself moves from any biblical passage to Christ in his own preparation, and how to do the same in your own preaching and in evaluating others' sermons.

## The Practice [coverage: high — 1 source]

In lecture 05 (and the practical heart of lecture 04), Keller names **four ways of getting to Christ organically**. He developed these by reading Alec Motyer's *Look to the Rock* and Sidney Greidanus's work on preaching Christ from the Old Testament, then synthesizing his own version. He insists you must have all four in your head when you come to a text, "or I'm going to miss opportunities for preaching Christ."

**1. Theme Resolution.** Find an intercanonical theme running through the text — what Don Carson calls "intercanonical themes" and Greidanus calls "longitudinal themes" — and pull on the thread. These themes (King and Kingdom, Grace and Law, Worship and Sanctuary, etc.) cut across the entire biblical corpus and have "thickening plots" as Scripture unfolds: dramatic tensions that look unreconcilable until Christ resolves them. Keller's canonical example is the King-and-Kingdom theme working its way through Judges, the failed kings, the Psalms, and Isaiah until "the question that runs through the Bible is this: how can any king be powerful enough to liberate us from the slavery that is this great? And the answer is, only the Creator Himself could possibly be a big enough king." You don't need a David or a Solomon type; *any* leader narrative becomes a Christ-pointing text by comparison or contrast.

**2. Law Completion.** Take any ethical principle — "tithe," "be generous," "do not commit adultery," "love your neighbor" — and listen to it hard enough that you see it is impossible to keep. Paul says in Galatians 3:24 that the law leads us to Christ. Keller's rule: "we have not listened to the full weight of the rule until we see that Christ will have to fulfill this ethical principle for us or we're dead." So every ethical command gets to Christ in three moves: (a) the law's demand is total; (b) you cannot fulfill it, so you need a Savior who already has; (c) the principle itself images something about Christ's work (marriage images his jealous love; honesty images his truthfulness on the cross). Canonical example: tithing in Malachi. If anything, New Testament Christians are *more* obligated to grace, "and therefore is it possible at all that we would be expected by God to be giving less than 10% of our income? No." You'll never do it. You need Christ — and his sacrificial generosity is the only thing that will produce generosity in you.

**3. Story Insertion.** Take the individual story you are preaching and place it inside the bigger story — the history of redemption. This operates at three levels: (a) **individual storylines** ("Jesus is the true and better Adam... the true Abel whose blood cries out for our acquittal not condemnation... the true Isaac who was offered up for us all... the true Jacob who took the blow of justice we deserve... the true Joseph who is the right hand of the King, forgiving those who betrayed and sold him... the true Moses who mediates a new covenant... the true rock struck with the rod of God's justice... the true Joshua... the true Job who suffered innocently and intercedes for his friends... the true David whose victory becomes his people's though they never lifted a stone... the true Jonah who went into the belly of the earth so people could be saved"); (b) **corporate storylines** — the Exodus, the wilderness, the exile — all fulfilled in Christ as the true Israel, the faithful remnant of one (Matthew quotes Hosea's "Out of Egypt I called my son" of Jesus alone); (c) **grace-event narrative patterns** — life-through-death, triumph-through-weakness, the powerful clueless and the marginal seeing (Naaman, Esther, Ruth, the order of Exodus-then-Law). Canonical example: David and Goliath. The story is not "be brave like David"; it is that Israel needs a substitute, and David is a vulnerable, weak figure who fights for them — placing the hearer behind David in the crowd, needing the true champion.

**4. Symbol Fulfillment.** The most familiar approach: major figure typology (every prophet, priest, king, judge who brings salvation points to Christ in their strengths and flaws, and the outsiders God uses are also types) plus non-personal salvation symbols — the bronze snake, the water from the smitten rock, the Passover, the tabernacle and temple, the altar, the candelabra, the bread, the clean laws, the whole sacrificial system. "Jesus fulfills them all." This is the easy one; it is what most preachers default to when asked "how do you preach Christ?" Keller's point in lecture 04 is that this is only *one* of four pathways, and over-fixating on it makes preachers miss Christ in texts that have no obvious type.

Keller's working summary: "I look at the text and I say... unless I have all that in my head I come to the text I'm going to miss opportunities for preaching Christ. So you have symbol fulfillment. You have story insertion, which is a little bit more nuanced than just types. You've got law completion and you've got theme resolution."

## Themes of Christ [coverage: high — 1 source]

Keller draws a sharp distinction between *types* of Christ and *themes* of Christ. A type is a person, event, or object that prefigures Christ. A theme is "an intercanonical thread" — a question, tension, or longing that runs from Genesis to Revelation and only resolves at the cross. "You couldn't have a type without a theme, but you can sure have a theme without a type."

The themes have "thickening plots." The Old Testament asserts truths in apparently unreconcilable tension; the tension worsens as Scripture proceeds (Isaiah is the typical climax); only Christ resolves it. Keller groups the major themes:

**Three broad themes:**

- **King and Kingdom.** Freedom and glory of God's kingdom lost at the fall; the search for a true king absorbs Israel's history; every judge and king fails; Isaiah brings the tension to a head — what king could be powerful enough to liberate slavery this deep? Only the Creator himself. Christ is the king who comes.

- **Grace and Law.** How can God be both holy *and* merciful, both faithful to his covenant *and* just? Ray Dillard's analysis of Judges through 2 Chronicles: the narratives are propelled by the unresolved question of whether the covenant is conditional or unconditional. The conservative reading says conditional; the liberal reading says God just loves everybody. Both are wrong. "Only in the cross are both the law of God and the love of God fulfilled, together." The cross gives "a God far more holy than any legalist dare believe in" and "far more loving and accepting than any relativist dares believe in."

- **Worship and Sanctuary.** How can we connect to the presence of God? Presence lost in Eden, restored partially in tabernacle and temple, broken open in Christ ("Jesus is the temple"), now indwelling believers, eventually filling the whole earth. When preaching a psalm about longing for the sanctuary, don't moralize ("come to church"); say: "We are the temple now... we have more access to the presence of God than they did back then."

**Narrower (interlocking) themes:**

- **Righteousness and Nakedness.** How can we be free of shame and condemnation? Originally righteous and naked without shame; Christ the obedient Son clothes us in his righteousness; we stand boldly, unashamed.
- **Love and Faithfulness (Bridegroom and Bride).** Marriage as the controlling image of covenant; Christ the true bridegroom who sacrificially wins his bride.
- **Image and Likeness.** How can we become fully human? Image defaced but not lost; Christ is the perfect image ("the icon") — both showing us who God is and who we are meant to be.
- **Rest and Sabbath.** Shalom lost; physical rest points to spiritual rest from our works in Christ (Hebrews 4); ultimate rest in the city of God.
- **Wisdom and Word.**
- **Judgment and the Judge.** Without a final judge, the strong eat the weak with no appeal. "Only in Christ is there hope because he's the judge who took our judgment. He's the judge who was judged" — so God can be both just and justifier.

Keller's crucial point: God did not look at marriage and decide it would be a nice symbol for the gospel. God invented marriage *to tell us about Christ*. The same holds for every theme. The themes are not illustrations God borrowed; they are realities God designed so that we would recognize Christ when he came. Therefore every ethical principle, every institution (family, church, state), every image actually tells us something about Christ.

## Pitfalls to Avoid [coverage: high — 2 sources]

**1. Moralism (Bible-character-as-life-coach).** The most common failure mode. "It is so easy to preach a gospel narrative moralistically, even though it's all about Jesus." Keller's running examples are Chuck Swindoll's David-and-Goliath ("the bigger they come the harder they fall — go fight your giants with faith") and the conventional Jairus sermon ("trust Jesus even when his timing seems wrong"). Both can be true in content but moralistic in effect: they leave the hearer trying harder under their own steam, never seeing Christ.

**2. The Conservative Reading: Legalism.** Treating the Old Testament covenant as basically conditional. "Unless you live right, your children won't turn out right." This says God will only be faithful to people good enough to deserve it — and it shrinks God's holiness to something a religious person can meet.

**3. The Liberal Reading: Sentimental Flattening.** "God really loves everybody. Doesn't matter how you live, basically God's going to accept you all." This dissolves the law's demand entirely. Keller pairs this with the conservative error and rejects both: the tension is real, "unresolvable in the Old Testament," and only the cross resolves it.

**4. Forced Allegory / Type-Fixation.** Hunting frantically for a "type" in every text and refusing to preach Christ if you can't find one. "Be careful about being too fixated... 'Where's the type? Where's the symbol? Then I can preach Christ. I don't see one. I guess I can't.' That's not what you should be doing." This is precisely why Keller develops theme resolution, law completion, and story insertion as alternative routes.

**5. Resolving the Text's Tension Apart from the Cross.** Any sermon move that takes the dramatic tension a passage raises — Can God forgive? Can a king liberate us? Can the covenant survive our failure? — and resolves it by anything other than Christ's finished work is sub-Christian preaching, however orthodox the surrounding theology.

**6. Justification-by-Faith / Sanctification-by-Effort.** The pastoral counterpart of moralism. "We may say you're justified by faith and sanctified by faith, theoretically, but in our practice what we tell people is, you're justified by faith and you're sanctified by trying very, very, very, very hard to live up to what Jesus says you should live up to." Christ-centered application refuses this split: any flaw in our actual righteousness "stems from a corresponding failure to orient yourselves toward our imputed righteousness." The cure for sin is not more effort but deeper "commerce with" (Berkouwer's word) the gospel.

**7. Skipping Redemptive-Historical Location.** "If you don't show that there are stages in the redemptive historical flow, you may mislead people." The themes progress — God is *bringing* his rest, *bringing* his presence, *bringing* his judgment to fruition. Locate your text in the progress, or you flatten the gospel.

## Worked Examples [coverage: high — 2 sources]

**David and Goliath (Story Insertion / Grace-Event Pattern).** The moralistic reading makes you David and tells you to summon faith. Keller's reading places you in the *crowd behind* David: Israel cannot fight Goliath and needs a substitute. David is "not a full-grown man but a vulnerable and weak figure." "Until I see Jesus fought the real giant — sin, law, and death — for me, I'll never have the courage to fight ordinary giants in life like suffering, disappointment." The cross is what gives the moral courage the moralistic sermon merely demands.

**Joseph (Integrating Moral Example with Christocentric Preaching).** Is the Joseph narrative about sexual purity or about Joseph as a Christ-type? Keller refuses the either/or. You preach Joseph as a type of Christ (the betrayed brother exalted to the right hand who saves those who sold him) *and* you ground the moral exhortation about sexual fidelity in that gospel — because sexual sin almost never is "just, I'm horny." It is rooted in a failure to find one's identity, comfort, and approval in Christ. His example of a pastor's affair: when the man's children left for college he lost their affirmation and could not handle the emptiness. Sin's root is always functional idolatry — breaking Commandment 1 underneath any breach of Commandments 2-10.

**The Tithe / Malachi (Law Completion).** New Testament believers are more, not less, indebted to grace; therefore the floor of generosity cannot be lower than 10%. But you will never do it under your own steam — Langdon Gilkey's *Shantung Compound* shows the "incredible wise ways that you can rationalize a lack of generosity." Therefore you must take people to Christ's own sacrificial self-giving as both the demand and the power.

**The Psalmist's Longing for the Sanctuary (Theme Resolution + Redemptive-Historical Stage).** Don't say "come to our sanctuary every week too." Say: "We are the temple now... how much more is fellowship with the presence of God available to us than it was there?"

**Jairus and the Bleeding Woman (Mark 5) (Theme Resolution + Grace-Event Analogy).** Keller works this passage in detail as his sample sermon. The natural moralistic sermon: "Trust Jesus even when his timing makes no sense." True but flat. Keller's Christ-centered move: *why* can't we handle Jesus's delays? Because of works-righteousness — "we believe God owes us." Three help-points: (a) the *wisdom* of Jesus — he could have raised the dead girl as easily as healed the sick one; "Jesus will always give you what you would have prayed for if you knew everything he knows"; (b) the *grace* of Jesus — he makes the male synagogue ruler with great faith wait while he attends to the unclean woman with poor faith ("you have a picture of the gospel right here"); (c) the *tenderness and power* — Bill Lane's note that "Talitha cumi" is a pet name, "honey, time to get up," and Jesus reaches *through* death to wake her "because on the cross he lost his Father's hand... that's why we have his hand." The moral application — trust God's delays — is grounded in the cross, where Jesus's own prayer ("let this cup pass") was turned down for us.

**Naaman, Esther, Ruth (Grace-Event Pattern).** None of them are obvious types of Christ. But the *narrative pattern* — "every person with power and worldly status in the story of Naaman is clueless about salvation. Every servant, every little nobody, every marginal person knows what's going on" — itself images the gospel's reversal. So Esther and Ruth: "in order to redeem the people they love they have to risk loss and do many things that mirror how Christ brought salvation to us."

**Joshua Meeting the Captain of the Lord's Host.** "One of the few places where the type actually meets the anti-type face to face. Joshua meets the real Joshua." Asked "Are you for us or for our enemies?" the captain answers "No." "It's not a question of whose side I'm on. Are you on my side or not?"

**Isaiah's Lord and Servant.** The clearest Old Testament foretaste of the Grace-and-Law resolution: the high King and the suffering Servant held in tension that only Christ unites. "When you're done with the end of the book of Isaiah, you say, how in the world could anyone fulfill both grace and love in the covenant? And the answer is Christ."

## Connection to Keller's Own Preaching

When studying Keller's actual sermons, listen for this four-step pattern: (1) he names the *theme* or *tension* the text raises (often phrased as a question — "how can God be both holy and faithful?" "how can we connect to God's presence?"); (2) he traces the theme across the canon, showing the plot *thickening* through the OT until the tension feels insoluble; (3) he resolves the tension at the cross, often by showing that Christ underwent the very thing the text's protagonist fears or fails at; (4) he applies the resolution to a present idolatry by exposing the work-righteousness or fear underneath the listener's behavior. This is exactly what he models on Jairus, on David and Goliath, and on the tithe.

For the topical sermons wiki, this method illuminates how Keller handles cross-topic sermons:

- On the cross and atonement — see [wiki/topics/the-cross-and-atonement.md](../../topics/the-cross-and-atonement.md) — the Grace-and-Law theme is his usual structural backbone.
- On idolatry — see [wiki/topics/idolatry.md](../../topics/idolatry.md) — note how he routes the diagnosis through Luther's reading of the First Commandment: any breach of Commandments 2–10 is rooted in breaking the First, which is precisely the "law completion" move applied pastorally.
- On the gospel and grace — see [wiki/topics/the-gospel-and-grace.md](../../topics/the-gospel-and-grace.md) — Berkouwer's "commerce with justification" and Chalmers's "expulsive power of a new affection" are his canonical citations for why moralism fails and Christ-centered application works.
- On sanctification — see [wiki/topics/sanctification.md](../../topics/sanctification.md) — Keller's claim that you grow not by trying harder but by deeper orientation to your imputed righteousness is the engine of his application method.
