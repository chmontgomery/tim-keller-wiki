# Application Strategy — Getting Down to Earth

## Summary [coverage: high — 2 sources]

"Getting down to earth" is Keller's name for the move from accurate exposition into specific, personally-applied claims that change how a hearer lives on Monday morning. After you have exegeted the text and grasped its theme, the working question becomes, "How do I apply it to people in a way that doesn't just fall into general moral exhortation?" Keller is blunt that this is where most preachers fail: they confirm what the average person already believes — "Christianity means trying hard, being good, keeping your nose clean, being a nice person" — and never actually convict, renew, or convert anyone.

Specific application requires three things. First, it has to distinguish the gospel from both moralism and relativism every time, or the sermon will be heard wrong even when it is theologically correct. Second, it has to dig beneath behavior to the heart motive — the functional idol or self-justification that produces the behavior — rather than just commanding behavior change. Third, it has to refuse to let the preacher's temperament narrow the kind of application the text gets: doctrinal, devotional, and cultural-public dimensions all belong in play.

The underlying conviction, carried over from the previous lectures on sanctification by faith, is that you cannot get to specific application by tightening the screws on the will. You only get there by exposing what the hearer is actually trusting in place of Christ and then showing them Christ in such a way that the false trust loses its grip.

## The Three Macro-Strategies [coverage: high — 2 sources]

Keller calls these "macro-strategies" because they are not parallel choices; they overlap and a single sermon can use all three. He says explicitly that if you carry these three grids into your study, "you're not going to have any problem coming up with practical application."

1. **Critique religion as well as irreligion.** Show the gospel as a "third way" against both moralism (right-hand thief) and relativism / antinomianism (left-hand thief). Use this whenever you exhort, whatever the topic — giving, suffering, sexuality, family, guilt. The reason is partly cultural (postmodern hearers have rejected religion, often for good reasons, and will only listen if you offer something other than what they rejected) and partly pastoral (most Christians "functionally base their sanctification on their justification rather than the other way around" — Lovelace). Tertullian: "Just as Christ was crucified between two thieves, so this doctrine of justification is ever crucified between two opposite errors." Luther: this article must be known, taught, and "beaten into their heads continually."

2. **Aim at the heart motives under the behavior.** Self-justification (Luther) is the root of every sin. If Christ is not your righteousness, something else will be — power, approval, comfort, or control. So the preacher does not stop at "don't do X"; he asks, "Why are you doing X? What are you trusting that only Jesus should be giving you?" The behavior is the symptom; the functional savior is the disease.

3. **Apply the text through three perspectives** (drawn from John Frame via Vern Poythress's *God-Centered Interpretation*): the doctrinalist (normative — what truth does the text teach?), the pietist (existential — how does this text address my heart, my prayer life, my walk with God?), and the cultural transformationist (situational — how does this text bear on the public, communal, world-renewing reign of God?). Read every text through all three lenses so your temperament does not flatten the application.

## The Basic Outline [coverage: high — 2 sources]

Keller credits Dick Kaufman for naming the underlying four-beat shape of every Christ-centered application sermon. It works as either an explicit outline or the implicit dramatic spine of a talk:

1. **What you must do / be.** State the moral principle the text presses on us. Every text has one, because all revelation is covenantal — there is always a way we are being called to live, either by command, promise, warning, or example.
2. **Why you can't do it.** Preach the law in the Protestant sense — show that this obligation, taken seriously, is impossible. Confront the hearer with the reasons they will fail. This is "plot thickening": the sermon needs the same tension a story needs. (N.T. Wright: "Little Red Riding Hood took her grandmother some food and goodies and then went home" is a fact, not a story.)
3. **But there is one who did.** Show how Christ is the ultimate example, embodiment, or fulfillment of this principle. Keller notes the change of tone of voice here: "you're moving from a lecture into a sermon."
4. **Through him you can do it too.** Frame our inability not as a lack of trying but as a lack of faith in him. The way forward is repenting of our failure to believe the gospel, resting in what Christ has done, and being melted by it into the behavior the text called for.

**Case study — honesty.** Before he learned this, Keller's sermons on honesty went: forms of dishonesty → why it's bad → how to be honest → "Jesus was a great truth-teller; if you fail, he'll forgive you." Result: liars walk out crushed, non-liars walk out smug. The gospel rewrite asks "Why do I lie in this particular situation?" Keller lies when telling the truth would cost him approval; Kathy lies for different reasons. The lie is not the disease — trusting approval as a functional savior is. The cure is not "try harder to be truthful" but "repent of your unbelief in the gospel; you do not need that approval, because you have Christ."

**Case study — the beauty myth (NYU talk on Naomi Wolf).** Worked as a full deployment of the outline:
- *What we must do:* The power of physical beauty over us must be broken — it distorts women's self-image, demoralizes the aging, makes men reject good spouse prospects for superficial reasons.
- *Why we can't:* Genesis 3 — we use physical beauty to cover shame and inadequacy; we use it to deny our mortality. These are too deep to fix by trying.
- *But there is one who did:* Jesus was "beautiful beyond bearing" — too glorious to look at — yet "became ugly that we might become beautiful," took on homeliness so we could receive the beauty that actually counts (transparent character, freedom from shame).
- *So now you can:* Only seeing what he did melts the heart's grip on the cult of image. Keller's claim: at the end "the angry lesbian feminists' tongues will be hanging out" wishing it were true — which is the preacher's job.

The outline is especially well-suited to non-sermon talks (campus, mixed audiences) where you cannot announce three points off a divided text but need conversational tension that lands somewhere real.

## Audience Differentiation [coverage: medium — 2 sources]

Keller does not use the labels "mature / non-Christian / newer Christian" explicitly, but he distinguishes audience tracks that map onto them and warns the preacher to "diversify your people context" in his head as he writes.

- **Mature Christians (especially elder-brother types).** "Many, many real Christians are elder-brotherish." They are converted but legalistic, prideful, Pharisaical — their practice is out of line with their theology (Lovelace). Renewal for them means being pulled out of legalism by being shown that they are functionally trusting moral performance, not Christ. Application should expose this gently — let the Holy Spirit ferret them out rather than the preacher publicly diagnosing them.

- **Non-Christians (especially postmodern, modern, secular hearers).** "99.9% of the people in this country who think they've rejected Christianity have rejected religion, moralism." If you only contrast Christianity with immorality, they conclude what they walked in believing — that Christianity is moralism — and tune out. The only way they will listen is if your preaching critiques religion as severely as it critiques irreligion, showing the gospel is not what they rejected. Keller's NYU beauty-myth talk is the model: meet them on a cultural touchpoint, expose the depth of the problem, and present Christ in a way that makes them wish it were true even if they cannot yet believe it.

- **Newer / very immature Christians, and professing-but-unconverted "Bible Belt" attenders.** "Plenty of people in churches simply are non-Christians. They're professing Christians; they're not converted." Keller's discipline: you must not decide who they are — that is "uncharitable" and "spiritually dangerous." Instead preach the gospel against religion every week; over time people will come and tell you, "When you began your ministry, I was the treasurer and I was not really a converted believer." If nobody ever says that, "you're not doing this right."

The unifying advice: the same passage must hit all three tracks. The way to do this is not to address each in turn but to consistently distinguish gospel from both moralism and relativism, and to dig beneath behavior to functional idols, so that each hearer is convicted in their own particular bondage.

## The Three Preaching Pitfalls [coverage: high — 1 source]

These are the *doctrinalist*, *pietist*, and *cultural-transformationist* perspectives. Strictly, Keller (following Poythress) presents them as three *legitimate* perspectives the text must be read through; they become pitfalls only when one is used to the exclusion of the others. Each tends to attract a particular temperament, and each "comprises the other two" if pursued deeply enough — they are interpenetrating like the Trinity, not three slices of a pie.

- **Doctrinalist.** Reads every text for what doctrine it supports. On Luke 4:31–37, the doctrinalist preaches the deity of Christ, his sovereignty over evil spirits, and his grace toward those in bondage. Attracts Reformed evangelicals and people of rationalist temperament. The danger is the Enlightenment fallacy that you can have objective knowledge without making it personal — sound doctrine without devotional or public traction. Keller sees current calls to "real expository preaching" as sometimes a coded plea to stay stuck here. Maps to the **gospel of Christ** (object-historic work, propitiation in space and time) — Stott, Packer, InterVarsity of Keller's youth.

- **Pietist.** Reads every text for psychological and devotional application. On Luke 4, the pietist preaches "Jesus can solve my problems if I let him, and once delivered I must tell my friends." Attracts revivalists, evangelistic personalities, and "bleeding hearts." Best of the three at evangelistic decision-pressing on individual hearts and consciences. The danger is therapeutic individualism — peace, love, happiness in Jesus, with no public or doctrinal weight. Maps to the **gospel of sonship** (new identity in Christ, Spirit-renewed psyche) — Lovelace, Jack Miller. **Keller's confession: this is his own bent.** "I'm not a cultural liberationist, I'm not a doctrinalist, I'm a pietist. I became a Christian through InterVarsity."

- **Cultural transformationist.** Reads every text for its bearing on corporate, public, communal, world-renewing kingdom themes. On Luke 4, this perspective preaches Christ's active power liberating people from oppressive structures. Attracts those drawn to social justice, urban ministry, deed-and-justice work. The danger is commerce with Marxist-liberationist ideology and losing personal conversion. Maps to the **gospel of the kingdom** (reversal of values, healed community, ministry of deed) — Harvey Conn.

Keller's correction: every temperament's instinct is to throw bricks at the other two perspectives and call its own "real preaching." If you go deep enough into any one, you arrive at the others. Pietism pursued to the root (self-justification) becomes anti-racist cultural critique. Kingdom pursued to the root (God's reign) becomes salvation by sheer grace. The three should run together in every sermon.

## Idols and Application [coverage: high — 1 source]

Keller's method-level use of idolatry in application turns on a single diagnostic move: take the hearer's actual emotional response (anger, despair, anxiety, shame, inability to forgive themselves) and reverse-engineer back to the functional god that response implies. The theology of idolatry sits in [the idolatry topic article](../../topics/idolatry.md); what follows is the homiletic technique.

**The "I can't forgive myself" diagnostic** (Keller credits Lloyd-Jones). When someone says, "I know God has forgiven me, but I can't forgive myself," do not tell them they are being more holy than God. Tell them their *real* god has not forgiven them. "Idols will not forgive you. If the real God of your life is being a successful businessman, if the real master of your life is to be a mother whose children all love her, if you fail that god, that god will never, ever forgive you." Inability to forgive yourself reveals "some standard or condition or person more essential to your identity than the grace of God." The application is not to lower the standard (relativist) or claim the verse harder (moralist) but to expose the idol and replace it.

**Generalizing the move across topics.** Keller works the same logic across several application examples in part 1:

- *Discouragement.* Moralist says "tighten up, trust God more." Relativist says "loosen up, accept yourself." Gospel says: if you are downcast, something has become more important than Christ — repent of the pseudo-savior. (Keller's own example: his church becoming his functional salvation; Kathy's children becoming hers.)
- *Suffering.* Moralism puts God in your debt, so suffering forces "I hate thee" or "I hate me" with nowhere to go. Relativism (God owes everyone a nice life) leaves only "I hate thee." The gospel — propitiation by a suffering God — removes both: no retribution, only chastening with instruction.
- *Family.* Moralism enslaves to parental approval / family-as-everything; relativism dissolves family loyalty. Gospel frees from both because God is the ultimate Father.
- *Guilt.* Same structure: lower standards (relativist) vs. claim forgiveness without diagnosis (moralist) vs. uncover the false god whose forgiveness you actually crave.

The homiletic rule: every emotional complaint a parishioner brings is a window onto a functional savior. Sermon application that names the idol and offers Christ in its place is what "getting down to earth" actually looks like.

## Worked Examples [coverage: high — 2 sources]

- **Honesty / lying** (part 2). Old method: forms of dishonesty → command → "Jesus forgives." Gospel rewrite: ask why this person lies in this situation; surface the functional god (Keller: approval; Kathy: peace-keeping); repent of unbelief in the gospel that already secures what the lie is trying to manufacture.
- **NYU "Beauty Myth" talk** (part 2). Full deployment of the four-beat outline against Naomi Wolf's cultural diagnosis; Christ as the one beautiful beyond bearing who became ugly so we could become beautiful.
- **Galatians 2:14 / Peter's racism** (part 1). Paul does not say "you're breaking the no-racism rule"; he says "you're not living in line with the truth of the gospel." Going to a rule produces either crushing failure or smug compliance ("liberal bigots… so self-righteous against self-righteousness"). Going to the gospel ("I'm a sinner who should be going to hell") dissolves the felt superiority that produced the racism in the first place.
- **Sermon on the Mount** (part 2). Dick Lucas's insight that the "two ways" at the end of Matthew 7 are not "obey vs. disobey the SOM" but the two ways Jesus has been contrasting throughout: when you give, give *like this not like they do*; when you pray, *like this not like they do*. The whole sermon is gospel-vs.-religion, not gospel-vs.-irreligion.
- **Esther** (part 2). The major worked example for the three-perspectives strategy:
  - *Cultural-transformationist:* Esther in the harem legitimates serving God in unclean secular structures; alongside Ezra (clergy/teaching) and Nehemiah (lay/infrastructure), Esther (justice in the secular realm) shows the church needs all three callings to rebuild a city.
  - *Doctrinalist* (Ray Dillard): God is never named, yet the cascade of coincidences declares total providential rule; "the main actor is not so much as mentioned."
  - *Pietist:* Human weakness is strength; orphan-concubine-woman ascends and protects her uncle; the world's expectations stood on their head.
  - *Four-beat closing* (from a Pentecostal pastor's sermon Keller heard): "You are in the palace. You must be willing to risk losing it." But you won't — until you see that Jesus lost the *real* palace so that you are guaranteed citizenship in it. Then the little palaces lose their grip. Frank Sinatra inverted: if you know you have a place in *that* palace, you can make it anywhere.
  - *Redemptive-historical wrinkle:* Esther ends with the Jews slaughtering enemies; Dillard's resolution is that post-cross we pray for our enemies and win them, the rest now coming by sacrifice and intercession.

## Connection to Keller's Own Preaching

These strategies are visible in nearly every Keller sermon if you know what to look for. Watch for: (a) explicit "religion / irreligion / gospel" three-way contrasts, often by name; (b) the four-beat shape — moral principle, why you can't, the one who did, so now you can — sometimes structuring the whole sermon, sometimes compressed into the last few minutes; (c) the "What are you actually trusting?" diagnostic redirecting a behavior or emotion to a functional idol; (d) the "I can't forgive myself" / "you're downcast because something has become more important than Christ" move; (e) deliberate triangulation across doctrinal, devotional, and public-cultural angles within a single sermon, especially when the text seems to push only one direction. The underlying theology of why this works lives in [idolatry](../../topics/idolatry.md) (functional gods, the diagnostic) and [sanctification](../../topics/sanctification.md) (change by faith in the finished work, not by tightened will).
