Question 1: How does the concept of common virtue versus true virtue play out as we are leaders in terms of accountability? We're not the arbiters of why people are engaging in behaviors, yet it's our job to be accountable.

Answer: Look at page 12. Jonathan Edwards said common virtue in the short run is fine. There's no reason in the short run why a Christian can't simply use willpower to make a change when necessary. But in the long run, change will only come from changing the heart's deepest affections with the melting, moving grace of God. Titus 2:11-14 says, "It's the grace of God that teaches us to say no to ungodliness and live self-controlled lives."

When you're talking to somebody as a leader, when you're giving peer counseling, and you're asking people not to sleep with a boyfriend or girlfriend, when you're asking them to be generous, you appeal to the gospel. You can't look down deep in the heart and see whether their motivations are right. In fact, their motivations will never be perfect. But the way you exhort people—the attitude, even your facial expression—can show that you're trying to motivate people out of grace rather than guilt them and make them afraid. The key is: in the long run, say to them, "You've really got to get the gospel in."

Question 2: How do you go about identifying a particular idol? In your example, this woman who had bitterness against her husband was actually holding her son as an idol. But the other woman could have had something else as an idol. So you could have the same behavior but have different potential idols. How do we go about identifying them in ourselves and helping others identify them?

Answer: The second one is easier than the first. Look at the example I just gave you. There was no way to tell up front quickly. It only came out as time went on. Here you have a woman who in other areas of her life was a pretty exemplary Christian, seemed to really understand Christianity, just did not have problems in other areas. But in this one area, no matter how hard she tried, a newer Christian left her in the dust. So it took time. The way you can tell we've got idol problems is the pattern. It's a chronic pattern that comes back again and again.

One particularly dangerous thing is that an idol is often like a root underground—up comes a tree, you cut the tree down, and you think you've done it. Because you can only cut the tree down and stop the behavior. But it can come up in another way.

Let me give you a quick example. There was a guy who came into our Christian fellowship in college and he had been sleeping with almost every woman on campus except the five women in our campus fellowship. He became a Christian and said, "No more sleeping around for me. I believe in the biblical teaching about chastity." However, he absolutely wreaked havoc in the campus fellowship because he was so power hungry. If he was in any small group Bible study, he was the nightmare of leaders. He was always right. He answered every question. He contradicted anybody who disagreed with him. We realized he'd made an idol out of power. He hadn't been sleeping with those girls because he liked them. In fact, he lost interest the minute he got them into bed. It was a power thing. It was a conquest thing.

And we realized that underground, beneath this idol of power, there was another idol. He said, "That's not my idol. My idol is approval. I'm happy to give up power as long as everybody likes me." You see? And he was a person who didn't care what people thought. He didn't mind if people didn't like him as long as he was in charge. And that idol was underground. And its original shoot had been sexual licentiousness. He cut that down completely. What he did was come in—he wanted to be a Christian leader. He wanted to be a minister. He didn't stick with it, fortunately. But the fact was that he was going to probably work his idolatry out as a power-hungry person. He was going to be a power-hungry Christian minister, which is just another shoot from the same root.

So you have to look carefully for patterns over a period of time. When it comes to looking at your own idols, there are some diagnostic questions on page 15. But let me just look at it this way: look for inordinate emotion. Not just anger, because everybody gets angry. Not just worry, because everybody worries. Not just despondency, because everybody gets despondent sometimes. But inordinate, inconsolable emotions that are really strong—emotions that you just can't seem to deal with by exhorting yourself or through accountability. That's a sign you're dealing with an idol.
